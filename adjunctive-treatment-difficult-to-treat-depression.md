# Adjunctive treatment of difficult-to-treat depression — Rx decision
*2026-07-10 · PubMed 20 · trials 9 · books 0*

Frame: **Rx** — which agent to add to an antidepressant for a patient whose depression persists despite adequate treatment. Chosen over Teaching because a wrong augmentation choice is paid by a patient (akathisia, metabolic load, lithium toxicity), not by a trainee in a lecture.

## Verdict
Confirm first that this is genuinely difficult-to-treat depression — reassess adherence, the diagnosis, comorbidity and pseudo-resistance, and reset the goal from remission to optimal symptom control and function — then augment rather than reflexively switch. Make **aripiprazole 2–10 mg/day** the default adjunct: it has the best efficacy–tolerability balance of any oral option (NNT ≈ 7 for response, versus ≈ 11 for the antipsychotic class as a whole), and it beat an antidepressant switch in the two largest pragmatic trials — watch for akathisia, the leading cause of dropout in its trials. Reserve **intranasal esketamine 56–84 mg twice weekly** for genuine treatment-resistant cases where rapid effect or relapse prevention justifies its monitoring burden and cost (it is the only adjunct with a positive head-to-head trial, beating quetiapine XR), and otherwise choose among **quetiapine XR, brexpiprazole, cariprazine, lithium, liothyronine, adjunctive rTMS, or added CBT/CBASP** by comorbidity, monitoring capacity and preference; do not promise remission, and do not promise that any of these lifts suicidality itself. Confidence: **moderate** — pooled effect sizes are small (SMD ≈ 0.2–0.3) against a high placebo response (ES ≈ 0.9), and the trials that actually rank the options against each other are open-label.

## What the evidence says
The request is framed around *difficult-to-treat depression* (DTD), and the framing matters more than any single drug. The 2020 international consensus defined DTD as "depression that continues to cause significant burden despite usual treatment efforts" and deliberately shifted the therapeutic goal, in the first instance, from remission to optimal symptom control, daily functioning and quality of life through shared decision-making across all modalities. This reframes the augmentation question: before adding anything, the load-bearing move is a structured reassessment, because at least 30% of patients meet the FDA/EMA treatment-resistance threshold (inadequate response to ≥2 adequate antidepressant trials) yet a substantial fraction are pseudo-resistant through underdosing, non-adherence, or an unrecognized bipolar, psychotic, or comorbid substance/anxiety picture. An adjunct added to an unaddressed barrier inherits that barrier.

Once true resistance is established, the augmentation-versus-switch question resolves toward augmentation, and within augmentation the second-generation antipsychotics carry the most consistent randomized evidence — though the effect is real and modest, not large. Three large network meta-analyses converge: a 65-trial synthesis (N=12,415, 19 agents) found response significantly superior to placebo for aripiprazole, brexpiprazole, quetiapine, cariprazine, olanzapine–fluoxetine, lithium, liothyronine, modafinil and lisdexamfetamine, while ziprasidone, mirtazapine and cariprazine carried significantly worse all-cause discontinuation; a 33-trial synthesis (N=10,602) found every antipsychotic except ziprasidone beat placebo for response (OR 1.34 for olanzapine and cariprazine up to 2.17 for risperidone) but singled out cariprazine as non-significant for remission (OR 1.21, 95% CrI 0.96–1.54) and rated quetiapine, brexpiprazole and cariprazine worst on acceptability, leaving risperidone and aripiprazole as the best joint efficacy-plus-acceptability options; and a 2025 network meta-regression (23 trials, N=10,679) confirmed aripiprazole, brexpiprazole, cariprazine, olanzapine–fluoxetine and quetiapine XR as significant (SMD −0.28 to −0.11) with aripiprazole "relatively more effective and better tolerated." The dissenting signal is instructive rather than fatal: a 72-trial network meta-analysis (N=12,105) that pooled antipsychotics alongside neurostimulation and psychedelics found brexpiprazole and quetiapine *not* significantly better than placebo for response and ranked ECT, ketamine, esketamine and psilocybin on top — which tells you the antipsychotic effect is small enough to disappear under some model and comparator choices, so the class earns a first move but not confident enthusiasm.

Aripiprazole is the pick within that class because the head-to-head and pragmatic data specifically favor it. In the geriatric OPTIMUM trial (N=619, open-label) aripiprazole augmentation improved well-being by 4.83 points versus 2.04 for a switch to bupropion (between-group difference 2.79, 95% CI 0.56–5.02, P=0.014) with remission of 28.9% versus 19.3%, while bupropion augmentation carried the highest fall rate. A 5-trial meta-analysis (N=4,480) found aripiprazole augmentation superior to bupropion augmentation on response (RR 1.15, 95% CI 1.05–1.25) and to a bupropion switch on remission (RR 1.22) with no excess of serious adverse events. The honest counterweight is ASCERTAIN-TRD (N=278, open-label), where aripiprazole augmentation did *not* significantly beat a switch to venlafaxine on the MADRS (P=0.069) while adjunctive rTMS did (P=0.015) — so aripiprazole's edge over switching is probable but not guaranteed, and rTMS augmentation is a genuine non-drug competitor with a clean tolerability profile. The other approved antipsychotics fill niches around aripiprazole rather than displacing it: quetiapine XR (150–300 mg) is effective and, in the 12-month pragmatic LQD trial (N=212), significantly outperformed lithium on symptom burden (area-under-curve difference −68.36, 95% CI −129.95 to −6.76, P=0.030) and was more cost-effective, but it carries the worst acceptability of the class (number-needed-to-harm ≈ 3, driven by sedation and metabolic load); brexpiprazole (1–3 mg) has the lowest adverse-event burden (NNH ≈ 19) but among the smallest effects; and cariprazine (1.5 mg) reached FDA approval as an adjunct in December 2022 on two positive phase-3 trials (n=752 and n=759) that followed an earlier, larger, mixed program (n=1022), consistent with its being the least consistent signal — significant for response but not remission.

Esketamine deserves its reserved second slot, and the disagreement about it must be adjudicated rather than averaged. The favorable case: a 5-trial meta-analysis (N=774) showed adjunctive esketamine beat placebo on MADRS change (SMD 0.36, 95% CI 0.24–0.49), response (RR 1.40) and remission (RR 1.45); a 2025 meta-analysis found a day-28 SMD of −0.24 in non-suicidal MDD and a relapse-prevention risk ratio of 0.60 (95% CI 0.45–0.80) at moderate-to-high certainty; the open-label head-to-head ESCAPE-TRD trial (N=676) gave esketamine remission of 27.1% versus quetiapine XR's 17.6% at week 8 (P=0.003) with less relapse through week 32 (21.7% vs 14.1%); and a dedicated NNT synthesis put esketamine at NNT 2 by day 1, rising to 11 by four weeks, with low NNH. The skeptical case, from a 2025 systematic review, is that the individual pivotal trials were "mostly negative or failed" at weeks 2–4 (only the flexible-dose TRANSFORM-2 was clearly positive; the fixed-dose and elderly trials failed their primary endpoint), that the pooled acute effect is only 0.15–0.23 — squarely in the antipsychotic range — that esketamine showed *no* significant effect on suicidality despite carrying an acute-suicidality indication, and that abuse potential and treatment-emergent deaths are unresolved. The reconciliation: esketamine works, its symptom effect is modest and roughly equal to the antipsychotics, but it owns the only positive comparison against an active adjunct and the strongest relapse-prevention data, which is exactly why it belongs after a trial of oral augmentation rather than ahead of it — and why the marketing emphasis on suicidality outruns the evidence for symptom reduction. Racemic intravenous ketamine is a cheaper glutamatergic alternative, non-inferior to esketamine at 24 hours (remission 24% vs 29%) and with its own NNT of 3 in the one-day-to-one-week window.

The older adjuncts have been quietly demoted by better trials. A 49-comparison meta-analysis flattering to lithium (NNT 5 for lithium, 7 for esketamine, 11 for antipsychotics; best risk–benefit ratio at NNH/NNT 1.80) rested largely on trials adding lithium to *tricyclics* at non-optimized doses, and modern head-to-head data undercut that pedigree: LQD put quetiapine ahead of lithium over a year, and OPTIMUM's second step found lithium augmentation essentially equivalent to a nortriptyline switch (remission 18.9% vs 21.5%). Lithium therefore keeps a legitimate but narrower place — justified mainly by its unique population-level anti-suicidal and mortality signal and reasonable where serum monitoring is already in place — rather than as a first adjunct. Liothyronine (T3, 25–50 µg) reached significance for response in the largest network meta-analysis, is inexpensive and better tolerated than lithium, and is a sensible low-intensity option, especially in settings without antipsychotic monitoring capacity. Finally, the non-pharmacological adjuncts are not an afterthought: cognitive behavioural therapy showed the single highest (though heterogeneous) effect size in a 115-trial synthesis (ES 1.58, 95% CI 1.09–2.07), the DTD-specific Danish guideline issued weak recommendations for adjunctive rTMS and CBASP while finding the evidence insufficient to recommend intravenous ketamine or rumination-focused therapy *for DTD specifically*, and the sobering anchor from that same 115-trial synthesis is a pill-placebo effect size of 0.89 — with only six treatments (aripiprazole, (es)ketamine, mirtazapine, olanzapine, quetiapine, risperidone) whose confidence intervals cleared placebo at all. That placebo figure is the reason no single patient's "it worked" should be trusted and why every oral adjunct here carries a number-needed-to-treat that is real but not small.

## Where it breaks
- **The construct is not the trial population.** Almost every RCT and meta-analysis above enrolled TRD or partial-response MDD (≥1–2 failures), not consensus-defined DTD; the effect sizes come from 6–12-week specialist trials and may not survive in a multimorbid, poorly-monitored Klaeng-OPD patient.
- **The rankings are open-label.** OPTIMUM, ESCAPE-TRD, LQD and ASCERTAIN-TRD are all open- or single-blind, so expectancy inflates the winner — most acutely for esketamine, where dissociation effectively unblinds.
- **Aripiprazole** — akathisia and restlessness are the dose-limiting harms (leading cause of dropout in its trials) plus weight gain; the patient with prior akathisia or high metabolic risk is the wrong patient. Start at 2–5 mg.
- **Quetiapine** — sedation and metabolic burden (NNH ≈ 3, worst acceptability of the class); wrong for the metabolically loaded, the elderly faller, or anyone who must drive or work early.
- **Esketamine** — REMS enrollment, ~2 h post-dose monitoring, dissociation, abuse potential and cost; no demonstrated reduction in suicidality itself; the elderly pivotal trial failed, so it is weak in the old.
- **Lithium** — narrow therapeutic index with renal and thyroid monitoring; its meta-analytic NNT is inflated by an old tricyclic-era trial base; overdose lethality is a real concern in an actively suicidal patient even as it lowers suicide risk at the population level.
- **Cariprazine and brexpiprazole** — approved but among the smallest and least consistent signals (cariprazine non-significant for remission); akathisia and cost without a clear efficacy advantage over aripiprazole.
- **The placebo problem** — with a placebo ES ≈ 0.9, adjunct effects are genuine but modest; expect partial gains, and pre-commit to a stop/switch rule so a marginally-helping adjunct is not continued indefinitely.

## What would change this
- Two registered trials could move the algorithm: esketamine versus racemic ketamine head-to-head (NCT06278779, N=162, readout April 2027) may reset the glutamatergic choice on cost grounds, and a pragmatic six-week *intensification-versus-usual-care* trial after a first treatment failure (NCT05973851, N=418, readout mid-2028) directly tests the DTD premise of early, structured intensification.
- If racemic ketamine's access and durability data mature, its non-inferiority to esketamine at far lower cost could demote esketamine to second among glutamatergic adjuncts.
- Psilocybin and accelerated theta-burst rTMS rank at the top of the newest networks but still lack durable, adequately-blinded *augmentation* data; a positive, blinded replication would promote either into the first tier.
- What would *not* change it: the esketamine and cariprazine development programs are fully reported in the registry (11 and 6 completed trials respectively, with results), so this verdict is not hostage to a completed-but-unpublished trial — the file-drawer risk that most often overturns a confident Rx verdict is, here, low.
