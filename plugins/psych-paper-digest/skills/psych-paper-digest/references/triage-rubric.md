# Triage rubric — Act / Read / Suppressed

The tier answers one question: **what should the user DO with this item?** Act = do
something differently. Read = know it. Suppressed = never see it.

## Act — practice-changing

An item is Act ONLY if, taken at face value, it would change drug choice, dose, sequence,
monitoring, a service decision, or a taught sentence **for a patient population the user
actually sees** — the watchlist domain is the proxy for that. Qualifying shapes:

- **Reversal or safety signal** — an adequately powered result contradicting current
  practice, or regulator-grade harm data. Direction matters: reversals outrank
  confirmations of any size.
- **Guideline or consensus update** in a watchlist domain — auto-Act, whatever it says.
- **Pivotal readout** — the first adequately powered RCT on a question the domain has been
  waiting on; or, from the registry, pivotal results posted or a standard-of-care trial
  terminated for futility or safety.
- **Design floor:** RCT, meta-analysis, or guideline. Nothing observational reaches Act
  without regulator-grade harm data.

**Hard cap: 5 per digest.** More qualifying items means the window was too long — keep the
top 5 by clinical consequence and say in the header that the cap bit.

Every Act item carries three things: its **one number** (the effect size, n, or event rate
that makes it practice-changing), the **decision it raises phrased as a question**, and the
**handoff line** to pubmed-research-note. It never carries the *answer* to that question —
adjudication is the other skill's job, and an Act item with a verdict in it has failed.

## Read — worth knowing, changes nothing yet

- Confirmatory positives — a result in the expected direction on settled practice.
- A new in-domain meta-analysis or systematic review that refines an effect size without
  moving it across a decision threshold.
- Large cohort or registry studies with a novel but unconfirmed signal.
- Secondary analyses of landmark trials.

One line each, still numbered. **The why-clause test:** if the one clause explaining what
the item adds to the domain is hard to write, the item is Suppressed, not Read.

## Suppressed — counted, never listed

Below floor or below interest: case reports, letters, editorials, narrative reviews,
preclinical and animal work, protocol-only registrations, pilots under n≈30 without a
novel mechanism, keyword-coincidence off-domain hits, re-publications and errata. One
count per domain in `## Suppressed`.

Listing a suppressed item is a rubric failure; suppressing silently — no count — is a
bigger one.

## The inflation guard

When unsure between Act and Read, **choose Read**. When unsure between Read and
Suppressed, **choose Suppressed** — the why-clause test is the tiebreak. The Act section
spends the user's clinical attention, and an all-clear digest that says "nothing
practice-changing this window" is the rubric working, not failing. Triage is calibrated by
what it excludes.
