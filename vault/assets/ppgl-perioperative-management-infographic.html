Perioperative Management of Pheochromocytoma & Sympathetic Paraganglioma (PPGL)

Surgical-safety reference for elective resection of functional (catecholamine-secreting) PPGL in adults · basis: Endocrine Society clinical practice guideline + the PRESCRIPT randomized trial · rendered for the anaesthetist / endocrinologist / surgeon team

Every functional PPGL is pharmacologically prepared before surgery — α-blockade first, always before any β-blocker — titrated over ~7–14 days to seated BP <130/80 mmHg and HR 60–70 bpm with high-sodium diet + fluid loading. Anticipate catecholamine surges on tumour manipulation, then an abrupt crash after venous ligation, then a 24–48 h watch for hypotension & rebound hypoglycaemia. Confidence: moderate-high.

Pre-operative preparation ~7–14 days

α-blockade first — for all functional PPGL

Phenoxybenzamine 10 mg twice daily → up to 1 mg/kg/day (tightest control, most post-op hypotension), or doxazosin 2 → 32 mg/day (fewer side effects, faster recovery). Titrate to BP target. RCT: either agent acceptable — time outside BP target 11.1% vs 12.2% (P=.75).

⚠ Alpha before beta — never reverse

A β-blocker given before adequate α-blockade risks a hypertensive crisis from unopposed α-stimulation. α-blockade always comes first.

β-blocker — only after, only for tachycardia

If HR >100 bpm ≥3–4 days after α-blockade started: propranolol 20 mg three times daily (→ 3×40 mg/day) or atenolol 25 → 50 mg/day.

Volume & sodium loading

High-sodium diet 5000 mg/day + fluids 2.5 L/day, or 1–2 L IV 0.9% saline the day before — to survive the post-ligation pressure drop.

Adjuncts when α-blockade is not enough

Calcium-channel blocker if BP still uncontrolled, or alone if normotensive / α-intolerant. Metyrosine 250 mg twice daily (↑ every 48 h) for α-intolerance or refractory hypertension.

Set-up & access

Radial arterial line for beat-to-beat pressure; large-bore / central venous access. Laparoscopic adrenalectomy for most pheochromocytomas, open for most paragangliomas.

⚠ Two opposite emergencies, one operation

Hypertensive surges on tumour manipulation, then an abrupt crash the moment tumour venous drainage is ligated. Have both sets of drugs drawn up.

Phase 1 — surges on manipulation

First: tell surgeon to stop manipulating until BP falls. Then short-acting titratable agents:

Phentolamine 5 mg IV bolus, repeat p.r.n. · Nicardipine 5 → max 15 mg/h · Nitroglycerin 5 → 100 µg/min · Magnesium sulfate 4 g in 250 mL D5W (≤3 mL/min) for refractory · Esmolol 0.5–1 mg/kg then 50–150 µg/kg/min (tachyarrhythmia, α-blocked patient only).

Phase 2 — crash after venous ligation

Pre-emptive IV isotonic fluid is the mainstay; add norepinephrine or vasopressin 0.03–0.04 units/min for catecholamine-resistant vasoplegia. Pure α-agonists (phenylephrine) are of limited use — α-receptors are still blocked.

Watch closely 24–48 h

ICU / high-dependency for higher-risk cases. Post-surgical complication rate ~19%; prolonged hypotension is the commonest (~10%).

Hypotension (BP <90/60 mmHg)

From sudden catecholamine withdrawal + residual α-blockade + expanded vascular bed — worst and most protracted after phenoxybenzamine. Fluids first, then norepinephrine / vasopressin.

Rebound hypoglycaemia

Rebound hyperinsulinaemia once the tumour is out — typically the first 4 hours. Check glucose every 6 h for ≥24 h; run IV 5% dextrose until oral intake; re-adjust insulin in diabetics.

Adrenal insufficiency

Not expected after unilateral adrenalectomy (contralateral gland compensates). Steroid-cover only bilateral / cortical-sparing cases or if clinically suspected.

CRITICAL SAFETY — MEDICATIONS / ACTIONS TO AVOID

🚫 β-blocker before α-blockade

Unopposed α-stimulation → hypertensive crisis. α-blockade is always established first; β-blocker only after, and only for reflex tachycardia.

🚫 Morphine & codeine

Avoided per the Endocrine Society guideline. Fentanyl is tolerated and a safe alternative for analgesia.

🚫 Dopamine antagonists, corticosteroids, TCAs

Dopamine-receptor antagonists (e.g. metoclopramide), corticosteroids, and tricyclic antidepressants are known to precipitate a catecholamine crisis.

⚠ Unprepared / inadequately blocked surgery

Operating on an un-optimised functional PPGL is the core avoidable catastrophe — and a crisis trigger alongside tumour manipulation, trauma, and surgical stress.

Clinical reference aid — not a substitute for clinical judgment or local protocol. Verify against the primary sources before acting.
