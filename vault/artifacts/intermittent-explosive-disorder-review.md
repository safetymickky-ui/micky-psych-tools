IED is the only DSM-5 diagnosis whose cardinal, defining feature is recurrent impulsive aggression, and it occupies an unusual position in psychiatry: it is common (lifetime ~5–7%), begins in childhood (~12–14 years), and carries one of the better-characterized cortico-limbic and serotonergic signatures of any behavioral disorder — yet it remains under-recognized, barely treated, thinly researched outside a single group, and still argued over as a distinct entity. This review synthesizes where the science stands across nosology, epidemiology, phenomenology, neurobiology, comorbidity, treatment, and the open questions.

## Definition and diagnostic evolution
A disorder of impulsive aggression has appeared in every edition of the DSM since DSM-III (1980), but for its first two decades the diagnosis was hobbled by its own criteria: the definition was poorly operationalized, and it excluded anyone whose aggression could be "accounted for" by a comorbid disorder — which, given IED's near-universal comorbidity, meant almost everyone. The disorder was therefore believed rare, an artifact of the exclusion rule rather than a true base rate. The correction came from integrated research criteria (the IED-IR), developed around 2000–2005: a convergent/discriminant validity study (56 IED-IR vs 56 healthy vs 33 Axis I vs 22 Axis II subjects) showed the research criteria identified individuals with higher aggression and greater impairment than DSM-IV, and captured aggressive people whom DSM-IV missed, while IED-IR subjects who failed DSM-IV criteria were no less aggressive than those who met them. DSM-5 (2013) absorbed this work into a two-pathway A criterion: **A1**, high-frequency/low-intensity outbursts — verbal aggression or non-destructive, non-injurious physical aggression roughly twice weekly for three months — or **A2**, low-frequency/high-intensity outbursts — three episodes involving destruction of property or physical assault within twelve months — plus aggression grossly out of proportion to provocation (B), impulsive and/or anger-based rather than premeditated or instrumental (C), marked distress or impairment (D), and age at least 6 (E). Validity data support both pathways independently, and the controversial broadening to include purely verbal aggression is defensible on the evidence: individuals meeting IED through verbal aggression and those meeting it through physical aggression show comparable cognitive-affective deficits and psychosocial impairment, while those with both are the most severe. Coccaro's 2012 synthesis made the case that carried IED into DSM-5: relative to DSM-IV, the integrated criteria better identify people with elevated aggression, impulsivity, familial risk of aggression, and neurobiological abnormality — a validity argument, not merely a reliability one.

## Epidemiology
IED is far more common than its historical "rare" label implied, and the treatment gap is its defining epidemiological fact. In the National Comorbidity Survey Replication (n=9282), lifetime prevalence was 7.3% and 12-month prevalence 3.9% under broad DSM-IV criteria, with a mean onset at age 14, a mean of 43 lifetime attacks, roughly $1,359 in property damage, injuries in 180 per 100 lifetime cases — and only 28.8% of affected people ever receiving treatment directed at their anger. The adolescent supplement (n=6483) found lifetime IED of 7.8% with onset around age 12 and 80% persistence into 12-month caseness, yet only 6.5% treated specifically for anger. A 2025 global meta-analysis (29 studies, N=182,112, 17 countries) pooled lifetime prevalence at 5.1% and 12-month at 4.4%, with substantially higher rates in clinical (10.5%), refugee (8.5%), and adolescent populations, and male sex the dominant correlate (odds ratio 3.39) alongside trauma exposure and psychiatric comorbidity. The consistent early onset — generally preceding the disorders that later accrue around it — is what makes IED a theoretically attractive target for early detection, even as the 70–90% who never receive anger-directed treatment make it one of psychiatry's most silently prevalent conditions.

## Phenomenology and the aggression construct
The construct that organizes everything else is **reactive (impulsive, affective) aggression**: an aggressive response to a perceived provocation or threat, accompanied by high autonomic arousal and a subjective sense of lost control — classically, tension building before the outburst and relief or even pleasure after (in McElroy's early case series, 88% reported pre-attack tension, 75% relief, and 48% pleasure). This is categorically distinct from the **proactive, instrumental aggression** of psychopathy — planned, goal-directed, low-arousal — and the distinction is not merely descriptive but neurobiological, which is why IED, defined around reactive aggression, behaves as a coherent phenotype while "aggression" in general does not. The cognitive engine of the reactive outburst is now reasonably well mapped: social-emotional information processing is abnormal across the board in IED (in a study of 100 IED vs 100 healthy and 100 psychiatric controls, the groups differed on every processing component), with a **hostile attribution bias** — the tendency to read hostile intent into socially ambiguous cues — coupled to a heightened negative emotional response as the proximal drivers of aggressive behavior. Consistent with this, IED subjects over-identify anger in others and misattribute anger to non-angry stimuli. This social-cognitive profile is the conceptual bridge from the neurobiology below to the psychotherapy that works: it names what CBT is actually retraining.

## Neurobiology
IED has one of the deepest neurobiological literatures of any behavioral diagnosis, spanning four levels. **Serotonin** is the oldest and most replicated: low central serotonergic function tracks impulsive aggression, seen as an inverse correlation between cerebrospinal-fluid 5-HIAA and both impulsive aggression and violent suicidality (the classic "low serotonin syndrome"), and as blunted neuroendocrine responses to serotonergic pharmacological probes — the mechanistic rationale for treating IED with serotonergic drugs. **Genetics** anchors the trait's heritability: aggression aggregates in the families of IED probands, and the landmark molecular finding is MAOA — a complete null point mutation in the MAOA structural gene (Brunner syndrome) produces a heritable phenotype of impulsive aggression and disturbed monoamine metabolism, while common low-activity MAOA variants interact with childhood maltreatment to predict later aggression. **Cortico-limbic circuitry** provides the signature finding, and it has replicated: on fMRI, individuals with IED show exaggerated amygdala reactivity together with diminished orbitofrontal cortex activation to angry faces, and — unlike controls — a loss of amygdala–OFC functional coupling (originally in 10 IED vs 10 controls), a picture of a hyper-reactive threat detector released from prefrontal regulation. A larger replication (40 IED vs 40 controls) confirmed amygdala hyper-reactivity specific to angry faces and to social threat, and absent for happy faces or general affective pictures — the abnormality is a threat-processing bias, not a global emotional one. A structural correlate accompanies the functional one: morphometric analysis (67 IED vs 73 controls) found no volume difference but significant shape deformation, suggestive of cell loss, in both amygdala and hippocampus. **Neuromodulators and immune signals** round out the picture: vasopressin facilitates aggression (CSF vasopressin correlates with aggression, and V1a-receptor antagonism reduces it — the rationale behind the SRX246 program), oxytocin opposes it, and a newer, replicated line links IED to elevated peripheral inflammatory markers (C-reactive protein, interleukin-6), with even the reported association between Toxoplasma gondii seropositivity and aggression/impulsivity in IED pointing at an immune pathway. No single mechanism is sufficient, but the convergence — low serotonin, MAOA, amygdala–OFC dysregulation, vasopressin, inflammation — is unusually consistent for a psychiatric phenotype.

## Comorbidity and nosological boundaries
IED almost never travels alone, and its comorbidity structure is central to the argument for its validity. It is highly comorbid with mood, anxiety, substance-use, and post-traumatic disorders; across the NCS-R, the adolescent supplement, and large clinical datasets, IED's onset generally *precedes* that of the comorbid disorder, and aggression scores are highest when IED and a comorbid condition coexist — the empirical basis for diagnosing both rather than dissolving the aggression into the other disorder. Roughly half of clinical IED patients also meet criteria for a personality disorder; comorbidity with antisocial or borderline PD marks the highest-aggression subgroup, yet IED predicts anger and aggressive history *above and beyond* a PD diagnosis, and does not have a unique relationship with any single PD. A 2026 Bayesian meta-analysis (47 studies, >8,000 individuals) and a large 2025 electronic-health-record cohort have begun to quantify the full psychiatric, neurological, and somatic comorbidity load. The nosological question this raises — is IED a distinct disorder, or merely a severity marker of whatever else the patient has? — is answered, on current evidence, by three convergent lines: a characteristic early age at onset that precedes the comorbidities, a familial and neurobiological signature the comorbid disorders do not explain, and incremental prediction of aggression beyond what comorbidity accounts for.

## Treatment
For so prevalent a disorder, the treatment evidence is strikingly thin — only about six IED-specific drug RCTs exist. The load-bearing pharmacological trial remains a 12-week double-blind study of fluoxetine versus placebo (n=100): a significant anti-aggressive effect emerged by week 2, but full or partial remission reached only 46% of fluoxetine-treated subjects, with full remission in a minority. The reflexive "mood stabilizer" is not supported for IED itself: the largest divalproex trial (n=246) was negative in its IED subgroup, positive only in Cluster B personality disorder. Other anticonvulsants (oxcarbazepine, phenytoin) are positive in mixed impulsive-aggression samples, but the Cochrane review of antiepileptics for aggression (14 trials, 672 participants) found the evidence insufficient for any firm recommendation. The one randomized psychotherapy trial (n=45) showed that 12 weeks of group or individual cognitive-behavioral therapy — which directly retrains the hostile-attribution and emotional-response profile described above — beat a wait-list on aggression, anger, and hostile thinking, with gains durable at 3-month follow-up. The defensible core is therefore an SSRI plus anger-focused CBT, with comorbidity and especially alcohol use treated in their own right; no agent is FDA-approved for IED, and there are no maintenance or head-to-head data. ## Controversies and open questions
Four genuine disputes remain live. First, **validity**: the historical skepticism — that IED is rare, artifactual, or "just a symptom" — has been answered by the integrated-research-criteria data, but the literature is still scant, heavily US-based, and dominated by a single research group, so independent replication of the core findings is thinner than the confident nosology implies. Second, the **boundary with normal anger**: the DSM-5 A1 pathway, by admitting frequent verbal aggression, extends the diagnosis toward the high end of normal-range irritability, and where pathology begins is genuinely contested. Third, **under-recognition**: with 70–90% of cases never treated for anger, IED is effectively invisible in most clinical settings, so the treated population is a small, non-representative slice of the disorder. Fourth, **the treatment vacuum**: one positive drug RCT and one small psychotherapy RCT for a disorder affecting one in twenty people is a remarkable evidentiary gap, and it means most prescribing rests on extrapolation from "impulsive aggression" samples rather than IED itself.

## Evidence gaps and future directions
The pipeline is finally moving after a long stall. Two genuinely novel mechanisms are in trials — pimavanserin, a 5-HT2A inverse agonist (NCT05895513, phase 2, ~2027 readout), and nitrous oxide (NCT06118567, ~2027) — the first new pharmacology tested in IED in a decade. The vasopressin V1a-antagonist SRX246 completed a 97-patient DSM-5 IED trial (NCT02055638, 2016) that posted results to the registry but never reached a dedicated peer-reviewed IED publication, leaving its efficacy outcome `[unverified]` in the literature despite the mechanism's strong preclinical rationale. An inflammatory-challenge study (NCT06665074) treats the CRP/IL-6 association as a causal probe rather than a correlate. Beyond these, the field's clearest needs are a large independent SSRI replication and a head-to-head against CBT; maintenance and relapse data (every trial to date is ≤12 weeks); biomarker-stratified trials that use amygdala reactivity or inflammatory load to predict response; replication of the neurobiology outside its originating group; and — given onset at age 12 — dedicated pediatric treatment trials, which currently do not exist beyond an 11-patient guanfacine pilot.
