# Outcome indicators for an addiction service: a KPI panel grounded in the evidence
*2026-07-14 · PubMed 6 · trials 0 · books 0*

> Decision handed over pre-locked (no intent-lock interview): *what international standard sets define as core
> outcomes (retention, remission windows, functioning), and how a small unit's KPI panel should look — to answer
> the surveyor recommendation at the root rather than patching individual columns.* The reading it beat: a list
> of every possible metric. Answered the design question — a compact, defensible panel.

## Verdict

**There is no single universally-adopted addiction outcome "standard set" to copy — but the evidence converges on four outcome domains. Build a compact KPI panel across them (retention, substance use/remission, functioning & quality of life, safety/harm), give each domain ONE explicitly-defined metric held constant over time, and pair the outcome measures with the process measures that predict them. That answers the surveyor recommendation at the root — a principled panel, not a patched column.** Concretely:

1. **Retention** — the best-evidenced, most actionable indicator: proportion of a defined intake cohort **still in treatment at ≥3 and ≥6 months**, with the definition (denominator, allowable treatment gap) fixed and never changed between reporting periods.
2. **Substance use / remission** — **days of use** (or % substance-negative urine screens) and a **defined remission window** (sustained abstinence/low-risk use over a stated period). Sustained *in-treatment* abstinence is itself a strong predictor of later outcome, so it doubles as an early indicator.
3. **Functioning & quality of life** — the **WHOQOL-BREF** (Thai-validated; report #13) plus a functioning/role measure, read as complementary domains, not the headline (generic QoL moves modestly with treatment).
4. **Safety / harm** — overdose or serious-harm events and, where ascertainable, mortality; plus the pathway-specific indicators already defined (8Q-positive follow-up completion, report #10; aggression-incident tracking via OAS, report #11).

Design principles: **mix process and outcome measures** (process measures like retention are actionable and predict outcomes); **define each metric once and hold it constant** (definition drift is the commonest way a number falsely "improves" — report #7); keep the panel **small enough for a small unit to collect reliably**; collect at **fixed timepoints**; and always **report the denominator**.

Confidence: **high** on the domains and the measurement discipline; **moderate** on the exact thresholds, which should be set locally.

## Why a domain panel, not a borrowed standard set

The surveyor's request presumes there is a canonical addiction outcome set to adopt. There is not one with the status that, say, oncology or cardiology standard sets enjoy — the field uses a patchwork (national reporting frameworks, the Treatment Outcomes Profile, ASI-derived measures, trial-specific endpoints). That absence is itself the finding: rather than wait for a standard set or copy an ill-fitting one, a unit should build a compact panel across the **domains the evidence consistently rewards**, because those domains are stable even though the instruments are not. The four domains below are each independently grounded in this portfolio's own sourced evidence.

**Retention is the anchor**, because it is both the most actionable and the best-evidenced link to outcome. Retention is standardly operationalised as still-in-treatment at fixed timepoints (the opioid-agonist-treatment literature reports it at 1/3/6/12/24 months), and treatment completion is a related but distinct definition (a large cohort using planned-discharge completion found ~58% completion, varying by substance and comorbidity). The critical discipline — carried over from report #7 — is to pick one definition and hold it constant, because retention numbers are easy to inflate by quietly changing the denominator or the allowable gap.

**Substance use and remission** are the clinical core. Use-days and objective substance-negative testing are the standard measures, and a remission window (a stated period of sustained abstinence or low-risk use) turns them into an outcome. Usefully, sustained abstinence *during* treatment is the strongest single predictor of abstinence at long-term follow-up — so tracking it is both an outcome and an early-warning process measure.

**Functioning and quality of life** capture what recovery means beyond substance use, and the WHOQOL-BREF is the instrument of choice for a Thai unit (Thai-validated, validated in substance-use populations — report #13), paired with a functioning measure and read as a complementary domain rather than the primary treatment-effect signal.

**Safety and harm** — overdose, serious harm, and mortality where it can be ascertained — are the floor that a service must not breach, and the two pathway-specific indicators this portfolio already defined (8Q-positive follow-up completion; OAS-tracked aggression incidents) slot in here.

## Mixing process and outcome — and keeping the panel small

Two design choices separate a KPI panel that works from one that decorates a report. First, **combine process and outcome measures.** Process measures (retention, session attendance, screening completion, follow-up-contact completion) are within the service's control and predict outcomes, so they are the ones a small unit can move and be held accountable for; outcome measures (remission, functioning) are the point of it all but are influenced by much the service cannot control. A panel of process measures alone (attendance without any outcome) is hollow; a panel of outcome measures alone gives the team nothing to act on. Second, **keep it small.** A handful of well-defined indicators a small unit can collect reliably at fixed timepoints beats an exhaustive dashboard that is filled in inconsistently — reliability of measurement is worth more than breadth, especially for a unit trying to defend its numbers to a surveyor.

## Where this verdict inverts

- **If a national/DMH reporting framework already mandates specific indicators**, adopt those as the spine and add only what is missing — do not run a parallel private panel that conflicts with the mandated one.
- **If the unit cannot reliably collect an outcome at 6 months** (loss to follow-up), a 3-month indicator held honestly beats a 6-month indicator with a broken denominator — measure what you can measure well.
- **For a research/publication aim** rather than routine monitoring, tighten the definitions further and add a comparison or time-series design (per reports #7 and #9) — routine KPIs and publishable evaluation are different bars.

## What would change this verdict

A widely-adopted addiction outcome standard set, if one achieves consensus, would give a ready-made panel to align to — worth watching, but not worth waiting for. In the meantime the highest-value move is to implement the compact four-domain panel with fixed definitions and start collecting, because the unit's own longitudinal data — retention held to one definition, remission at a stated window, WHOQOL-BREF, and safety events — is exactly what both the surveyor recommendation and a future service-evaluation publication require. The root fix for the number-free column is a panel that produces numbers reliably, not a one-time patch.
