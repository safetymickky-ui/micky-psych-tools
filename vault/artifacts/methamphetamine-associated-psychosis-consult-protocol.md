# Methamphetamine-associated psychosis: which antipsychotic, for how long, and when a depot earns its place
*2026-07-14 · PubMed 14 · trials 3 · books 0*

> Decision handed over pre-locked (no intent-lock interview): *for a patient with methamphetamine-associated
> psychosis (MAP) seen on consult or admitted to a non-psychiatric/district ward — which antipsychotic at what
> dose, how long to continue it, how to identify who is transitioning to a primary psychotic disorder, and when
> a long-acting injectable (LAI) is justified for poor adherence.* The reading it beat: a survey of
> methamphetamine neurotoxicity and MAP pathophysiology. Answered the consult-protocol question — the one that
> feeds the SMI-V pathway you own.

## Verdict

**Treat the acute psychosis with a second-generation antipsychotic — olanzapine or quetiapine first-line — at the lowest dose that achieves remission, then time-limit it; do not default to indefinite therapy or a depot after one resolving episode.** Concretely:

1. **Agent:** olanzapine or quetiapine are the best-supported first-line choices; risperidone and haloperidol are effective and acceptable (haloperidol at the cost of more extrapyramidal effects); **avoid aripiprazole as acute monotherapy** — it is the one agent shown *inferior* for acute MAP and can worsen agitation. Most patients respond, and often at **lower doses and faster than primary schizophrenia** — that lower-dose responsiveness is itself a soft marker that the psychosis is substance-induced.
2. **Duration:** continue through the acute episode and a period of stability; if the patient achieves and sustains abstinence and full remission, **taper and stop** rather than committing to maintenance. Reserve indefinite antipsychotic therapy for those who declare themselves primary-psychotic (see transition markers).
3. **Transition surveillance is the core of the protocol, not the drug choice.** About **1 in 5 (≈22%)** of amphetamine-induced psychoses convert to schizophrenia, most within 2–5 years. Flag and track the higher-risk patient — younger, male, family history of psychosis, prolonged or recurrent episodes, psychosis that persists despite abstinence, prominent negative symptoms.
4. **LAI: reserve, and know the evidence is borrowed.** There is **no trial of LAIs specifically in MAP**. Offer a depot when poor adherence is demonstrated *and* the picture has declared itself a recurrent or emerging primary psychotic disorder — extrapolating from LAI benefit in schizophrenia — not reflexively at first presentation.
5. **Name what the antipsychotic will not do:** it treats the psychosis, not the addiction. Ongoing methamphetamine use, craving, and relapse need psychosocial treatment (contingency management, CBT); no antipsychotic reliably reduces use.

Confidence: **moderate** on the antipsychotic ranking (the trials are small and rated low-quality), **moderate-high** on the transition epidemiology, **low** on the LAI recommendation and on the exact treatment duration — both rest on extrapolation and expert consensus, not direct MAP trials.

## Which antipsychotic, and at what dose?

The head-to-head evidence is thin but consistent, and — usefully for this setting — much of it is regional. The network meta-analysis that anchors the choice (six RCTs, 395 patients, from Chiang Mai University) ranked **olanzapine and quetiapine above risperidone**, with **aripiprazole significantly inferior** to olanzapine (standardised mean difference 1.36, 95% CI 0.46–2.26), quetiapine (1.13, 95% CI 0.28–1.98), haloperidol (0.87, 95% CI 0.14–1.60) and paliperidone-ER — while dropout rates did not differ across agents. The evidence was rated low-quality (small trials, risk of bias), so this is a lean toward olanzapine/quetiapine, not a hard ranking.

The individual trials fill in the texture. The **Thanyarak Institute** double-blind RCT (n=80) found **quetiapine and haloperidol essentially equivalent** — remission in 89% vs 84%, PANSS falling ~48–53 points, no significant difference in efficacy or adverse effects — meaning a cheap, available agent like haloperidol is a legitimate choice where the second-generation drugs are constrained, accepting more extrapyramidal burden. Aripiprazole-versus-risperidone trials (China, n=42; Iran, n=45) both found risperidone at least as good on positive symptoms with better retention and craving reduction, while aripiprazole caused more akathisia and agitation and higher dropout. Aripiprazole versus placebo (n=37) did reduce psychotic symptoms and improve retention — so it is not inert — but its partial-agonist pharmacology makes it the wrong *first* choice for a floridly agitated, hyperdopaminergic presentation. The practical read: **start olanzapine or quetiapine; haloperidol or risperidone are fine substitutes; keep aripiprazole out of the acute regimen.** Doses can be lower than in schizophrenia, titrated to remission — the antipsychotic response in MAP tends to be brisk, with effect largely established within the first two weeks.

## How long to treat — and who actually needs long-term antipsychotics?

This is where the consult protocol earns its keep, because the wrong answer runs in both directions: under-treating someone who is quietly converting to schizophrenia, or committing a transient, abstinence-responsive patient to years of unnecessary antipsychotic.

The base rate sets the prior. The definitive meta-analysis (50 studies, 40,783 people) puts transition from **amphetamine-induced psychosis to schizophrenia at 22% (95% CI 14–34%)** — lower than cannabis (34%) but far from negligible, and pooled substance-induced transition sits at 25%. A population cohort of 9.8 million found substance-induced psychosis carried an 18.5% three-year transition risk versus 0.1% in the general population (adjusted hazard ratio 163), with younger age and male sex raising it further. The Scottish cohort (n=3,486) is the one that tells you *how long to watch*: 15.5-year cumulative transition 17.3%, but **over half of conversions occur within 2 years and over 80% within 5 years**, and a first admission lasting more than two weeks predicted conversion. So the surveillance window is concrete — active follow-up for at least 2, ideally 5, years, front-loaded.

Against that, the same literature says most MAP is genuinely transient and abstinence-responsive, and a **lower, faster antipsychotic response is a marker favouring substance-induced over primary** aetiology. The duration rule that follows: treat the acute episode, keep the antipsychotic through a stabilisation period, and if the patient sustains abstinence with full symptom resolution, **taper and stop under observation**. Escalate to maintenance-duration therapy only when transition markers accumulate — persistence of psychosis despite documented abstinence, recurrent episodes, prominent negative symptoms, family history, or early age of onset. Cross-sectional features alone cannot separate MAP from schizophrenia; only longitudinal course can, which is exactly why the follow-up structure matters more than the initial drug.

## When does a long-acting injectable earn its place?

Honestly: on borrowed evidence. The completed MAP trials — quetiapine-versus-haloperidol, paliperidone-versus-risperidone (n=120) — used **oral** agents, and a registry search finds **no randomised trial of a long-acting injectable specifically in methamphetamine-associated psychosis**, nor any such trial ongoing. The LAI case therefore rests on two indirect pillars: the established relapse-prevention benefit of depots in schizophrenia, and the fact that adherence in this population is genuinely poor.

That makes the LAI a **reserved, conditional** move, not a default. It is defensible when two conditions are both met: adherence has demonstrably failed on oral therapy, *and* the clinical picture has declared itself a recurrent or emerging primary psychotic disorder (the transition markers above) — i.e. the patient is now being treated as schizophrenia-spectrum, where LAI evidence actually applies. Reaching for a depot at a first, potentially transient, substance-induced episode over-commits a patient who may fully remit with abstinence, and imports evidence from a different diagnosis. State this uncertainty in the protocol rather than papering over it: the LAI recommendation is an extrapolation flagged as such, and generating local data on it would be worthwhile.

## What the antipsychotic will not fix — and the violence layer

Two boundaries belong in the protocol because they are where a psychosis-only plan fails the patient and the ward.

First, **antipsychotics treat the psychosis, not the addiction.** The meta-analysis of antipsychotics in stimulant dependence found no advantage over placebo on use, craving, or abstinence, and *more* discontinuation for intolerability; the broader pharmacotherapy review of methamphetamine use disorder found no agent with robust benefit (only low-strength evidence for methylphenidate). So the drug that clears the hallucinations does nothing for the relapse that will bring the patient back — ongoing use needs contingency management and CBT, and that hand-off should be written into the consult, not left implicit. A pragmatic service adaptation worth noting: an emergency programme dispensing small as-needed olanzapine "assist packs" to patients with methamphetamine psychosis was associated with fewer psychiatric emergency visits over six months, a low-cost bridge model.

Second, **the violence signal.** MAP presentations carry markedly elevated violence rates (reported as high as ~76% in a recent phenomenology review), which is the substantive link to the SMI-V pathway — the acute psychotic, agitated methamphetamine user is a core SMI-V population, and the antipsychotic-plus-observation plan here is also a violence-risk-mitigation plan. That connection is where this report feeds the violence-risk work (report #11) rather than duplicating it.

## Where this verdict inverts

- **Clear, sustained primary psychosis** (persistent despite abstinence, strong family history, insidious negative-symptom onset): treat as schizophrenia — maintenance antipsychotic, LAI on the usual schizophrenia indications, not the time-limited MAP approach.
- **Pure intoxication-phase agitation without established psychosis** is a different problem — benzodiazepines and supportive care manage acute agitation; a standing antipsychotic course is not automatically indicated.
- **Severe agitation/aggression or QT-relevant comorbidity** shifts the agent choice (haloperidol's EPS vs the metabolic/sedation profile of olanzapine/quetiapine vs QT considerations) and may exceed a district ward's safe ceiling — the transfer/observation threshold applies as in any acute psychosis.

## What would change this verdict

Little is coming that would move the antipsychotic choice: the ongoing MAP registry activity is **observational** (biomarker and phenomenology cohorts in Turkey; a schizophrenia-versus-MAP comparison in Egypt) — **no ongoing RCT tests antipsychotic selection, treatment duration, or LAIs in MAP.** The evidence gaps that matter most — a head-to-head adequately powered antipsychotic trial, any LAI trial in MAP, and a duration/discontinuation trial — are simply not being filled, which means the protocol should be built on the current low-quality base and on longitudinal course, and a service that tracked its own MAP cohort's transition and relapse rates would again be generating the missing evidence.
