# Tension-Type Headache and Migraine — Comprehensive Review
*2026-07-13 · PubMed 30 · trials 5 · sections 10 · clinician self-study, balanced*

Skipped: nothing from the arc.

According to PubMed and ClinicalTrials.gov. Tension-type headache (TTH) and migraine are the two disorders almost everyone means when they say "headache," and reviewing them together exposes a paradox the single-disorder literature hides: TTH is far more prevalent yet migraine carries almost all of the disability, the research money, and the therapeutic revolution. They share an onset decade, stress triggers, and the trigeminovascular substrate, and they blur into each other at the frequent-episodic boundary — but one now has monoclonal antibodies and a second novel drug target in trials, while the other has seen "little progress since the early 2000s." This review walks the full arc for both, holding them side by side, and treats that asymmetry as the story rather than an aside.

## Definition and nosology

Both are **primary** headaches — headaches that are the disease rather than a symptom of one — and both are defined operationally by the **International Classification of Headache Disorders, third edition (ICHD-3, 2018)**, the universally adopted framework now so authoritative that ICD-11's headache chapter is essentially an abbreviated ICHD-3. There is no confirmatory test for either; the criteria *are* the diagnosis.

**TTH** is the deliberately featureless headache: bilateral, **pressing or tightening** (non-pulsating) quality, mild-to-moderate intensity, **not aggravated by routine physical activity**, and without the migrainous accompaniments — no more than one of photophobia or phonophobia, and no moderate/severe nausea. It is graded purely by frequency: **infrequent episodic** (<1 day/month), **frequent episodic** (1–14 days/month), and **chronic** (≥15 days/month for >3 months). **Migraine** is the opposite temperament — recurrent attacks of moderate-to-severe, often unilateral, **pulsating** pain, **aggravated by activity**, with nausea and/or the photophobia-plus-phonophobia pair, lasting 4–72 hours untreated. It splits into **migraine without aura**, **migraine with aura** (reversible focal neurological symptoms, in roughly a third of patients), and **chronic migraine** (≥15 headache days/month, of which ≥8 are migrainous, for >3 months).

The nosological live wire is whether these are two disorders or two ends of one spectrum. They share onset in the **20s**, stress as the dominant trigger, and trigeminovascular involvement; frequent-episodic TTH with a little nausea is clinically almost indistinguishable from mild migraine without aura; and no biomarker adjudicates. The classification treats them as distinct, but the convergence view — that TTH and migraine are severity variants of a shared mechanism — remains unresolved and shapes how the overlap cases are counted.

## Epidemiology

The Global Burden of Disease 2016 analysis put **almost three billion people** as having one of these headaches in a single year: **1.89 billion with TTH** and **1.04 billion with migraine**. TTH is the more prevalent — the single most common neurological disorder worldwide, affecting on the order of **38%** of the population in large surveys — while migraine affects roughly **12–14%**. Yet the disability ledger inverts the prevalence ledger. Because migraine's per-episode disability weight is far higher, migraine generated **45.1 million years lived with disability (YLDs)** globally in 2016 against TTH's **7.2 million**, making migraine the **second leading cause of disability worldwide** and the single most disabling condition in **women aged 15–49**, in whom it accounted for **11.2% of all YLDs**. TTH's burden is a high-prevalence, low-per-person phenomenon; migraine's is the reverse.

Sex and age sharpen the contrast. Migraine is **two to three times** more common in women (about **17%** of all women meet criteria) and peaks in the reproductive years, with episodic migraine prevalence cresting in the **late 30s**; TTH shows only a slight female excess. The chronic forms are the clinically expensive minority: **chronic migraine affects 1–2%** of the general population and about **8% of people with migraine**, arising from episodic migraine at an annual conversion rate of roughly **2.5–3%**.

## Etiology and pathophysiology

**Migraine** is a **neurovascular** disorder with a well-mapped attack cascade. The premonitory phase begins up to **3 days** before pain and engages the hypothalamus and brainstem nociceptive-modulating nuclei; **aura** corresponds to **cortical spreading depression**, a slow wave of neuronal-glial depolarization; the headache phase is **trigeminovascular activation**, in which **calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP)** released from trigeminal terminals drives peripheral and then central sensitization. A modeled shift from activity-dependent to activity-independent central sensitization is a leading candidate mechanism for the episodic-to-chronic transition. Genetically, migraine is substantially heritable and polygenic: a genome-wide association study of **102,084 cases and 771,257 controls** identified **123 risk loci (86 previously unknown)**, including subtype-specific signals (HMOX2, CACNA1A, MPPED2 for migraine with aura; SPINK2 and FECH for migraine without aura) and — strikingly — the genes for the drug targets themselves, **CALCA/CALCB (CGRP)** and **HTR1F** (the triptan/ditan receptor); enrichment in both vascular and CNS tissue confirms the disorder is genuinely **neurovascular**. Rare monogenic forms (familial hemiplegic migraine, via CACNA1A, ATP1A2, SCN1A) prove single genes can suffice.

**TTH** pathophysiology, by contrast, "remains poorly understood." The best evidence assigns **peripheral myofascial mechanisms** — pericranial muscles that are measurably harder, more tender to palpation, and richer in trigger points — as the generator of pain in episodic TTH, with **central sensitization** taking over to drive the transformation from episodic to **chronic** TTH. The shared thread across both disorders is the trigeminovascular system and central sensitization as the common engine of chronification; the divergence is that migraine's peripheral driver is a defined neuropeptide with a druggable receptor, and TTH's is muscle.

## Clinical features and course

Migraine is an **event with a shape**: a premonitory prodrome (yawning, mood change, food craving, up to 3 days out), an optional aura (typically visual, evolving over and resolving within **60 minutes**), the headache itself (**4–72 hours** untreated), and a postdrome of fatigue and cognitive fog. TTH is defined by the *absence* of that architecture — the nondescript "band around the head," episodic attacks lasting **30 minutes to 7 days**, and, in the chronic form, a near-daily unremitting pressure. This featurelessness is not incidental; it is the diagnostic definition.

Course differs in trajectory. Migraine tends to **remit with age** after its reproductive-years peak — episodic migraine prevalence falls after the late 30s — whereas **chronic migraine is more likely to persist** into later life. Both disorders show substantial **short-term frequency fluctuation independent of treatment**, which complicates judging whether any intervention worked and inflates placebo responses in trials.

## Diagnosis and assessment

Diagnosis for both is **clinical**: ICHD-3 criteria applied to a headache history against a **normal neurological examination**, with no imaging or laboratory test to confirm a primary headache. Since roughly **90%** of people experience headache in their lifetime, the diagnostic task is less "does this headache exist" than "is this a benign primary headache or a dangerous secondary one." That triage runs on **red flags** — abrupt/thunderclap onset, new neurological signs, new headache at **age ≥50**, known cancer or immunosuppression, and provocation by exertion or posture — whose presence, not the headache phenotype, drives neuroimaging. In their absence, imaging is **not routinely indicated** in a stable primary headache with a normal exam.

The everyday assessment problem is separating **frequent-episodic TTH from migraine without aura**, which the featureless-versus-featured distinction (activity aggravation, nausea, photophobia, unilateral pulsatile pain all favor migraine) usually resolves, aided by a **headache diary** and disability instruments such as **MIDAS** and **HIT-6** that quantify impact and set the threshold for preventive treatment.

## Differential diagnosis and comorbidity

The first differential is the two disorders against **each other** — the crux above — and both against **secondary headache**, gated by the red-flag list. The shared secondary complication that every clinician must actively exclude is **medication-overuse headache (MOH)**: a daily or near-daily headache (**≥15 days/month**) in a primary-headache patient who regularly overuses acute medication — **≥10 days/month** for triptans, opioids, or combination analgesics, **≥15 days/month** for simple analgesics — for >3 months. MOH has a worldwide prevalence of **1–2%**, is intimately bound to chronic migraine, and turns the acute treatment into the disease.

Comorbidity is where migraine's systemic reach shows. It is bidirectionally linked with **depression and anxiety**, and migraine — particularly **migraine with aura** — roughly **doubles ischemic stroke risk** (pooled odds ratio **2.04**, 95% CI 1.73–2.40; **2.32** for migraine with aura), with the excess concentrated in **women** (OR 2.26) and **younger** patients (OR 2.39). Further associations run to cardiovascular disease, epilepsy, patent foramen ovale, and sleep disorders. TTH's comorbidity profile is quieter and centers on **coexisting migraine**, pericranial musculoskeletal complaints, stress, and mood — but the overlap with migraine is itself the point: the two travel together far more often than chance.

## Treatment

Treatment is where the two disorders diverge most and where the review's central asymmetry is starkest — a transformed, mechanism-based armamentarium for migraine and a static, borrowed one for TTH — so it is split by modality below. Across both, every acute drug carries MOH liability, which is why frequency, not just efficacy, governs the plan.

### Acute pharmacotherapy

For **migraine**, simple analgesics (NSAIDs, acetaminophen, aspirin ± caffeine) come first, escalating to migraine-specific drugs. The **triptans** (5-HT1B/1D agonists) remain the acute standard: a network meta-analysis of **137 RCTs and 89,445 participants** found every active drug superior to placebo for 2-hour pain freedom and ranked **eletriptan, rizatriptan, sumatriptan, and zolmitriptan** highest — notably **more effective than the newer lasmiditan, rimegepant, and ubrogepant** — supporting triptans as preferred and worth adding to the WHO essential-medicines list. Triptans render **20–30%** of patients pain-free by 2 hours at the cost of transient flushing, tightness, or tingling in about **25%**, and are contraindicated in cardiovascular disease for their vasoconstriction. That contraindication is the opening for the newer classes: the **gepants** (ubrogepant, rimegepant; ~20% pain-free at 2 hours) and the **ditan** lasmiditan act without vasoconstriction and are appropriate for patients with cardiovascular risk. A separate AHRQ meta-analysis of **115 RCTs (28,803 patients)** graded triptans and NSAIDs at moderate-to-high strength of evidence. Opioids and butalbital combinations should be avoided — weak evidence, high MOH and dependence risk.

For **TTH**, acute treatment is **simple or combined analgesics/NSAIDs**, which are effective and first-line; there is **no TTH-specific acute drug**, and combination analgesics are the commonest route into MOH. The absence of a targeted acute agent, thirty years after the triptans transformed migraine, is itself a finding.

### Preventive pharmacotherapy

**Migraine** prevention is offered when attacks are frequent or disabling; the traditional orals — beta-blockers (propranolol), **topiramate**, **amitriptyline**, candesartan, and valproate — reduce headache by roughly **1–3 days/month** versus placebo. A network meta-analysis of **74 trials (32,990 patients)** rated **CGRP monoclonal antibodies, gepants, and topiramate** at high certainty for the ≥50%-responder outcome and judged the **CGRP monoclonal antibodies to have the best combined safety and efficacy of any preventive, followed closely by gepants**, while valproate and amitriptyline carried the most treatment-limiting adverse effects. The CGRP era is the substance of this: the **monoclonal antibodies** (erenumab, fremanezumab, galcanezumab, eptinezumab) and **oral gepants** used preventively (atogepant, rimegepant) all significantly cut monthly migraine days across **19 phase-3 trials (14,584 participants)**, and **onabotulinumtoxinA** is established for chronic migraine specifically.

**TTH** prevention has none of this. The first-line preventive is **amitriptyline**, supported by multiple placebo-controlled trials, with venlafaxine and mirtazapine as alternatives and weaker evidence for gabapentin, topiramate, and tizanidine. CGRP-targeted drugs have **no role** in TTH. The preventive toolkit is essentially the pre-1990 tricyclic, unchanged.

### Neuromodulation and behavioral

Device-based **neuromodulation** applies mostly to migraine: **remote electrical neuromodulation** and **noninvasive vagus nerve stimulation** reached moderate strength of evidence for acute treatment, with external trigeminal nerve stimulation and single-pulse transcranial magnetic stimulation lower. **Biobehavioral therapy** — cognitive behavioral therapy, biofeedback, relaxation, and mindfulness — is endorsed for prevention in both disorders and is, for TTH, one of the **few evidence-based preventive options** alongside amitriptyline, which is why the modality carries disproportionate weight in the TTH-poor landscape.

## Special populations

**Women and menstrual migraine.** With migraine two-to-three times more common in women, the reproductive-hormone axis dominates management. About **a quarter of women** with migraine have **menstrual migraine**, whose attacks are more severe, longer, and less treatment-responsive; **sumatriptan and lasmiditan** give the best acute relief (odds ratio ~**4.62** for 2-hour pain relief), **frovatriptan** is favored for perimenstrual mini-prophylaxis, and NSAIDs or mefenamic acid help at onset. Falling estrogen is the mechanistic trigger.

**Pregnancy and lactation.** Migraine usually **improves during pregnancy**; non-pharmacological measures are preferred, acetaminophen is the safest analgesic, and — critically — **valproate and topiramate must be avoided in women of childbearing age** for teratogenicity, with CGRP-drug pregnancy data still limited. Around menopause, **venlafaxine** treats both migraine and vasomotor symptoms.

**Children and adolescents.** The pediatric evidence is a cautionary tale: the **CHAMP** trial randomized 8–17-year-olds to amitriptyline, topiramate, or placebo and was **stopped for futility** — 50%-responder rates were **52%, 55%, and 61%** respectively (no significant difference), with the active drugs merely adding adverse effects (including one suicide attempt on topiramate). Psychological therapies fare better relatively, reducing headache frequency post-treatment (risk ratio **2.35**, number-needed-to-treat **2.86**) though gains were not sustained at follow-up. The lesson is behavioral-first, with pharmacology disappointing and placebo response enormous.

**Older adults.** Migraine tends to remit with age, so a **new headache at ≥50** is a red flag for secondary causes; comorbidity and polypharmacy narrow triptan use, and older patients are routinely **excluded from the RCTs**, leaving the newer agents' safety in this group largely uncharacterized.

## Prognosis

Migraine's modal course is **favorable** — episodic migraine peaks in the reproductive years and often attenuates thereafter. The adverse trajectory is **chronification**: episodic migraine converts to chronic at **~2.5–3% per year**, but the process is **reversible**, with about **26% of chronic-migraine patients remitting within 2 years**. The strongest, and largely **modifiable**, drivers of progression are high **headache-day frequency**, **depression**, and **acute-medication overuse**, followed by obesity, stressful life events, and ineffective acute treatment — which makes controlling attack frequency and treating comorbid depression genuine prognostic levers. **Episodic TTH** carries a good prognosis; **chronic TTH** is more refractory and distressing, sustained by central sensitization. For both, superimposed **MOH worsens the course**, and its reversal is often the single most effective prognostic intervention.

## Controversies and future directions

Three disputes are live. First, the **spectrum-versus-distinct-entities** question — whether TTH and migraine are one mechanism at different severities or genuinely separate disorders — remains open for want of a biomarker. Second, the **research-attention asymmetry** is now impossible to ignore: migraine has an industry-funded, mechanism-based pipeline while TTH, despite affecting nearly two billion people, has seen little therapeutic progress since the early 2000s — and the trial registry proves it, with the migraine pipeline dominated by **drugs** and the TTH pipeline almost entirely **physiotherapy, manual therapy, and neuromodulation**. Third, **MOH management** is contested — whether to withdraw the overused medication before, or simultaneously with, starting prevention; complete withdrawal for **8–12 weeks** benefits most patients, and the arrival of **gepants and ditans, which do not appear to cause MOH**, may reshape acute treatment away from the withdrawal problem altogether.

The pipeline confirms the split. Migraine's leading edge is a **new drug target beyond CGRP**: the anti-PACAP monoclonal antibody **Lu AG09222** completed a Phase 2 prevention trial in patients who had failed prior preventives (n=874). The rest of the migraine pipeline extends the CGRP era into the populations trials previously skipped — **atogepant** for pediatric prevention (n=450), **ubrogepant** for menstrual migraine prevention (n=496), and a **CGRP antibody plus onabotulinumtoxinA** combination for chronic migraine (n=450). Against all of this, a representative near-future **TTH** trial tests **multimodal physiotherapy plus a guided-meditation app** (n=78) — no drug, no new mechanism. The contrast is the review's closing point: two disorders, one arc, but only one of them is having a revolution.
