# Building a contingency-management program for stimulant use disorder: is it worth it, and how to design it
*2026-07-14 · PubMed 9 · trials 4 · books 0*

> Decision handed over pre-locked (no intent-lock interview): *whether to build a contingency-management (CM)
> program for stimulant use disorder — its effect size versus every other intervention; the design parameters
> that decide whether it works (incentive magnitude, escalation, reset rules, frequency, duration); and the
> low-cost adaptations feasible in a public system.* The reading it beat: a mechanism review of operant
> reinforcement. Answered the build-and-design decision — this is the strongest evidence in the field and the
> prime R2R/publication candidate.

## Verdict

**Build it. Contingency management is the single most effective intervention for stimulant use disorder — nothing else, drug or therapy, matches it — and it can be run cheaply if it is designed right.** Concretely:

1. **Build CM as the core stimulant intervention**, not an optional add-on. Across the comparative literature it is the *only* treatment reliably associated with reduced stimulant use, with a medium-large effect (Cohen d ≈ 0.7 for stimulant abstinence); no pharmacotherapy comes close (see the methamphetamine-pharmacotherapy report).
2. **Use the prize-based ("fishbowl") format** to make it affordable: patients draw from a bowl on each objectively-verified negative test, most draws small, a few large — comparable efficacy to vouchers at a fraction of the cost, and deliverable by existing counsellors.
3. **Get the design parameters right — they decide success or failure:** reward a **single, objectively-verified behaviour** (urine-verified abstinence gives the biggest effect; attendance is cheaper and still works); make the **incentive large enough** (effect scales with magnitude — under-powered incentives are the main way CM fails); pay **immediately** at the negative test; **escalate** the reward with each consecutive negative test and **reset** it to baseline on a positive or missed test (re-escalating on recovery); test **frequently** (2–3×/week); and run it **long enough** (≥12 weeks — longer active treatment is the strongest predictor of durable benefit).
4. **Plan for decay.** Effects attenuate once incentives stop, so pair CM with a skills/community-reinforcement component and use adequate duration rather than expecting a short course to hold.
5. **Mind the ethics of the prize format** — the fishbowl's gambling-like structure warrants screening patients with a gambling history (offer them fixed rewards instead).

Confidence: **high** — this is among the best-replicated findings in addiction treatment, with converging meta-analyses, a network meta-analysis, and dismantling studies on the design parameters.

## The effect size, against everything else

The case for building CM is that when the whole treatment landscape is compared head-to-head, CM is what survives. The most comprehensive cocaine-treatment meta-analysis (157 studies, 15,842 participants) clustered every treatment — psychotherapy, CM, and eight medication classes — and found that **only contingency management was significantly associated with a negative cocaine test (odds ratio 2.13, 95% CI 1.62–2.80), robust across every sensitivity analysis.** The definitive network meta-analysis of psychosocial interventions for cocaine/amphetamine addiction (50 RCTs, 6,942 participants) reached the same conclusion from the other direction: **CM combined with a community-reinforcement approach was the only intervention that increased abstinence over usual care** — at end of treatment (OR 2.84), at 12 weeks (OR 7.60), and at longest follow-up (OR 3.08) — while also cutting dropout (OR 3.63), and it beat CBT and 12-step programmes in head-to-head comparisons.

The effect-size estimates are consistent and clinically meaningful: a meta-analysis of CM in patients on opioid-agonist treatment put **stimulant abstinence at Cohen d = 0.70** (and abstinence generally at 0.58, treatment adherence at 0.62); the classic meta-analysis across substances gave d = 0.42 overall and 0.66 for cocaine; and the Cochrane review of psychosocial treatments for stimulant use disorder named CM the most promising approach, with continuous abstinence RR 1.89. For a disorder with no approved pharmacotherapy, this is the highest-yield intervention available — which is exactly why it is the strongest publication and reimbursement-reform (R2R) target in the accreditation portfolio.

## The design parameters — where CM is won or lost

CM is not a single thing; it is a schedule, and the schedule determines whether it works. The parameters below are not stylistic choices — each has been shown to move the effect size, and getting them wrong is how well-intentioned programmes fail.

- **Target one clearly-defined, objectively-verified behaviour.** The voucher meta-analysis (30 studies) found abstinence-targeted programmes produced roughly double the effect of attendance-targeted ones (r = 0.32 vs 0.15). Urine-verified stimulant abstinence is the highest-yield target; attendance CM is a cheaper, easier entry point that still works and is defensible when the immediate goal is retention (report #7).
- **Magnitude matters — this is the commonest failure.** The same meta-analysis found **greater monetary value associated with larger effect sizes**; a critical-period analysis showed higher-magnitude reinforcers drive early abstinence that predicts the whole trajectory. A token incentive produces a token effect. Escalating voucher programmes historically reached four figures over 12 weeks; prize-based designs achieve most of the benefit at far lower expected cost by making large prizes rare.
- **Immediacy.** Effects were larger when the voucher was delivered *more immediately* — reward at the moment the negative test is confirmed, not at a later visit.
- **Escalation with reset.** The reinforcer value should rise with each consecutive negative test (rewarding *sustained* abstinence) and reset to baseline after a positive or missed sample, then re-escalate — the schedule that shaped the original voucher evidence.
- **Frequency.** Test 2–3×/week: it matches the detection window for stimulants and multiplies the reinforcement opportunities.
- **Duration, and the decay problem.** Effects shrink once incentives stop — the honest limitation of CM — but they do not vanish: a meta-analysis of objective abstinence up to a year after incentives ended still favoured CM (OR 1.22), and **longer active treatment was the single moderator that improved long-term abstinence.** So run it for at least 12 weeks, taper rather than stop abruptly, and pair it with a community-reinforcement/skills component so there is something to maintain the behaviour when the bowl is put away.

## The low-cost adaptation for a public system

Cost is the standard objection, and the prize-based ("fishbowl") method is the standard answer. Instead of guaranteed vouchers, each negative test earns draws from a bowl in which most slips are small ("good job"/low value), a minority are worth more, and one is a jumbo prize; the *expected* cost per patient is a fraction of a voucher schedule while efficacy is preserved. Critically for a public clinic, it can be delivered by existing treatment staff: a community-programme implementation using Petry's fishbowl technique, run by the centres' own counsellors, significantly improved group attendance — evidence that the model transfers out of specialist research settings. Further cost levers include community-donated prizes, on-site goods rather than cash, and starting with an attendance target before graduating to abstinence.

Two guardrails belong in the protocol. First, the **ethics of the prize-draw**: its gambling-like structure is a documented concern, so screen for gambling history and offer those patients a fixed-reward schedule instead. Second, **avoid coercion and protect the therapeutic relationship** — incentives are for engagement, not leverage.

## Where this verdict inverts

- **A gambling-disorder history** shifts that patient from prize-draw to fixed vouchers.
- **If the binding problem is getting people through the door rather than abstinence**, an attendance-targeted CM (cheaper, smaller effect) is the right first build — it doubles as the retention intervention in report #7.
- **CM is not a monotherapy for comorbid psychosis or severe medical instability** — those need their own pathway; CM sits alongside, targeting use.
- **If the programme cannot fund an adequate incentive magnitude at all**, a deliberately under-powered CM is worse than an honest decision to invest elsewhere first — magnitude is not the place to economise; the prize format is.

## What would change this verdict

Nothing about *whether* CM works — that is settled. The live questions are implementation and durability, and the registry reflects it: ongoing trials are dominated by CM **deployment** studies, including in low- and middle-income and Southeast Asian settings directly relevant here (a Vietnam CM-plus-Matrix trial, a Hawaii community CM implementation, and app-based CM platforms), plus work on sustaining gains after incentives end. The evidence gap CM research is now filling is exactly the one a public service cares about — how to run it affordably and make the benefit last — which makes a well-designed local CM programme both a clinical intervention and a publishable implementation study.
