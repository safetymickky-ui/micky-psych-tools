# Choosing an Antidepressant by Mechanism — a Phenotype-to-Drug Selection Guide
*2026-07-13 · PubMed 17 · trials 3 · books 0 · a mechanistic self-study selection reference (with the α₂δ ligands as the non-monoamine option)*

> Decision locked with an intent-lock round: *which antidepressant — or, when the mechanism fits better, an α₂δ ligand — to start or switch to for an adult outpatient, chosen by matching the patient's symptom/tolerability phenotype to the drug's receptor/transporter mechanism, occupancy behaviour, pharmacokinetics, and adverse-effect liability.* Built as a **selection instrument** (the answer to item 8), not a receptor encyclopedia: items 1–7 are the mechanistic argument that earns the choice. Full antidepressant spectrum + the α₂δ ligands; dense clinician self-study register.

> Skipped: bipolar depression and paediatric selection (out of the locked scope); MAOIs and TCAs carried only where they anchor a mechanistic contrast, not as routine first choices; augmentation/combination beyond the single-agent choice.

## Bottom line

**For the average patient, no antidepressant is meaningfully more effective than another — so stop selecting on efficacy and select on mechanism matched to phenotype.** The class is a near-tie on response (the 21-drug network meta-analysis spreads all agents between OR 1.37 and 2.13 over placebo, with head-to-head differences of only 1.15–1.55), and PET shows why: the minimum effective dose already occupies ~80% of the serotonin transporter, and pushing higher buys dropout, not response. What *does* separate the drugs is their off-target receptor/transporter fingerprint, their half-life, and their early-treatment behaviour — and those map cleanly onto patients:

- **Anxious / agitated / activation-prone, or young:** start low and titrate slowly (escitalopram or sertraline), because the first two weeks are anxiogenic before they are anxiolytic; avoid a fast serotonergic ramp and watch for akathisia.
- **Insomnia, weight loss, poor appetite, agitation:** mirtazapine (H1 + 5-HT2A/2C/α2 antagonism → sedation and appetite) turns "side effects" into the therapeutic effect.
- **Emotional blunting or sexual side effects are the deal-breaker:** bupropion (NDRI, no serotonergic blunting or sexual dysfunction), or vortioxetine or agomelatine; but first rule out that the blunting is residual depression, not the drug.
- **Will miss doses, or wants a clean stop later:** avoid short-half-life paroxetine and venlafaxine (worst discontinuation); fluoxetine self-tapers; whatever you pick, plan a hyperbolic taper from the start.
- **Prominent somatic/psychic anxiety, needs fast relief, SSRI/SNRI-intolerant or partial responder, and no substance-use history:** an α₂δ ligand (pregabalin) is a legitimate non-monoamine choice for anxiety — but it is not an antidepressant, and its dependence liability rules it out in anyone with an opioid or addiction history.

**What not to promise:** that switching within the serotonergic drugs will reliably fix emotional blunting (it affects ~46% and is partly the illness); that a dose increase will add efficacy (mostly it adds side effects); or that stopping is trivial (about 1 in 6–7 get discontinuation symptoms, a minority severe). **Confidence: moderate-high** for the spine — efficacy near-tie, select on mechanism/tolerability — which rests on two large network meta-analyses plus PET occupancy data; **moderate** for the specific phenotype→drug mappings, which lean on mechanistic inference and adverse-effect epidemiology rather than phenotype-stratified head-to-head trials, of which there are few.

## Why efficacy barely sorts the class — and occupancy explains it

The single fact that makes this a *mechanism* decision rather than an efficacy decision: the drugs are nearly interchangeable on average response. In the definitive 522-trial, 116,477-patient network meta-analysis, every one of 21 antidepressants beat placebo, but the whole class fell between OR 2.13 (amitriptyline) and OR 1.37 (reboxetine), and once placebo-controlled trials were included the between-drug efficacy differences shrank to OR 1.15–1.55 with wide credible intervals. Acceptability (all-cause dropout) separated them a little more — agomelatine, citalopram, escitalopram, fluoxetine, sertraline, and vortioxetine were the best tolerated; amitriptyline, clomipramine, duloxetine, fluvoxamine, reboxetine, trazodone, and venlafaxine the worst — which is the first hint that *tolerability*, not efficacy, is the axis that actually moves.

PET occupancy tells you why efficacy is flat. The usually-effective minimum dose of every marketed SSRI already produces ~70–80% serotonin-transporter (SERT) occupancy; high therapeutic doses of venlafaxine, sertraline, and citalopram push that only to ~85%. Because the dose–occupancy curve is saturating, the dose–efficacy curve is flat while the dose–adverse-effect (and dropout) curve keeps climbing — so a dose increase, on average, trades a sliver of extra occupancy for a measurable rise in side-effect discontinuation. The clinical reading is precise: **dose is a tolerability lever, not an efficacy lever.** Raising the dose is justified for the individual non-responder (the ~15% of unoccupied transporter is real headroom for some), but it is not a reliable route to more average benefit, and "the drug failed" after a dose that was already at ~80% occupancy is usually a reason to switch mechanism, not to climb.

This is the foundation of the whole instrument: if the drugs are a near-tie on efficacy and dose mostly buys side effects, then the rational selection variable is *which side-effect and pharmacokinetic profile fits this patient* — which is set by mechanism.

## The receptor/transporter fingerprint is the side-effect forecast

An antidepressant's off-target binding profile predicts, before you prescribe, the adverse phenotype the patient will meet — so the "serotonin receptor map" is really a side-effect map you select against:

- **Serotonin receptor subtypes** are the mechanistic vocabulary. Raising synaptic 5-HT hits many receptors at once, and the downstream effects diverge: 5-HT2A/2C and 5-HT3 stimulation drives much of the early anxiety, insomnia, sexual dysfunction, and nausea of SSRIs, whereas *antagonism* at 5-HT2A/2C (mirtazapine, agomelatine, vortioxetine's partial profile) tends to protect sleep, appetite, and sexual function. The same review literature that maps agonist/antagonist actions across 5-HT1A/1B/2A/2C/3/4/6/7 also explains the class's signature slowness — the therapeutic effect waits on receptor adaptation, not on the immediate reuptake block (see the next section).
- **The SSRIs are not interchangeable within their own class.** Escitalopram is the cleanest — an allosteric SERT ligand with minimal off-target binding, and the head-to-head data rate it at the top of the efficacy/tolerability trade-off. Paroxetine carries muscarinic (anticholinergic) antagonism, mild noradrenaline reuptake inhibition, sedation, weight gain, the most sexual dysfunction, a short half-life, and potent CYP2D6 inhibition — the busiest, least forgiving fingerprint. Sertraline adds dopamine-transporter and sigma-1 activity (mildly activating, GI-heavy) with only moderate interaction risk. Fluoxetine is activating (partly 5-HT2C antagonism) with a very long half-life. Fluvoxamine is sigma-1-active and a strong CYP1A2 inhibitor. So "start an SSRI" is under-specified: the *within-class* choice is already a mechanism decision.
- **The non-SSRIs are selected for their fingerprints, not against them.** Mirtazapine's H1/5-HT2A/5-HT2C/α2 antagonism *is* the reason to choose it in the insomniac, anorexic, agitated patient. Bupropion's noradrenaline–dopamine reuptake inhibition explains why it is the one agent with essentially no sexual dysfunction and the least emotional blunting — and why it is activating and lowers seizure threshold. Vortioxetine's multimodal profile (SERT inhibition plus 5-HT3/5-HT7 antagonism and 5-HT1A agonism) is marketed as pro-cognitive and lower-blunting. Agomelatine (melatonergic MT1/MT2 agonism + 5-HT2C antagonism) is chosen for sleep and a benign sexual/discontinuation profile.

The practical move: read the patient's vulnerable systems (sleep, weight, sexual function, cognition, cardiac QT, drug interactions) and pick the fingerprint that either avoids the vulnerability or converts it into benefit.

## The first two weeks: activation, akathisia, and the anxiogenic lag

Selection has to account for the *transient* phase, because that is when patients stop the drug. Reuptake block raises synaptic 5-HT within hours, but the antidepressant response takes ~2–4 weeks — the lag reflects time-dependent neuroadaptation (autoreceptor desensitisation and downstream receptor remodelling), not the acute transporter block. In that window the raised serotonergic tone is anxiogenic before it is anxiolytic: early jitteriness, insomnia, agitation, and worsened anxiety are common, especially in anxious or younger patients and with more activating agents.

Akathisia is the sharp end of this. SSRI-induced akathisia — an inner restlessness distinct from the illness — is under-recognised and matters because it is linked to suicidal ideation and to non-adherence; its predisposing factors are precisely the things selection controls: a recent dose increase, multiple akathisia-inducing drugs, and prior akathisia. Recognise it with the Barnes Akathisia Rating Scale rather than reading it as "worsening depression," and treat by reducing the offending dose and adding a centrally-acting β-blocker, a short benzodiazepine, or an anticholinergic.

Selection consequences: in the anxious, agitated, or young patient, **start at half the usual dose for the first several days and titrate slowly**, prefer a less activating, cleaner agent (escitalopram, sertraline) over a fast serotonergic ramp, warn the patient that anxiety may transiently worsen before it improves, and treat early activation as a titration/recognition problem — not as evidence the drug has failed.

## Emotional blunting: half the patients, and half of it is the illness

Emotional blunting — a narrowed range of feeling, reduced ability to cry or feel pleasure, "not caring" — is reported by ~46% of depressed patients on antidepressants in the largest survey, is common to essentially all monoaminergic agents (somewhat less with bupropion), and is more frequent with SSRIs than with other classes. The mechanistic account is serotonergic dampening of limbic reactivity via 5-HT2A/2C and prefrontal circuits.

But the adjudication that changes practice is this: blunting scores correlate tightly with residual depression severity (the survey found blunting rising with Hospital Anxiety and Depression depression score), so **emotional blunting is partly a symptom of incompletely treated depression, not purely a drug side effect.** The first response to a blunting complaint is therefore to check whether the depression is actually in remission — because if it is not, the answer is better treatment, not less. When blunting genuinely persists into remission, the mechanism-guided moves are to switch away from pure serotonergic dampening: to bupropion (dopaminergic/noradrenergic, the lowest-blunting profile), or to vortioxetine or agomelatine. The vortioxetine evidence is real but soft — an 8-week open-label switch study in SSRI/SNRI partial responders with blunting showed a 29.8-point fall in Oxford Depression Questionnaire score with 50% no longer blunted and 47% remitting — but it was uncontrolled and industry-sponsored, so read it as "a reasonable mechanism-matched switch," not as proof of a specific anti-blunting drug effect separable from simply treating the depression better.

## Half-life is the discontinuation you are choosing at the start

Pharmacokinetics is where the selection reaches furthest into the future, because the half-life you pick at initiation sets the withdrawal you will manage at the end. The mechanism is the same occupancy curve as before, run in reverse: SERT occupancy is a hyperbolic function of plasma concentration, so as a drug clears, occupancy falls slowly at first and then steeply — and a short-half-life drug traverses that steep part fast. That is why the discontinuation-symptom burden sorts by half-life and active-metabolite status: the short-half-life agents with no self-tapering metabolite (paroxetine, venlafaxine, desvenlafaxine) are the worst offenders, while long-half-life fluoxetine (via norfluoxetine) effectively tapers itself.

The magnitude deserves a calibrated number rather than the alarmist or dismissive extremes. A 79-study, 21,002-patient meta-analysis found at least one discontinuation symptom in 31% after stopping an antidepressant versus 17% after stopping placebo — so the *drug-attributable* incidence is roughly 15%, about one in six to seven patients, not the majority. Severe discontinuation symptoms were genuinely uncommon (2.8% on antidepressant vs 0.6% on placebo), but they concentrate in the same short-half-life agents (imipramine, paroxetine, and the venlafaxine/desvenlafaxine pair had the highest severity). The reading: withdrawal is real, a minority experience is severe, most of the "everyone gets terrible withdrawal" signal in uncontrolled reports is inflated by non-specific and nocebo effects — and the risk is largely chosen at prescription by half-life.

This drives two selection rules. First, in a patient likely to miss doses or who already anticipates wanting to stop, **avoid paroxetine and venlafaxine**; fluoxetine is the forgiving choice. Second, whatever you start, plan the exit as a **hyperbolic taper** — reduce by proportions of the *occupancy* effect (increasingly small dose decrements, down to doses well below the minimum therapeutic dose, over months) rather than by fixed linear dose steps, because equal dose cuts produce escalating occupancy drops near the bottom of the range. Half-life also governs interaction-based selection: paroxetine and fluoxetine are potent CYP2D6 inhibitors, fluvoxamine a strong CYP1A2 inhibitor, and vortioxetine a CYP2D6 substrate (halve the dose in CYP2D6 poor metabolisers or with strong 2D6 inhibitors; its own ~66-h half-life makes it comparatively forgiving on missed doses).

## Reaching past the monoamines: when an α₂δ ligand wins, and its ceiling

The α₂δ ligands (pregabalin, gabapentin) are the deliberate non-monoamine option in this list — a different mechanism entirely, useful exactly where the serotonergic drugs are the wrong tool. They bind the α₂δ-1 auxiliary subunit of voltage-gated calcium channels and reduce the activity-dependent synaptic release of excitatory transmitters (glutamate, noradrenaline, substance P), damping neuronal hyperexcitability rather than manipulating monoamine tone. That mechanism has no antidepressant action but a genuine, fast anxiolytic one.

The efficacy is competitive *for anxiety*: in the 89-trial, 25,441-patient generalised-anxiety network meta-analysis, pregabalin reduced HAM-A by 2.79 points versus placebo — in the same band as duloxetine (−3.13), venlafaxine (−2.69), and escitalopram (−2.45) and with relatively good acceptability, whereas quetiapine was numerically strongest (−3.60) but poorly tolerated and paroxetine and benzodiazepines were effective but poorly tolerated. Pregabalin works across 150–600 mg/day as monotherapy and, usefully, as add-on in patients who have only partially responded to an SSRI/SNRI; it relieves somatic and psychic anxiety alike and acts faster than antidepressants. Its dose-related liabilities are somnolence and dizziness.

The ceiling is dependence, and it is a hard one. Manufacturer-linked reviews describe a "low potential for withdrawal," but the independent literature disagrees: a systematic review found gabapentinoid misuse in ~1.6% of the general population but 3–68% among people who misuse opioids, with 11,940 abuse reports logged to an international database over 2004–2015 (over 75% of them since 2012) and increasing detection in post-mortem toxicology, especially combined with opioids. So the selection rule is sharp: an α₂δ ligand is a legitimate choice for prominent somatic or psychic anxiety when speed matters, when SSRIs/SNRIs are intolerable or only partially effective, and when the patient has **no** history of substance use — and it is contraindicated as a matter of selection in anyone with an opioid or addiction history. Prefer pregabalin over gabapentin (cleaner, dose-linear pharmacokinetics), keep it time-limited where possible, taper over at least a week (hyperbolically if used long-term), and never treat it as an antidepressant substitute — it will not treat the depression it is sitting next to.

## Where this inverts

The "efficacy is a near-tie, select on tolerability" spine has real boundaries:

- **Severe or melancholic inpatients:** the efficacy differences the head-to-head data hint at (amitriptyline, venlafaxine, escitalopram, mirtazapine at the top) may matter more, and with monitoring available the tolerability penalty of a higher-efficacy agent is more acceptable — here efficacy can legitimately outrank fingerprint.
- **Maintenance, not just acute:** the selection should survive the relapse-prevention phase. The maintenance network meta-analysis found desvenlafaxine, paroxetine, sertraline, venlafaxine, and vortioxetine reasonable on combined relapse-prevention, acceptability, and tolerability — but note the collision with the discontinuation data (paroxetine, venlafaxine prevent relapse yet are the hardest to stop), which is exactly the trade-off to name to the patient up front.
- **Cardiac and elderly:** citalopram (and to a lesser extent escitalopram) carry dose-dependent QTc prolongation — a selection constraint at higher doses and in older patients.
- **Pharmacogenetics:** CYP2D6 poor metabolisers are a real selection signal for drugs cleared by 2D6 (dose-adjust vortioxetine; be wary of paroxetine/fluoxetine self-inhibition).
- **Out of scope by design:** bipolar depression (antidepressant monotherapy is the wrong frame entirely) and paediatric selection were excluded at intent-lock.

## What would change this

Three live trials sit on the load-bearing parts of this instrument:

- **Hyperbolic vs linear tapering (NCT07393919, Phase 4, n=150, University of Verona, readout ~2027)** tests the Horowitz mechanism head-on. A positive result hardens the half-life-based selection rule and the mandatory hyperbolic taper; a null result would soften "the taper you choose at the start" back toward "any slow taper."
- **Primary-care supported deprescribing (NCT07398053, n=324, Ghent, readout ~2027)** speaks to whether the discontinuation burden is manageable in routine practice — the real-world counterpart to the Henssler incidence estimate.
- **Pharmacogenomic-guided prescribing (NCT06729541, multigenic, n=600, Peking University, completion ~2026)** is the one that could partly *replace* phenotype-matching with genotype-matching. If large multigenic-guided selection finally shows a robust outcome advantage (prior evidence, e.g. the completed n=99 oncology trial, is thin), the front-line question shifts from "which fingerprint fits this presentation" toward "which drug fits this metaboliser/receptor genotype." Until then, CYP2D6/2C19 status is the only genetic input that is already actionable, and mechanism-by-phenotype remains the operative logic.

Nothing on the horizon threatens the core finding — that antidepressant efficacy is a near-tie and selection belongs to mechanism, occupancy, pharmacokinetics, and phenotype. That is stable; what is moving is only how finely we individualise within it.
