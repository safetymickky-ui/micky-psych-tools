# Intermittent explosive disorder — Rx decision
*2026-07-11 · PubMed 16 · trials 6 · books 0*

Frame: **Rx** — how to treat an adult presenting with recurrent impulsive-aggressive outbursts diagnosed as IED: which drug, at what dose, with what psychotherapy, and what not to reach for. Chosen over Teaching because a wrong first move is paid by a patient and the people around them (an ineffective mood stabilizer with a 17% adverse-event dropout, an untreated comorbid substance problem, a promise of "cure" that fails), not by a trainee in a lecture. According to PubMed, the evidence base is unusually thin for how common the disorder is.

## Verdict
First secure the diagnosis — recurrent, out-of-proportion, *reactive/impulsive* aggression, not the premeditated aggression of antisocial/psychopathic patterns, and not aggression better explained by mania, psychosis, intoxication/withdrawal, or a neurological cause — and treat the near-universal comorbidity, because IED almost always co-occurs with (and usually predates) a mood, anxiety, substance, or personality disorder. Make the evidence-based core an **SSRI plus anger-focused CBT**: **fluoxetine 20–60 mg/day** (titrated to tolerance) is the single best-supported drug, with a randomized anti-aggressive effect that is real but incomplete — under half remit — and **12 weeks of group or individual CBT** is the only psychotherapy with randomized support and is durable at 3 months, adverse-event-free, and scalable in groups. Do **not** reflexively reach for divalproex (the one adequately powered trial was negative in IED specifically), reserve anticonvulsants/antipsychotics/lithium/β-blockers for SSRI non-response or a specific comorbidity, and do not promise the patient that treatment abolishes the outbursts — the honest, evidenced target is a reduction in frequency and intensity, not cure. Confidence: **moderate-low** — the entire pharmacological case rests on one positive RCT (n=100) and the psychotherapy case on one small RCT (n=45), with no head-to-head, no maintenance data, and no drug FDA-approved for IED.

## What the evidence says
IED is common, early, and almost never treated for what it is — and that neglect, not any single drug, is the clinical problem the evidence must serve. In the National Comorbidity Survey Replication (n=9282), DSM-IV IED carried a lifetime prevalence of 7.3% and a 12-month prevalence of 3.9% under broad criteria, with a mean onset at age 14, a mean of 43 lifetime attacks, injuries in 180 per 100 lifetime cases — and yet only 28.8% ever received treatment directed at their anger. The adolescent supplement (n=6483) put lifetime IED at 7.8% with onset around age 12 and 80% persistence into 12-month caseness, with just 6.5% treated for anger. A 2025 global meta-analysis (29 studies, N=182,112, 17 countries) pooled lifetime prevalence at 5.1% and 12-month at 4.4%, running higher in clinical (10.5%), refugee (8.5%), and adolescent samples, with male sex the dominant correlate (OR 3.39) alongside trauma exposure and psychiatric comorbidity. So the target is a disorder affecting roughly one in twenty people, beginning in childhood, reaching a clinician — if ever — after years of damage, against a treatment literature of only a handful of randomized trials.

IED is the clinical face of impulsive, reactive (affective) aggression, and that phenotype is what makes the pharmacology rational. Reactive aggression is neurobiologically distinct from the premeditated, instrumental aggression of psychopathy — the separation matters because it maps onto low central serotonergic function and a hyper-reactive amygdala poorly regulated by orbitofrontal and prefrontal cortex. Enhance serotonergic tone, the reasoning goes, and the impulsive-aggressive response should dampen. That is exactly what the one well-designed pharmacological RCT tested, and it remains the load-bearing evidence for the whole field: Coccaro's 12-week, double-blind trial of fluoxetine versus placebo in 100 individuals with IED. Overt Aggression Scale–Modified (OAS-M) aggression and irritability scores fell significantly as early as week 2 (p<.01 for aggression, p<.001 for irritability at endpoint), fluoxetine beat placebo on Clinical Global Impressions–Improvement response (p<.001), and the anti-aggressive effect held independent of any effect on depression or anxiety — this is a genuine anti-aggressive drug action, not a mood side-effect. But the ceiling is as important as the signal: full or partial remission of impulsive aggression occurred in only 46% of fluoxetine-treated subjects, and full remission in a minority. The best drug we have, in the best trial we have, still left more than half of patients symptomatic.

The instinct to reach for a "mood stabilizer" in an explosive patient is not supported for IED itself, and here the literature has to be adjudicated rather than pooled. The largest relevant trial — Hollander's 12-week divalproex-versus-placebo study (n=246 across Cluster B personality disorder, IED, and PTSD) — was negative in the intent-to-treat sample and, decisively, negative in the IED subgroup specifically (n=116); its only positive signal came from the separate Cluster B subgroup (n=96), and it carried a 17% versus 3% discontinuation rate for adverse events. The one trial that actually enrolled a real IED cohort and tested an anticonvulsant found nothing for IED. The anticonvulsant "successes" cited elsewhere come from mixed impulsive-aggression samples, not IED: oxcarbazepine beat placebo on both primary measures in 48 impulsively aggressive outpatients (9 dropped out for adverse events), and phenytoin, carbamazepine, and valproate each beat placebo in a 29-man impulsive-aggression trial. When the Cochrane review pooled all 14 antiepileptic trials (672 participants), it concluded the evidence was insufficient for any firm recommendation — valproate, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, and phenytoin were each positive in at least one study and null in another, with adverse effects consistently more common on drug. A 2022 systematic review of impulse-control-disorder pharmacotherapy found only six IED RCTs in existence and named oxcarbazepine and fluoxetine as the best supported, which is itself a statement about how bare the cupboard is. So where an SSRI and an anticonvulsant both look "positive," I believe the SSRI signal more for IED — it is the only agent tested in a pure-IED randomized trial with a clean design, while the anticonvulsant positives are borrowed from heterogeneous samples and the single anticonvulsant trial that did contain a genuine IED subgroup was negative in it. That ranks an SSRI first and an anticonvulsant as a second-line option, most defensible when an affective-instability or Cluster B coloring is present — not the reverse.

The psychotherapy evidence is thinner in volume but cleaner in direction, and it should sit in the core of the plan rather than after the drug fails. The only randomized psychotherapy trial — McCloskey's 12-week study in 45 adults with IED — compared group and individual cognitive-behavioral therapy (a cognitive-relaxation-coping-skills model adapted from Deffenbacher) against a wait-list; both active arms reduced aggression, anger, hostile thinking, and depressive symptoms while improving anger control, gains were maintained at 3-month follow-up, and group delivery was essentially equal to individual. For a chronic, early-onset disorder that leaves roughly half of drug-treated patients still symptomatic, a durable, adverse-event-free intervention deliverable in scalable group format belongs alongside the SSRI from the start — the combination, not either alone, is the defensible evidence-based package.

IED is almost never the whole picture, and its comorbidity structure changes what you do. It is highly comorbid with mood, anxiety, substance-use, and post-traumatic disorders; across the NCS-R, its adolescent supplement, and a large clinical dataset, IED's onset generally *precedes* that of its comorbid disorders, and aggression scores are highest when IED and a comorbid disorder coexist — the empirical argument for diagnosing and treating both, rather than dismissing the aggression as "just" a symptom of the depression or the drinking. About half of clinical IED patients also meet criteria for a personality disorder, and comorbidity with antisocial or borderline PD marks the highest-aggression subgroup (though not, notably, higher impulsivity). Two practical consequences follow: treat the comorbid disorder in its own right — an SSRI conveniently serves both an IED and a depressive/anxious target — and address substance use directly, because alcohol is the single most powerful modifiable amplifier of impulsive aggression, and an SSRI layered on top of unaddressed drinking inherits that barrier.

## Where it breaks
- **Wrong diagnosis, wrong treatment.** Premeditated/instrumental aggression (antisocial personality, psychopathy — proactive, not reactive), and aggression driven by mania, psychosis, intoxication or withdrawal, delirium, traumatic brain injury, epilepsy, or dementia are **not IED** and demand their own management; treating them as IED mis-medicates and wastes the window. Confirm the aggression is impulsive and out of proportion before applying any of the above.
- **Fluoxetine's ceiling.** ~46% partial/full remission means most patients are not fully controlled; anti-aggressive onset takes ~2 weeks; watch for SSRI-emergent activation/irritability and, in adolescents, the class suicidality warning. It reduces, it does not abolish.
- **Divalproex / anticonvulsants.** Negative for IED per se (divalproex); valproate is teratogenic and should be avoided in anyone who may become pregnant; 17% adverse-event discontinuation in the pivotal trial; oxcarbazepine risks hyponatremia. The supporting data are mostly non-IED "impulsive aggression."
- **Antipsychotics, lithium, β-blockers, α2-agonists.** Used off older case series and aggression-in-other-disorders trials, **not IED RCTs** — reserve for refractory cases or a specific comorbidity, and weigh metabolic, extrapyramidal, and monitoring burden; not first-line for uncomplicated IED.
- **Children and adolescents.** No positive dedicated IED drug RCT; the one childhood guanfacine (Intuniv) trial enrolled just 11. Extrapolating adult drug data downward is unsupported — favor parent management training and psychosocial intervention first.
- **No maintenance evidence.** Every trial is ≤12 weeks; how long to continue a responder, and the relapse rate on stopping, are unknown — a real gap for a lifelong-onset disorder.

## What would change this
- **Novel mechanisms now in trials.** Two genuinely new pharmacological mechanisms are being tested in IED for the first time in a decade, both from Coccaro's group: **pimavanserin** (5-HT2A inverse agonist, NCT05895513, phase 2, ~2027 readout) and **nitrous oxide** (NCT06118567, phase 2, ~2027). A positive result in either would be the first new drug class for IED since the SSRI data.
- **A completed trial that never reached the literature.** **SRX246**, a brain-penetrant vasopressin V1a-receptor antagonist — mechanistically compelling, since vasopressin drives aggression and V1a blockade reduces it in animal and fMRI work — was tested in a completed 97-patient DSM-5 IED trial (NCT02055638, done 2016) that posted results to the registry but has **no dedicated peer-reviewed IED publication** I could locate; the same compound was published only for Huntington's-disease irritability. Its IED efficacy outcome is therefore `[unverified]` in the published record — a classic completed-but-unpublished signal that could move the field in either direction if reported.
- **An industry program that stalled.** Otsuka's **AVP-786** (deudextromethorphan/quinidine) IED trial (NCT03420222, phase 2) was **terminated** having enrolled only 10 of a planned multi-site cohort — a soft negative signal for that mechanism.
- **What would *not* change it.** No large SSRI replication and no head-to-head or maintenance trial is registered, so fluoxetine's primacy rests on a single 100-patient study for the foreseeable future, and continuation duration stays a matter of clinical judgment rather than evidence.

## Close
Delivered: a decision-first Rx report on treating intermittent explosive disorder — secure the (reactive-aggression) diagnosis and treat comorbidity, then an SSRI (fluoxetine 20–60 mg) plus 12-week anger-focused CBT as the evidence-based core, with divalproex de-prioritized on its negative IED trial and newer mechanisms flagged as pending. PubMed 16 · trials 6 · books 0; confidence moderate-low, limited by a base of one positive drug RCT and one small psychotherapy RCT with no maintenance or head-to-head data. One `[unverified]` gap remains: the completed SRX246 IED trial's efficacy outcome is registry-posted but unpublished.
