Attention-deficit/hyperactivity disorder is the most-studied childhood neurodevelopmental disorder and, increasingly, an adult one. This review is written against a specific backdrop: the field is in the middle of three quiet paradigm shifts — from a categorical illness to the extreme of a continuous trait, from a childhood condition to a lifespan one, and from a disorder studied in isolation to one whose defining feature may be how thoroughly it overlaps with everything else, autism most of all. Each section carries the older consensus and the newer signal.

## Definition & nosology

ADHD is defined by developmentally excessive, impairing inattention and/or hyperactivity–impulsivity with onset in childhood. The construct is old; its boundaries have moved sharply. DSM-5 (2013), carried into DSM-5-TR, recast the former subtypes as **presentations** (predominantly inattentive, predominantly hyperactive-impulsive, combined) that can shift over time, raised the age-of-onset criterion from 7 to 12 years, lowered the adult symptom threshold from six to five symptoms, and — the change that anchors much of this review — **deleted the long-standing rule that autism excluded a concurrent ADHD diagnosis**. A World Federation of ADHD international consensus statement, endorsed by 80 authors across 27 countries, distilled the evidence base into **208 empirically supported conclusions**, explicitly to counter the perception that ADHD is a contested or manufactured diagnosis; it judged diagnosis "reliable and valid when evaluated with standard diagnostic criteria."

The deeper nosological shift is dimensional. Genome-wide data show that the case–control genetics of clinically diagnosed ADHD are strongly concordant with the genetics of continuously distributed ADHD symptoms measured in the general population — evidence that "clinical diagnosis of ADHD is an extreme expression of continuous heritable traits" rather than a discrete category with a natural boundary. This reframing runs underneath the diagnostic, epidemiological, and treatment questions that follow.

## Epidemiology

Two prevalence numbers, honestly reconciled, tell the epidemiological story. The best pooled worldwide estimate of childhood/adolescent ADHD is **3.4% (95% CI 2.6–4.5)**, and — importantly — that pooled figure has **not varied by geographic region or by year of data collection** once methods are standardized. Apparent international and temporal "epidemics" are largely artifacts of differing diagnostic practice, not a rising true prevalence. Against that, administrative U.S. figures are far higher and still climbing: the 2022 National Survey of Children's Health put **ever-diagnosed ADHD at 11.4% (about 7.1 million children, roughly 1 in 9)** and current ADHD at 10.5%, approximately **one million more diagnosed children than in 2016**; U.S. household-survey data likewise show a rise from **8.5% to 9.5% across 2009–2017**. The gap between a ~3.4% pooled prevalence and an 11.4% diagnosed prevalence is the empirical seed of the overdiagnosis debate taken up in the final section.

ADHD is now firmly a lifespan diagnosis. Adjusting to 2020 world demographics, **persistent (childhood-onset) adult ADHD affects an estimated 2.58% of adults (about 139.84 million people)** and **symptomatic adult ADHD, regardless of documented childhood onset, 6.76% (about 366.33 million)**; both estimates decline with advancing age. The childhood sex ratio of roughly 2:1 (boys:girls) narrows toward parity in adult clinical samples, consistent with under-recognition of girls in childhood.

## Etiology & pathophysiology

ADHD is among the most heritable psychiatric conditions: twenty twin studies converge on a **heritability of about 0.76**. Molecular genetics has caught up unevenly. Genome-wide association studies scaled from **12 independent genome-wide-significant loci** (20,183 cases / 35,191 controls) to **27 loci and 76 candidate risk genes** (38,691 cases / 186,843 controls), the associations enriched in genes expressed in early brain development and in **midbrain dopaminergic neurons**, with a converging rare protein-truncating-variant signal. The single most consequential genetic finding for this review is the pleiotropy: bivariate modelling estimates that **84–98% of the common-variant liability influencing ADHD is shared with other psychiatric disorders**. Comorbidity, in other words, is written into the genome; the co-occurrence sections below are downstream of this one number.

Neurobiology has been humbler than the genetics. The largest coordinated structural-imaging effort (1,713 cases / 1,529 controls) found **small subcortical volume reductions** in ADHD — accumbens (Cohen's *d* = −0.15), amygdala (−0.19), caudate (−0.11), hippocampus (−0.11), putamen (−0.14) and intracranial volume (−0.10) — but the effects were **largest in children, non-significant in adults, and unaffected by medication**. These are group-level averages with effect sizes far too small, and distributions far too overlapping, to identify any individual. The load-bearing clinical message about "biomarkers" is therefore a negative one: as of this review **no neuroimaging, EEG, or genetic test can diagnose ADHD**, and marketed variants that claim to should be treated with suspicion. Catecholaminergic (dopamine/norepinephrine) signalling remains the mechanistic through-line that also explains why the first-line drugs work.

## Clinical features & course

The presentation is heterogeneous by design — inattention, hyperactivity, and impulsivity in varying mixtures, layered over near-ubiquitous co-occurring conditions, neurocognitive deficits (working memory, response inhibition, delay aversion, temporal processing), and the small structural/functional brain differences noted above. Functional impairment, not symptom count, defines the disorder in practice: academic underachievement, occupational instability, relationship and driving difficulties, and injury.

The course has been substantially rewritten. The old teaching that "half outgrow it" does not survive careful longitudinal follow-up. In the landmark multimodal cohort followed to a mean age of 25, only **9.1% showed durable, sustained recovery** and only **10.8% showed stable persistence**; the large majority — **63.8% — followed a fluctuating course of remission and recurrence**, and roughly **90% carried residual symptoms into young adulthood**. ADHD is better understood as a waxing-and-waning lifespan condition than as a childhood disorder that resolves. Its most sobering feature is mortality: an all-cause mortality rate ratio of **2.07 (95% CI 1.70–2.50)**, with accidents the leading cause of death.

## Diagnosis & assessment

Diagnosis is clinical: DSM-5-TR or ICD-11 criteria applied across settings, with developmental history and collateral information (school reports, partner or parent report) doing much of the work, because self-report alone under-captures childhood onset and over-captures nonspecific adult complaints. Standardized instruments support, but do not replace, that judgment. The WHO Adult Self-Report Scale is the best-characterized screen: its refined **six-item screener achieves sensitivity 68.7%, specificity 99.5%, and 97.9% total classification accuracy (κ = 0.76)** against blinded clinical diagnosis, outperforming the full 18-item version — a reminder that a short, well-weighted screen can beat a longer one. Other scales in routine use (Conners, SNAP-IV, and the DIVA structured interview for adults) are validated but were not separately quantified for this review *[thin]*. No biological or cognitive test is diagnostic; continuous-performance tests measure attention but do not establish or exclude ADHD.

## Differential & comorbidity

Comorbidity is the rule, not the exception: **77.9% of U.S. children with current ADHD carry at least one co-occurring disorder**. The differential — developmentally normal high activity, anxiety, mood disorders, sleep disorders, trauma, learning disorders, and substance use — is largely a matter of sorting primary from secondary and of recognizing that most of these coexist rather than compete.

The emerging concept that reorganizes this section is **AuDHD** — the co-occurrence of ADHD and autism, permitted as a dual diagnosis only since DSM-5 removed the exclusion in 2013 and now understood as common at the overlap. Pooled comorbidity data place **ADHD in 28% (95% CI 25–32) of autistic people, by a wide margin the most frequent co-occurring psychiatric condition in autism**. The reciprocal traffic — autistic traits within ADHD populations — is heavy but less precisely pooled; what is firm is the shared substrate. Cross-trait genomic analyses identify on the order of **170 pleiotropic loci across ADHD, autism, and intelligence**, and the broad **84–98%** psychiatric variant-sharing noted earlier makes co-occurrence expected rather than incidental. The overlap is not total: Mendelian-randomization evidence shows that greater genetic liability to intelligence **raises autism risk (OR ≈ 1.12) but lowers ADHD risk (OR ≈ 0.78)**, so the two conditions share much of their architecture while diverging on specific axes. Clinically, AuDHD is the case where the two disorders' management collides — a tension resolved in the treatment and special-populations sections, and covered in depth from the autism side in [[Autism Spectrum Disorder — Comprehensive Review]].

## Treatment

### Pharmacological

Stimulants remain the most effective psychiatric medications for any disorder by effect size. The definitive network meta-analysis (133 double-blind RCTs) gives, for core symptoms on clinician ratings: in children and adolescents, **amphetamines SMD −1.02 (95% CI −1.19 to −0.85)**, **methylphenidate −0.78 (−0.93 to −0.62)**, and **atomoxetine −0.56 (−0.66 to −0.45)**; in adults, **amphetamines −0.79 (−0.99 to −0.58)**, **methylphenidate −0.49 (−0.64 to −0.35)**, **bupropion −0.46 (−0.85 to −0.07)**, and **atomoxetine −0.45 (−0.58 to −0.32)**, with **modafinil no better than placebo (0.16, −0.28 to 0.59)**. Weighing efficacy against tolerability (amphetamines were less well tolerated than placebo: children OR 2.30, adults OR 3.26), that analysis nominates **methylphenidate as first-line in children/adolescents and amphetamines as first-line in adults** for short-term treatment.

A 2025 component network meta-analysis of adult ADHD (113 RCTs, 14,887 participants) sharpens the picture and delivers two uncomfortable findings. Only **stimulants** (clinician-rated SMD −0.61) and **atomoxetine** (−0.51) reduced core symptoms on *both* self- and clinician-rated scales; cognitive-behavioural therapy, cognitive remediation, mindfulness, psychoeducation, and **transcranial direct-current stimulation (−0.78, −1.13 to −0.43)** beat placebo *only* on clinician-rated measures, raising the specter of assessor optimism. And **no medication improved quality of life** — symptom reduction and life improvement are not the same endpoint. A pharmacovigilance counterweight to prescribing caution: within-individual analysis of 3.87 million patients found ADHD medication associated with **lower odds of suicide attempts (OR 0.61, 95% CI 0.57–0.66)**, the reduction driven by stimulants (OR 0.72).

**Novel pharmacology** is incremental rather than transformative. Viloxazine extended-release, a non-stimulant, showed adult efficacy in a phase III trial (AISRS change **−15.5 vs placebo −11.7, p = 0.0040**). Centanafadine, a triple-reuptake inhibitor with low abuse-related adverse events, produced modest but consistent adult benefit (effect sizes **−0.24 to −0.40**). Neither displaces stimulants; both widen the non-stimulant bench.

### Non-pharmacological and device-based

For preschoolers and where families prefer to avoid medication, behavioural parent training and classroom interventions are first-line. The genuinely new modalities are digital and neuromodulatory. The **prescription digital therapeutic AKL-T01 (EndeavorRx)** — the first video-game treatment cleared by the FDA — improved objective attention in an unmedicated pediatric RCT (TOVA attention index change **+0.93 vs +0.03 control, p = 0.0060**), a real but narrow effect on a computerized measure. **Home-based transcranial direct-current stimulation** reduced adult inattention over four weeks (**β = −3.18, 95% CI −4.60 to −1.75, p < 0.001**), and **external trigeminal nerve stimulation (the Monarch eTNS system)** is already FDA-cleared for pediatric ADHD. These are adjuncts and options, not replacements for pharmacotherapy, but they mark the first expansion of the treatment class in decades.

## Special populations

**Preschoolers (ages 3–5):** methylphenidate is effective but with **smaller effect sizes (0.4–0.8) than in school-age children**, a mean optimal dose near 14 mg/day, and only **21% meeting remission on best-dose medication** — which is why behavioural parent training is recommended first below age 6. **Adults** carry the highest mortality risk at diagnosis (mortality rate ratio **4.25, 95% CI 3.05–5.78** for those first diagnosed at ≥18 years), reflecting accumulated untreated risk. In **pregnancy**, first-trimester **methylphenidate** exposure carried a small increased cardiac-malformation signal (relative risk **1.28, 95% CI 1.00–1.64**), whereas **amphetamines were not associated** with malformations — a distinction that should inform agent choice when treatment is continued.

## Prognosis

Prognosis follows directly from the rewritten course. Sustained recovery is the minority outcome (**9.1%**); a fluctuating course (**63.8%**) is the norm; persistence predictors include symptom **severity (OR ≈ 2.33)**, psychiatric comorbidity, and treatment history. The mortality signal (rate ratio **2.07, 95% CI 1.70–2.50**) is driven chiefly by unnatural causes, especially accidents; it persists at **1.50 (1.11–1.98)** after excluding oppositional/conduct and substance-use disorders, and is **higher in females (2.85) than males (1.27)**. Adverse substance-use and self-harm trajectories are partly mitigated by pharmacological treatment. The prognostic frame for clinicians is chronic-disease management, not cure.

## Controversies & future directions

**Overdiagnosis at the mild end.** A scoping review of 334 studies concluded there is both a reservoir of undiagnosed ADHD and, simultaneously, expanding diagnosis and treatment concentrated at the **milder end of the severity spectrum**, where only a handful of studies have weighed benefit against harm — supporting a "diminishing returns" hypothesis in which, for the mildest presentations, **harms may outweigh benefits**. This is the reconciliation of the 3.4%-versus-11.4% gap: not a true epidemic, but a widening diagnostic net whose marginal cases are the least studied.

**Dimensional versus categorical nosology.** The genetic evidence that ADHD is the extreme of a continuous trait feeds directly into transdiagnostic frameworks; the Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology (HiTOP) explicitly locates neurodevelopmental dimensions within a hierarchical structure *(the retrieved HiTOP work is neurodevelopment-general rather than ADHD-specific)*, and an RDoC (Research Domain Criteria) reframing is frequently invoked but was **not separately sourced here** *[unverified]*.

**Stimulant cardiovascular safety** is, on current evidence, reassuring at the population level. A large adult cohort found serious cardiovascular events *no more common* with stimulant use (relative risk **0.83, 95% CI 0.72–0.96**), and an updated meta-analysis (19 studies, 3.9 million participants) found **no statistically significant association with any cardiovascular disease** (children RR 1.18, 0.91–1.53; adults 1.04, 0.43–2.48), while cautioning that a modest risk — particularly for tachyarrhythmia (RR 1.60, 0.94–2.72) — could not be fully excluded.

**The pipeline** is dominated by non-stimulant pharmacology, devices, and — notably — trials built around the AuDHD overlap. Centanafadine is in phase 2/3 across adults and children (readouts around 2027); viloxazine has long-term pediatric follow-up ongoing. Device development spans transcranial direct-current stimulation (a phase 3 pediatric trial reading out imminently), transcranial random-noise stimulation, external trigeminal nerve stimulation, repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation, and photobiomodulation, alongside home digital-therapy and personalized-neurofeedback trials. Two registered trials treat the ADHD–autism overlap as the population of interest — external trigeminal nerve stimulation for autistic children with ADHD, and a digital executive-function therapeutic enrolling children with ADHD *or* autism — a concrete sign that AuDHD is moving from a nosological observation to a treatment target.
