## Definition & scope

"CBT for panic disorder" names not a single technique but a small family of manualised, time-limited (typically 8–15 session) packages built on a shared logic: panic is a learned, self-perpetuating fear *of the body's own alarm responses*, and it is unlearned by correcting the catastrophic beliefs that sustain it and by deliberately re-exposing the patient to the sensations they dread. The two canonical protocols are Barlow's **Panic Control Treatment** (psychoeducation + cognitive restructuring + interoceptive exposure ± breathing retraining) and Clark's **cognitive therapy** (identifying and testing catastrophic misinterpretations through behavioural experiments). Both are first-line psychotherapy in every major guideline, positioned alongside SSRIs. Panic disorder itself is common (1–4% prevalence) and, untreated, chronic and relapsing — which is why the *durability* of any treatment, not just its acute effect, is the question that matters, and where this review places its weight.

## Theoretical model — the cognitive-behavioural account of panic

CBT for panic works only as well as the model it operationalises, so the model is the right starting point. The **cognitive theory** (Clark) holds that panic patients carry a relatively enduring tendency to **catastrophically misinterpret benign bodily sensations** — a racing heart read as an impending heart attack, dizziness as impending collapse or insanity — creating a vicious circle in which the fear response amplifies the very sensations being feared. A 2024 systematic narrative review of this theory (53 studies) found *substantial* evidence consistent with its central predictions, and a meta-analysis comparing groups found **medium-to-large effects** distinguishing panic patients from healthy controls *and* from patients with other anxiety disorders on the strength of catastrophic misinterpretation of bodily sensations — i.e. the misinterpretation is relatively specific to panic, not generic anxiety.

The **behavioural** layer adds interoceptive (fear-of-fear) conditioning: internal sensations become conditioned stimuli for anxiety, maintained by avoidance and safety behaviours. A **respiratory/biological** strand runs underneath — panic patients show heightened CO₂ sensitivity and a "respiratory subtype," and the fear network (amygdala, medial prefrontal cortex, hippocampus and brainstem projections) appears abnormally sensitive to interoceptive threat. Formal causal-modelling work concludes the cognitive and biological accounts are **compatible rather than rival**: the biology supplies an unusually reactive alarm, cognition supplies the catastrophic reading that turns a false alarm into a disorder. This matters clinically because it licenses CBT's core move — you do not need to abolish the physiology to break the disorder; you break the catastrophic appraisal and the conditioned avoidance.

## Components & protocol — what is actually doing the work

Because CBT-for-panic is a package, the load-bearing question is *which ingredients earn their place*. The most rigorous answer is a **component network meta-analysis** (72 RCTs, 4,064 participants) that disentangled the individual components. Its findings are unusually decisive and partly counter-intuitive: **interoceptive exposure** and a **face-to-face setting** were associated with *better* efficacy **and** acceptability; **muscle relaxation** and **virtual-reality exposure** were associated with *significantly lower* efficacy; and **breathing retraining** and **in vivo exposure** improved acceptability while adding only small effects on efficacy. The gap between the best- and worst-assembled "evidence-based CBT" packages was large — an odds ratio for remission of **7.69 (95% CrI 1.75–33.33)** — meaning that *how* the package is built matters as much as *whether* the patient gets "CBT." The implied optimal package is **face-to-face + interoceptive exposure, with muscle relaxation and VR left out.**

This partially collides with an older treatment-technique meta-analysis (42 studies, 65 comparisons) that found the *combination* of exposure, relaxation training and breathing retraining gave the most consistent benefit, with homework and a follow-up program adding value and comorbidity subtracting it. The disagreement centres on relaxation and breathing retraining, and it is a genuine controversy (below) rather than a settled point. What both agree on, and what the broader anxiety literature confirms, is that **exposure is the engine**: head-to-head, exposure-based strategies outperform purely cognitive techniques on effect size, though direct trials of exposure versus cognitive therapy in panic specifically have found **no significant difference** — consistent with the two being different routes to the same disconfirmation of catastrophic belief.

## Efficacy — how well it works

CBT for panic is effective, with the honest caveat that the effect size depends heavily on the comparator. The most current benchmark — a 2025 unified series of CBT meta-analyses using uniform methods (375 trials, 32,968 patients across disorders) — places panic disorder in the **Hedges g 0.5–1.0** band versus all control conditions, the same mid-range as social and generalised anxiety and depression. Crucially, that number inflates against a **waitlist** control (g > 0.94, the design most common in anxiety trials) and shrinks against **care-as-usual** (0.22–1.13) — so a headline "large effect" is partly an artefact of weak comparators. Earlier meta-analytic reviews likewise rated CBT's panic effect as large, and against pill placebo the response advantage is real (odds ratio for response ≈ 2.97 across anxiety disorders). CBT is also comparatively **acceptable**: dropout in the 2025 series ran 8–24% across disorders (panic toward the lower end), and in head-to-head data patients are **1.76 times more likely to refuse pharmacotherapy than psychotherapy** (overall refusal 8.2%). The fair summary: a robust, mid-to-large acute effect, well tolerated, with the magnitude honest only when the control condition is named.

## Delivery formats — the same therapy, many vehicles

CBT-for-panic survives translation across delivery formats better than almost any psychiatric treatment, which is the basis for scaling it. A network meta-analysis of delivery formats (74 trials, 6,699 patients) found **face-to-face group (SMD −0.47), face-to-face individual (−0.43), and guided self-help (−0.42)** all superior to treatment-as-usual and statistically indistinguishable from each other — while **unguided self-help was not** superior (−0.21, non-significant). The active ingredient in the "digital" story is therefore the **guidance**, not the medium. Remote CBT (internet/telehealth) delivers large within-group improvement (Hedges g ≈ 1.18 post-treatment, 1.51 at follow-up across 21 studies, n=1,604). Virtual-reality exposure produces a large effect versus waitlist (g ≈ 0.90) and is statistically **equal to in-vivo exposure** (g ≈ −0.07) — a finding that sits in unresolved tension with the component NMA's demotion of VR (see Controversies). The practical reading: guided internet-CBT and group CBT are legitimate, evidence-backed ways to widen access without sacrificing efficacy; unguided apps are not.

## Mechanisms of change — what mediates improvement

Consistent with the model, the changes that *mediate* CBT response are reductions in the catastrophic appraisal system rather than in physiology per se. **Anxiety sensitivity** — the trait fear of anxiety-related sensations — is elevated in proportion to panic severity, falls significantly after cognitive-behavioural group therapy, and predicts response, positioning it as both a mechanism and a target. Interoceptive exposure, the component the dismantling analysis singled out, is understood to work by driving **inhibitory (extinction) learning**: repeated, deliberate provocation of feared sensations (hyperventilation, spinning, breath-holding) without the catastrophic outcome disconfirms the threat prediction. Cognitive restructuring works on the same disconfirmation from the belief side. The convergent picture is that CBT does not calm the alarm; it **teaches the patient that the alarm is safe**, and the measurable signature of that learning is a drop in anxiety sensitivity and catastrophic misinterpretation. This is why treatments that merely dampen arousal (relaxation) underperform in the component analysis — they can function as safety behaviours that *prevent* the disconfirming experience.

## Predictors & moderators of response — who does not get better, and why

Despite CBT's efficacy, a substantial minority do not improve, and the review question the user foregrounded is *who*. A systematic review of predictors and moderators (52 studies) found the single most consistent predictor of **reduced** improvement is **agoraphobic avoidance** — the more the patient's world has already narrowed, the worse the CBT outcome — followed by **low expectancy for change, high functional impairment, and Cluster C (anxious/avoidant, dependent, obsessive-compulsive) personality pathology**. Beyond these, the striking finding is a *negative* one: few other variables predict outcome consistently; many are underpowered or contradictory, and formal moderation tests are rare. For **dropout** specifically (a 200-patient trial comparing CBT, psychodynamic therapy and applied relaxation), **unemployment and higher psychosocial disability** raised the dropout hazard, while — counter-intuitively — **higher anxiety sensitivity predicted treatment completion**, perhaps because sensation-fearful patients are more motivated by a sensation-focused rationale. Moderator work comparing CBT with acceptance and commitment therapy suggests CBT does relatively better in **older patients, men, and shorter illness duration**, and ACT in younger patients, women, and longer duration — hypotheses, not allocation rules, given the small sample. Biologically, the once-hyped **5-HTTLPR serotonin-transporter polymorphism does not moderate** CBT outcome (meta-analysis, 2,195 patients) — a clean negative that closes a line of "therapygenetic" personalisation. The usable clinical message: **agoraphobic avoidance and comorbid Cluster C pathology flag the patient who will need more than a standard course**, and there is no validated biomarker or genotype to triage by.

## Durability & relapse — the honest weak point

CBT's reputation rests on the claim that it outlasts medication because the patient keeps the skills, and for panic that claim is **only partly supported by the strongest data**. A meta-analysis of long-term outcomes (69 RCTs, 4,118 outpatients) found CBT's advantage over controls for panic disorder held at **Hedges g 0.22–0.35 through 12 months** post-treatment — but at **≥12 months the effect was no longer statistically significant for panic disorder** (k=5 studies), even while it remained significant for generalised and social anxiety and large for PTSD. In other words, panic has the *weakest long-term evidence base* of the anxiety disorders, not because CBT clearly fails but because the durability trials past a year barely exist. Reported **relapse rates at 3–12 months were 0–14%**, but were available in only six trials (predominantly panic). This is a real limitation, not a technicality: the durability advantage everyone attributes to panic-focused CBT is asserted more confidently than the ≥12-month data license, and high-quality long-follow-up trials with reported relapse are explicitly what the field still needs. Two practical correlates: building a **relapse-prevention/booster** component in is rational given the chronicity, and combining CBT with a benzodiazepine may actively *harm* durability (below).

## Limitations, non-response & augmentation

The boundaries of CBT-for-panic are well mapped. Roughly a third of patients drop out or fail to remit; agoraphobic avoidance, Cluster C pathology and high impairment mark them in advance. Attempts to *pharmacologically augment* the therapy have largely failed: **D-cycloserine**, meant to accelerate extinction learning during exposure, gave only a small, non-durable benefit in an individual-participant meta-analysis (d ≈ −0.25, gone by follow-up) and **no significant difference** from placebo in a Cochrane review (responder RR 1.10, 0.89–1.34); **cannabidiol** augmentation of exposure was outright negative in refractory panic. Combining CBT with medication is nuanced: adding an **SSRI** tends to help acutely with the advantage attenuating after the drug stops, whereas adding a **benzodiazepine** is, if anything, counter-productive — a Cochrane review found the evidence inadequate and the combination possibly **inferior to behaviour therapy alone at follow-up** (RR 0.62, 0.36–1.07, P=.08), consistent with the theoretical worry that state-dependent, sensation-blunting drug effects undercut the very interoceptive learning CBT depends on. The non-response pathway, then, is *more or better-targeted CBT* (interoceptive-exposure-heavy, agoraphobia-focused) rather than a bolt-on molecule.

## Controversies & future directions

Three live debates and a forward look. First, **breathing retraining**: physiologically motivated by the CO₂/respiratory subtype, yet the component NMA found it adds little to efficacy and it is increasingly reconceptualised as a possible *safety behaviour* that can blunt the disconfirming experience — hence its demotion in modern protocols, unresolved against the older meta-analysis that favoured it. Second, **virtual-reality exposure**: rated *equal to in-vivo* as a standalone exposure vehicle, yet *inferior* as a within-package component — likely because VR substitutes for, rather than adds to, real interoceptive/in-vivo exposure. Third, **exposure versus cognitive primacy**: they are statistically equivalent in panic, leaving open whether cognitive restructuring is a genuinely separate mechanism or simply another delivery route for disconfirmation. The registry points the field's near-term work at **precision and scaling** rather than new techniques: trials testing **CO₂-reactivity as a biomarker of exposure non-response** (NCT05467683), **brief-intensive versus weekly** CBT (NCT05942391), **transdiagnostic (Unified Protocol) versus diagnosis-specific** CBT (NCT05249543), **VR versus in-vivo** exposure in agoraphobia (NCT06514495), and **stepped versus stratified** care (NCT06942429). The trajectory is toward matching the right dose and format to the right patient — exactly the personalisation the predictor literature says is missing.
