# Adjustment Disorder — Comprehensive Review
*2026-07-11 · PubMed 30 · trials 3 · sections 10 · clinician self-study, treatment-weighted*

> Emphasis locked to treatment and management (the thin evidence base, watchful waiting vs active treatment, and the suicide question foregrounded); every arc section is still covered, because breadth is the contract. Assumed: full-length comprehensive review, clinician self-study register. Skipped: nothing.

According to PubMed and ClinicalTrials.gov. Adjustment disorder (AjD) is the diagnosis every clinician uses and almost no one researches. It sits at the crossroads of two uncomfortable facts: it is among the most frequently assigned diagnoses in consultation-liaison psychiatry, primary care, oncology, and the military, yet its evidence base — for what causes it, how to measure it, and above all how to treat it — is thinner than that of almost any disorder of comparable prevalence. This review walks the full arc but leans, as requested, into management: what the (small) trial literature actually supports, when to watch and when to act, and why the "mild and self-limiting" label is dangerous when suicide risk is on the table.

## Definition and nosology

AjD entered formal classification with DSM-III in 1980 and has survived every edition since, but its construct has just undergone the most significant revision of its 45-year history. DSM-5-TR keeps AjD as a **residual, subthreshold category**: emotional or behavioural symptoms arising within **3 months** of an identifiable stressor, out of proportion to that stressor or causing significant impairment, and — critically — resolving within **6 months** once the stressor and its consequences have ended. It is defined largely by what it is not (not major depression, not an anxiety disorder, not normal bereavement), and it is explicitly excluded when the presentation meets criteria for another disorder. This "diagnosis of exclusion" architecture is the source of most of its problems.

ICD-11 (in force since 2022) breaks from this by giving AjD, for the first time, **positive symptom content**. It is now a stress-response syndrome defined by two core features: **preoccupation** with the stressor or its consequences (excessive worry, recurrent distressing thoughts, rumination) and **failure to adapt**, with symptoms emerging within **1 month** of the stressor and typically resolving within **6 months** unless the stressor persists. This shift — from a residual placeholder to a specified syndrome with a measurable phenotype — is the single most consequential development in the field and the reason a wave of new research became possible after 2018. The two systems now diverge meaningfully on onset window and, more deeply, on whether AjD is a "leftover" category or a disorder in its own right.

## Epidemiology

Prevalence estimates for AjD are almost uninterpretable without naming the setting, because the diagnosis lives in medical and help-seeking populations rather than the community. In **primary care** the point prevalence runs roughly **11–18%**, and in **consultation-liaison psychiatry 10–35%** — figures that make AjD one of the most common conditions either setting sees, even as general-practitioner recognition remains poor and antidepressant prescribing for it is often reflexive and unmatched to evidence. Setting-specific reviews put the range as wide as **0.2% to 40%** depending on the stressor studied. In cancer, the most rigorous data — a meta-analysis of 94 interview-based studies — pooled **AjD at 15.4%** in palliative-care settings, with **depression-or-AjD combined at 24.7%**, making AjD, not major depression, the single most common formal psychiatric diagnosis in people with cancer. Against these clinical figures, the true **general-population** rate is far lower: ICD-11–operationalised community surveys estimate on the order of **~2%**, consistent with a disorder that is, by definition, tethered to an identifiable recent stressor. Male sex, prior psychiatric history, and low social support recur as correlates across settings.

## Etiology and pathophysiology

AjD is the rare psychiatric diagnosis with a **necessary external cause built into its definition**: by criterion, **100%** of cases follow an identifiable psychosocial stressor, whether a discrete event (job loss, divorce, a serious diagnosis) or a continuing difficulty. The stressor is necessary but not sufficient — most people exposed to the same stressor do not develop AjD — so the explanatory weight falls on the **individual's stress-response system and its regulation**. The reconceptualisation of AjD as a trauma- and stressor-related condition placed it squarely within the psychobiology of stress-response syndromes: hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis reactivity, autonomic arousal, and the cognitive appraisal machinery that determines whether a stressor is experienced as manageable or overwhelming. Resilience — the capacity to maintain function under stress — is the protective mirror of this vulnerability, and the individual variation in it is what a single shared stressor exposes. No AjD-specific biomarker, imaging signature, or genetic finding exists; the pathophysiology is currently understood as **normal stress biology pushed past adaptive limits**, not a distinct lesion, which is both scientifically honest and part of why the diagnosis's validity is contested.

## Clinical features and course

Phenomenologically AjD is protean: depressed mood, anxiety, worry, and disturbance of conduct appear in varying mixtures, which is why DSM historically split it into subtypes (with depressed mood, with anxiety, mixed, with disturbance of conduct, mixed emotional and conduct). What unifies presentations under ICD-11 is the **preoccupation-plus-failure-to-adapt** dyad rather than any specific affective content. Onset is tightly time-locked to the stressor — within **1 month** (ICD-11) to **3 months** (DSM-5-TR) — which is the feature that most cleanly separates it from disorders with insidious or endogenous onset. Course is, in the majority, **self-limiting**: symptoms are expected to remit within **6 months** of the stressor and its sequelae resolving. The important clinical caveat is that when the stressor is **chronic or recurrent** (an ongoing illness, a persistent workplace conflict, a protracted legal or financial crisis), AjD can run a prolonged course, and a meaningful minority do not follow the tidy short-and-mild script the textbooks promise.

## Diagnosis and assessment

For most of its history AjD had **no dedicated, validated instrument** — a direct consequence of its residual status — and was captured only as a by-product of broad diagnostic interviews or by exclusion. ICD-11's positive criteria fixed this. Two self-report measures now anchor assessment. The **International Adjustment Disorder Questionnaire (IADQ)** operationalises the ICD-11 narrative directly (preoccupation + failure to adapt, tied to a nominated stressor) and has been developed and validated in general-population and cross-cultural samples, with a community symptom-endorsement rate around **2%** in its Irish validation and replication across Israeli, Swiss, and Italian cohorts. The **Adjustment Disorder New Module (ADNM)**, most used in its **20-item (ADNM-20)** form (with a 4-item screener), assesses core and additional stress-response symptoms and has demonstrated a replicable factor structure and sound psychometrics across multiple large samples. A practical assessment therefore now runs: confirm an identifiable stressor and its timing, apply IADQ or ADNM-20 to quantify preoccupation/failure-to-adapt, and — indispensably — screen for suicidality and rule out a full-threshold mood, anxiety, or trauma disorder that would take diagnostic precedence.

## Differential diagnosis and comorbidity

The differential is where AjD is won or lost, because every neighbour is a disorder AjD is defined *not* to be. Against **major depressive disorder**, the discriminators are symptom count and autonomy from the stressor: MDD meets a full symptom threshold and can persist independently of the trigger, whereas AjD is subthreshold and stressor-bound (in palliative populations, DSM-defined major depression runs ~14.3% alongside the 15.4% with AjD, and the two must be actively separated rather than conflated). Against **acute stress disorder and PTSD**, the divider is the nature of the stressor and the symptom profile: PTSD requires a threat-to-life traumatic event and its intrusion/avoidance/hyperarousal cluster, while AjD follows ordinary (if serious) life stressors and lacks the trauma-specific syndrome — a distinction sharpened in medically-ill populations such as cardiac patients, where ASD, AjD, and PTSD frequently need to be told apart. Against **prolonged grief disorder** (and complicated grief, which affects roughly **7%** of bereaved older adults), the boundary is the specific bereavement syndrome versus a more general maladaptive reaction. And against **normal, non-pathological stress reactions**, the only real criterion is disproportion and impairment — the softest boundary in the manual, and the one most open to over-diagnosis. Comorbidity is common; the diagnostic exclusion rule (assign the other disorder if criteria are met) means AjD is frequently under-recorded when it co-travels.

## Treatment

The organising truth of AjD management is that **the evidence base is small, heterogeneous, and dominated by the anxiety subtype** — no medication is licensed specifically for AjD, and the best-supported psychological approaches are supported weakly. Two corollaries follow: first, because the condition is usually self-limiting, **watchful waiting** — psychoeducation, validation, and active mobilisation of social support, with a planned review — is a legitimate and often correct first step for mild presentations; second, because a non-trivial minority are neither mild nor brief and because suicide risk is real, escalation criteria (persistence beyond expectation, worsening severity, functional collapse, any suicidality) must be set at the outset. Treatment intensity should be matched to severity in an explicit **stepped-care** frame.

### Psychotherapeutic

Psychotherapy is first-line, but the honest summary is that the specific evidence is **evolving rather than settled**: systematic reviews of psychological and pharmacological treatments for AjD in adults find benefit for psychological interventions but few high-quality, AjD-specific randomised trials and considerable heterogeneity in what "psychotherapy for AjD" even means. The interventions with the most rationale are **problem-solving therapy, cognitive-behavioural therapy, stress-management and relaxation training, behavioural activation, and brief supportive or psychodynamic work** — all aimed at the appraisal-and-coping machinery the disorder implicates. Across reviews the practical message is consistent: structured, brief, stressor-focused psychotherapy is the backbone of active treatment, and the field's task is to run the confirmatory trials that its widespread use has outpaced.

### Pharmacological

Medication in AjD is **adjunctive and symptomatic, not curative**, and the controlled evidence clusters almost entirely on **AjD with anxiety**. The most studied agent is **etifoxine**, a non-benzodiazepine anxiolytic (GABA-A modulation plus neurosteroid effects): in a double-blind primary-care trial of **191** outpatients it was **non-inferior to lorazepam** for AjD-with-anxiety, and it has been compared against alprazolam, with the appeal that it lacks the dependence and rebound liability of benzodiazepines (though it is not available in all markets, including the US). **Benzodiazepines** themselves provide short-term relief of anxiety and insomnia but carry dependence risk and serve mainly as the comparator that etifoxine is measured against — they are a bridge, not a treatment. Several **herbal preparations** have positive placebo-controlled data in the anxiety subtype: a six-extract combination (Euphytose) beat placebo in **182** patients with AjD-with-anxious-mood, and a kava extract (WS 1490) outperformed placebo across an anxiety sample including AjD over **25** weeks — the latter now heavily constrained by kava hepatotoxicity concerns. Lavender-oil preparations are discussed favourably in the pharmacotherapy review literature for stress-related and subsyndromal anxiety. **SSRIs and other antidepressants** have **no AjD-specific RCT support**; their use is extrapolated from adjacent disorders and reserved for persistent or clearly comorbid depressive/anxiety states. The overall pharmacotherapy verdict from dedicated review is that drug evidence in AjD is sparse and that pharmacology should support, not replace, psychological management.

### Digital and low-intensity

The best-fitting innovation for a high-prevalence, low-resource condition is **low-intensity, technology-delivered** treatment, and here the evidence is unusually encouraging for AjD. An internet-based **self-help** intervention built on the ICD-11 model (BADI) reduced AjD symptoms versus a waiting-list control in a randomised trial of **284** participants, and a systematic review with preliminary meta-analysis of **9** studies (**8 RCTs**, including virtual-reality formats) found technology-supported treatments **superior to control** and associated with significant pre-to-post improvement. These interventions map naturally onto the bottom rungs of a stepped-care model and offer a plausible answer to the mismatch between how common AjD is and how little specialist time exists to treat it.

### Neuromodulation and somatic

There is **essentially no controlled evidence** for rTMS, ECT, or other neuromodulation/somatic treatments in AjD, and none is indicated. The absence is itself a finding: it reflects both the disorder's expected self-limiting course and its low research priority, and it correctly steers clinicians away from high-intensity somatic interventions for a condition whose first-line management is psychological and whose drug evidence is already marginal.

### The suicide question

The most important management point overrides the "mild" reputation: **AjD carries a clinically significant risk of suicide**, and this is explicitly flagged in the therapeutic-intervention review literature as the reason treatment is not optional. AjD is disproportionately represented among people presenting to emergency services after self-harm and among those in acute suicidal crisis, and the interval from ideation to act can be short, driven by the acute stressor rather than a long depressive prodrome. A precise, large, AjD-specific national-register estimate of suicide mortality was not located in this search and remains a genuine evidence gap `[unverified]`. The clinical implication is unambiguous regardless: **every AjD assessment must include a suicide-risk evaluation**, and the diagnosis's benign course statistics must never be used to downgrade an individual's risk.

## Special populations

AjD's clinical centre of gravity is the **medically ill**, and the strongest setting-specific data come from there. In **cancer**, AjD is the most common formal psychiatric diagnosis (15.4% pooled in palliative care), and prevalence-and-acceptance studies show that a substantial share of affected patients will engage psychological treatment when it is offered — an actionable finding for oncology services. Prospective work using ICD-11 criteria in **prostate-cancer** diagnostic pathways tracked AjD symptom trajectories across a **12-month** follow-up, and studies in **organ-transplant** patients and their relatives have documented ICD-11 AjD in both patients and caregivers, underscoring that the "population" at risk includes families. In **children and adolescents**, AjD is a common reaction to illness, family disruption, and school stress, though the dedicated treatment evidence is even thinner than in adults. **Older adults** shade into the bereavement/complicated-grief differential. Across all of these, the through-line is that AjD is most often encountered by non-psychiatrists managing a medical stressor — which is exactly why simple, deployable screening (IADQ/ADNM-20) and stepped-care pathways matter more here than specialist psychotherapy access.

## Prognosis

The prognostic literature was, until recently, almost as neglected as everything else about AjD. A systematic review of outcomes drawing on **31** cohort and randomised studies (over **1,380** patients) provides the current best characterisation: the modal course is **favourable and time-limited**, consistent with the diagnosis's built-in expectation of remission within 6 months of stressor resolution, but a meaningful subset shows **persistence, progression to a full-threshold disorder (most often depression), or recurrence** — particularly where the stressor is chronic. Prognosis is therefore genuinely bimodal in practice: reassuringly self-limiting for most, but not benign enough to justify blanket watchful waiting without follow-up, especially given the suicide signal. The predictors of a worse course — chronic/ongoing stressors, comorbidity, poor social support — are also the levers management can pull.

## Controversies and future directions

Four disputes remain live. First, **validity**: because DSM-5-TR still frames AjD as a residual "diagnosis of exclusion," critics have long called it a wastebasket — clinically indispensable but scientifically underspecified — and ICD-11's positive criteria are precisely the attempt to answer that charge; whether they succeed is the field's central open question. Second, the **DSM-5-TR vs ICD-11 divergence** is now a real-world problem, not an academic one: different onset windows (3 months vs 1 month) and, more fundamentally, a residual category versus a specified stress-response syndrome mean the two systems can identify different patients, complicating research synthesis and cross-system care. Third, the **boundary with normal stress reactions** — disproportion and impairment being the only criteria — keeps the risk of medicalising ordinary distress permanently open. Fourth, the **treatment vacuum**: for a condition this prevalent, the near-total absence of large, AjD-specific, modality-comparative trials is a striking gap.

The pipeline is finally moving on the last point. A **Phase 2b trial of psilocybin-assisted psychotherapy** for cancer-related AjD is recruiting (**87** participants, ICD-11 AjD by ADNM-20, primary endpoint HAM-A anxiety change at Week 10, readout expected late 2026) — the first genuinely novel pharmacology tested in the disorder. A large **work-focused CBT** effectiveness trial targets AjD-with-work-inhibition and stress-related sick leave (**204** participants, readout ~2030), addressing the occupational stressors that drive much of the primary-care caseload. And a **US-military** study is validating a military-specific ADNM-20 while mapping AjD disease trajectories (**60** participants, 2028). Beyond these, the field's clearest needs are confirmatory RCTs for the psychotherapies already in wide use, head-to-head comparisons of watchful waiting versus active treatment, scaled deployment of the digital interventions that already show benefit, and — overdue — a dedicated register study quantifying suicide risk so the diagnosis's most dangerous feature is no longer left `[unverified]`.
