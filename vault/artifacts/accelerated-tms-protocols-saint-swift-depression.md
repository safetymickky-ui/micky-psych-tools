# Accelerated TMS protocols for depression (SAINT/SNT, SWIFT, accelerated iTBS) — what to teach and how much to believe
*2026-07-17 · PubMed 12 · trials 7 · books 0*

> Assumed: indication is **unipolar treatment-resistant depression** (the default you were shown) — bipolar, adolescent, and non-TRD trials appear only as boundary evidence, never as the headline.
> Skipped (below the line for a teaching/reference map): the **build-a-service** economics (throughput, staff-time, cost-per-responder — a different decision, route it back if you want it); the efficacy of **conventional** once-daily rTMS/iTBS beyond a comparator baseline; **non-depression** indications (OCD, addiction) carried by these coils.

Two named protocols sit behind this question, and they are **not the same kind of thing** — conflating them is the first teaching error to avoid. **SAINT/SNT** is Stanford/Magnus's fMRI-guided, figure-8-coil, ultra-high-dose accelerated iTBS. **SWIFT** is BrainsWay's accelerated *deep*-TMS (H1-coil) protocol with no neuroimaging. "Accelerated iTBS" is the generic category under both. They earn different verdicts because they rest on different evidence designs.

## The verdict — what to teach, and the one number for each

Teach the category as **real but oversold at the headline**. Delivering TMS as multiple sessions per day compresses a 6-week course into **1–3 weeks with equal or slightly better antidepressant effect** — that much is now established by sham- and active-controlled RCTs. What is *not* established is that acceleration or high dose produces a *larger* effect than conventional TMS, and the famous "90% remission" figure is an open-label artefact that does not survive blinding. Overall confidence **moderate**; it is not higher because every sham-controlled trial is small and single-site, and durability is barely characterised.

The three sentences to say out loud, each with its load-bearing number and its expiry:

- **SAINT/SNT** — *"An fMRI-guided, 90,000-pulse accelerated iTBS course put ~90% of treatment-resistant patients into remission open-label, but in the only sham-controlled trial (n=29) that shrank to a 52.5% vs 11.1% MADRS reduction — real, FDA-cleared in 2022, but proven only in one small single-site study."* Hold as **established that it beats sham; recognise the 90% as open-label, not the expected clinical result.**
- **SWIFT** — *"An accelerated deep-TMS (H1-coil) protocol, 30 sessions over 6 half-days, FDA-cleared in 2025 — but its pivotal trial (n=104) was non-inferiority against standard deep TMS, not against sham, so it shows 'as good as, and faster than' the existing treatment, not 'better than placebo' on its own."* Hold as **a convenience/speed advance over deep TMS; recognise it has no independent sham-controlled evidence.**
- **Generic accelerated iTBS** — *"You do not need the fMRI or the 90k pulses: a cheap, non-navigated protocol of 3 sessions/day for 3 weeks still beat sham (54.7% vs 31.9% HDRS-17 reduction, d≈0.65)."* Hold as **the pragmatic, portable version — a moderate effect, not a miracle.**

If a trainee remembers one thing: **acceleration mostly buys speed, not a bigger effect.** The place it should *not* go is a patient consult promising "90% remission."

## Does the acceleration itself add efficacy, or only speed?

Only speed — and teaching it as a speed advance rather than a potency advance is the whole point. Three independent lines converge. SWIFT's pivotal multisite trial randomised 104 adults with moderate-to-severe MDD to accelerated deep iTBS versus standard once-daily deep TMS and found them statistically **non-inferior** at week 6 (HDRS-21 change −19.02 vs −19.79; between-group difference 0.76, upper 97.5% CI 2.73, inside the 3.0-point margin), with the accelerated arm reaching remission faster (21 vs 28 days). The pragmatic São Paulo RCT (n=100) compared active versus sham accelerated iTBS and got a **medium-to-large** effect (Cohen d 0.65, 95% CI 0.29–1.00) — solidly positive, but nowhere near the open-label headlines. And the network meta-analyses that place iTBS above sham (iTBS odds ratio 3.20, 95% CI 1.45–7.08 in the 113-trial BMJ analysis) still find *accelerated* protocols understudied rather than dominant. The honest teaching line is that compressing the schedule preserves the effect and shortens the wait — which is clinically valuable for an acutely suicidal or hospitalised patient — without evidence that packing sessions closer together makes TMS work *better*.

## How much of SAINT's 90% survives blinding?

Roughly half of it, and the collapse is the single most instructive number in this topic. The open-label SAINT study (n=21 completers) reported **90.5% remission** — 86.4% intention-to-treat — on MADRS, with no cognitive side effects, and that figure drove the media story and the commercial launch. When the same group ran the double-blind sham-controlled SNT trial, the active group's MADRS fell **52.5%** against **11.1%** for sham (n=29) — still a clear, statistically significant separation that earned FDA clearance, but less than two-thirds of the open-label remission rate, with response and remission favouring active in a sample of fewer than thirty patients at a single site. The meta-analytic view makes the deflation explicit: pooling the *randomised* accelerated-TMS trials gives a cumulative effect size of just **0.39 (95% CI 0.005–0.779)** — barely clearing zero — while pooling open-label and active-arm data from the same literature gives **g = 1.27**. That fourfold gap between the uncontrolled and controlled estimates *is* the placebo-plus-expectation contribution, and it is why the "90%" must be taught as a ceiling produced by an unblinded, self-selected, intensively-monitored sample — not as the remission rate to quote a patient. A five-RCT meta-analysis (n=239) confirms the direction — active accelerated iTBS beats sham on response — but labels its own conclusion "preliminary," which is the correct register.

## What does the fMRI targeting and the high pulse dose actually buy?

This is where the evidence is thinnest and where the teaching should be most cautious: **the incremental value of individualised fMRI targeting and the 90,000-pulse dose over a cheap, standard-located accelerated protocol is essentially unproven head-to-head.** SAINT/SNT bundles four changes onto conventional iTBS at once — many sessions per day, five days, a very high total pulse dose, *and* resting-state-connectivity-guided targeting of the left DLPFC region most anticorrelated with subgenual ACC — so its dramatic open-label numbers cannot be attributed to the targeting specifically. Meanwhile the pragmatic São Paulo protocol deliberately stripped the expensive parts away (3 sessions/day, 1200 pulses each, **craniometric** scalp location, no neuronavigation) and still separated from sham at d≈0.65, and SWIFT's deep-TMS protocol reaches standard-of-care outcomes with **no imaging at all**. Real-world naturalistic data point the same way — a 391-patient accelerated deep-rTMS case series reported ~60.9% response at day 6 without connectivity targeting — though uncontrolled series carry no weight against the placebo problem above. The defensible position: precision targeting and mega-dosing are **plausible and mechanistically motivated, but not yet shown to add clinical benefit over a simpler accelerated course**, and the trials that would isolate the targeting effect (individualised vs standard vs sham) are only now reading out.

## What is genuinely unsettled — durability and blinding integrity

Two gaps should be named rather than glossed. **Durability is barely characterised in the peer-reviewed record.** The SNT trial's primary endpoint was only 4 weeks post-treatment and its own authors called for durability studies; the adolescent accelerated-iTBS RCT (n=74) is the cautionary case — a solid early effect (d=0.86 at day 11, 0.72 at one month) that had **decayed to non-significance by three months** (d=0.33, 95% CI −0.13 to 0.79). The much-publicised SWIFT "80% remission at 12 months" figure comes from a **conference presentation and company press release, not a peer-reviewed publication** — the published SWIFT trial reports only to week 6 — so it should be quoted as company-reported and unverified, not as established durability. Practically, accelerated TMS should be taught as an *acute* treatment whose relapse curve and maintenance schedule are still being worked out, i.e. do not promise a one-and-done cure. **Blinding integrity** is the second caveat: the adolescent trial found clinician identification of active treatment exceeded chance, and sham TMS is intrinsically hard to blind — which inflates every effect size in this literature and is one more reason the controlled estimates (d≈0.4–0.65) are the ones to trust over the open-label spectaculars.

## Where the verdict would move

For a teaching map the "expiry conditions" are concrete and near. A **1000-patient, 7-site Magnus observational optimisation study** of SAINT (NCT05819021) is due to complete in late 2026 and is the first large real-world effectiveness read on whether the RCT effect holds outside Stanford; if community remission lands near the pragmatic-trial range rather than 90%, that confirms the teaching above. A head-to-head **accelerated-vs-conventional TBS RCT in late-life depression** (NCT06854367, n=280, readout 2029) will test whether acceleration helps a harder population. A peer-reviewed SWIFT durability paper would upgrade its evidence from "non-inferior at 6 weeks" to a real maintenance claim. Two Magnus multi-site trials (mechanistic and inpatient, NCT03693105 / NCT06462820) were terminated at near-zero enrolment — an operational rather than a futility signal, but worth watching for whether the commercial trial programme delivers the larger sham-controlled dataset the field still lacks. Nothing on the horizon is likely to overturn "accelerated iTBS beats sham"; what is genuinely open is *how much*, *for how long*, and *whether the expensive targeting earns its cost*.
