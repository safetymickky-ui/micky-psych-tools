# Relapse-prevention pharmacotherapy for alcohol use disorder with limited adherence support: what to put first
*2026-07-14 · PubMed 7 · trials 4 · books 0*

> Decision handed over pre-locked (no intent-lock interview): *which relapse-prevention agent to put first on
> formulary for a setting with limited adherence support — comparative NNTs of naltrexone, acamprosate, and
> disulfiram; second-line topiramate/baclofen; matched to a service that cannot supervise every dose.* The
> reading it beat: a comparative-efficacy survey ranked by effect size alone. Answered the formulary-under-
> constraint question — because in this setting adherence, not raw efficacy, is the binding variable.

## Verdict

**Lead with oral naltrexone 50 mg once daily; use acamprosate as the co-first-line for the abstinent or hepatically-impaired patient; and do NOT default to disulfiram — it works only under supervision.** Concretely:

1. **First-line, adherence-constrained setting: naltrexone.** Both naltrexone and acamprosate are genuine first-line agents with comparable, modest effect — but naltrexone's **once-daily** dosing (and a monthly extended-release injectable, XR-NTX) fits a low-supervision service far better than acamprosate's **two tablets three times a day**. Numbers to promise honestly: the number needed to treat (NNT) to prevent one return to *any* drinking is **~11 for acamprosate and ~18 for naltrexone**; naltrexone additionally prevents one return to *heavy* drinking for every **~11** treated.
2. **Acamprosate as co-first-line where its profile fits:** the patient who is already detoxified and abstinence-focused, or who has **liver disease** (acamprosate is renally cleared, naltrexone is hepatically handled and carries a hepatotoxicity caution). Its weakness in this setting is the six-pills-a-day burden.
3. **Disulfiram: only with supervision, never as the unsupervised default.** Its apparent efficacy is an artefact of trial design — it works in open-label/supervised trials and vanishes in blinded ones. Reserve it for the motivated patient with a reliable dose-supervisor (spouse, clinic-observed, directly-observed therapy); prescribing it to be taken unwitnessed in a low-support setting is the trap this decision must avoid.
4. **Second line, off-label:** **topiramate** (reduces heavy drinking; titrate slowly for cognitive/paraesthesia effects), **gabapentin** (useful when withdrawal symptoms, anxiety, or insomnia coexist), and **baclofen** (its real niche is **advanced liver disease**, where it is the best-studied option and not hepatically metabolised).
5. **Pharmacotherapy sits on top of psychosocial support** — the two are additive — but the support need not be intensive; brief/medical-management-level counselling is enough to carry the drug effect, which is what makes this deployable in a limited-resource clinic.

Confidence: **high** that naltrexone and acamprosate are first-line and disulfiram is supervision-dependent; **moderate** on the naltrexone-first ordering (it follows from the adherence constraint, not from a head-to-head efficacy win — the two are roughly equal on efficacy); **moderate** on second-line placement.

## The first-line pair, and why the setting picks naltrexone

The comparative-efficacy question is settled by a large, recent synthesis. The JAMA 2023 systematic review (118 trials, 20,976 participants) found, in conjunction with psychosocial care, that **acamprosate and oral naltrexone 50 mg/day are the two agents with the best evidence** for reducing drinking, with numbers needed to treat to prevent a return to any drinking of **11 (95% CI 1–32) for acamprosate and 18 (95% CI 4–32) for naltrexone**, and an NNT of **11 (95% CI 5–41)** for naltrexone to prevent a return to heavy drinking. Injectable extended-release naltrexone reduced drinking days (weighted mean difference −4.99 days over 30 days). The network meta-analysis of 156 trials (27,334 participants) corroborates the ordering and adds the others: for total abstinence versus placebo, risk ratios were baclofen 1.80, disulfiram 1.71, gabapentin 1.66, acamprosate 1.33, and oral naltrexone 1.15; for reducing heavy drinking, oral naltrexone (RR 0.81) and acamprosate (0.78) again anchor the evidence.

These two are close enough on efficacy that the choice belongs to *fit*, and in a setting defined by limited adherence support the deciding axis is dosing burden and monitoring. Naltrexone is **once daily**, targets craving and the heavy-drinking pattern (so it can be started in a patient who is still drinking), and has a **monthly injectable** form whose whole rationale is adherence — the XR-NTX trials show benefit precisely in higher-severity patients and frame it as "adherence pharmacotherapy." Acamprosate is **thrice-daily**, works best for maintaining abstinence in the already-detoxified, and its pill burden is exactly the failure point a low-support clinic cannot cushion. So the operational rule: **naltrexone first when adherence is the risk; acamprosate when the patient is abstinent, motivated, or has hepatic impairment.** The one hard contraindication to remember is that naltrexone (an opioid antagonist) cannot be given to a patient on opioids or needing opioid analgesia — there, acamprosate is the answer.

## Disulfiram: the drug whose evidence is a design artefact

Disulfiram looks strong in the network meta-analysis (heavy-drinking RR 0.19 — the largest single effect in the table), and that is exactly why it is dangerous to read naively. The dedicated meta-analysis of 22 randomised trials resolves the paradox: disulfiram was superior to controls overall (Hedges' g 0.58), **but the effect appeared only in open-label trials (g 0.70) and disappeared entirely in blinded ones** — because the drug works through the *threat* of the disulfiram–alcohol reaction, which a blinded patient cannot anticipate. The practical translation is unambiguous: disulfiram is effective **when, and only when, someone witnesses the dose** — a supervising partner, a clinic, directly-observed therapy. In a service that cannot supervise, an unsupervised disulfiram prescription is a placebo with a hepatotoxicity risk. Its legitimate niche is narrow and real — the motivated, abstinence-committed patient with a reliable supervisor — and it should be written into the protocol *with* that supervision requirement, never as the low-effort default it superficially resembles.

## The second line, and matching it to the patient

When the first-line agents fail, are contraindicated, or the patient needs a different target, three off-label options carry usable evidence, each with a distinguishing feature that decides when to reach for it:

- **Topiramate** reduces heavy drinking days (network meta-analysis: short-term SMD −0.35, and more days without drinking) and can be started while the patient is still drinking, but causes more adverse events than placebo (cognitive slowing, paraesthesia) and must be titrated slowly — the trade that keeps it second-line.
- **Gabapentin** improves abstinence (RR 1.66 vs placebo) and earns its place when **alcohol-withdrawal symptoms, anxiety, or insomnia coexist**, where it treats two problems at once; use with awareness of misuse potential in some populations.
- **Baclofen** reduces relapse risk (Cochrane: RR 0.87) and increases percentage of abstinent days (mean difference +9.07), mainly in already-detoxified patients, at the cost of sedation/fatigue. Its decisive niche is **advanced liver disease** — it is not hepatically metabolised and has the most alcohol-specific evidence of any agent in that population, where naltrexone's hepatic caution and the others' profiles are least comfortable.

Nalmefene (as-needed, for drinking *reduction* rather than abstinence) has long-term reduction evidence but more adverse events and uncertain local availability, so it sits outside the core formulary here.

## Where this verdict inverts

- **Concurrent opioid use or need for opioid analgesia:** naltrexone is contraindicated — acamprosate becomes first-line.
- **Significant hepatic impairment:** avoid naltrexone's hepatotoxicity caution and disulfiram entirely; acamprosate (renal clearance) and baclofen move up.
- **A reliable dose-supervisor exists and the patient wants an abstinence "insurance policy":** supervised disulfiram becomes a legitimate, even preferred, first choice — the one setting where its large open-label effect is real.
- **Goal is controlled reduction, not abstinence:** naltrexone (targeted/as-needed dosing) or nalmefene fit better than acamprosate, which is an abstinence-maintenance drug.

## What would change this verdict

Very little in the near term — this is a mature area. The ongoing Phase 3/4 alcohol-pharmacotherapy trials are mechanistic or formulation studies (emergency-department-initiated naltrexone/gabapentin, an acamprosate electrophysiology study, an XR-naltrexone reward-drinker-phenotype trial), **not** challenges to the first-line status of naltrexone and acamprosate. So a new drug will not rescue a patient this year; what moves outcomes is *deployment* — getting an effective agent, matched to the patient and the setting's adherence reality, actually started and continued. A clinic auditing its own initiation and 6-month continuation rates against this formulary would be measuring the variable that matters.
