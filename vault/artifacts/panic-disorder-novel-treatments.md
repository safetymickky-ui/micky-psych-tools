## Bottom line

**For panic disorder *specifically*, nothing novel has crossed the evidence threshold to prescribe today — with one exception that is "novel" only in delivery, not mechanism: digital/internet-delivered and VR-based exposure CBT, which have genuine panic efficacy and are the emerging option actually worth using now.** The pharmacology generating the headlines — psychedelics (psilocybin, LSD/MM120), ketamine — is real but is being tested in *generalized* anxiety, depression, PTSD and end-of-life anxiety, essentially *not* in panic disorder; there is no ongoing panic-specific psychedelic or ketamine trial. Neuromodulation splits the same way: rTMS improves generalized anxiety but **failed to reach significance in panic** (panic-severity SMD 1.19, *P* = .054), and tDCS/vagus-style stimulation have no panic RCT. Pharmacological augmentation of exposure — D-cycloserine, cannabidiol — has **underdelivered** (D-cycloserine near-null; cannabidiol frankly negative in refractory panic). So the honest verdict is **watch, don't prescribe**: for a panic patient past the standard algorithm, the evidence-based novel move is a *better-delivered* known therapy (digital/VR CBT, and returning to optimised pharmacotherapy + in-person CBT), while the molecular pipeline is aimed elsewhere or years away. Confidence **moderate-high** — the null/negative panic results and the *absence* of panic-specific novel-drug trials point the same way.

The useful thing to notice is that the novelty gradient in panic is **inverted from the hype**: where the field is loudest (psychedelics, ketamine, brain stimulation) the panic-specific evidence is absent or null; where it is quietest (delivery innovation) the panic-specific evidence is actually solid.

## The one novel approach with real panic-specific evidence — digital & VR-delivered therapy

If "novel" is allowed to mean *new delivery of an effective therapy* rather than *new molecule*, this is the category that pays off in panic. Virtual-reality exposure therapy shows a large effect versus waitlist (Hedges *g* ≈ 0.90) and versus psychological placebo (*g* ≈ 0.78), and — the load-bearing finding — is statistically **indistinguishable from in-vivo exposure** (*g* ≈ −0.07), so it is a genuine substitute where real-world exposure is impractical. For CBT delivery formats in panic disorder specifically, a network meta-analysis of 74 trials (6,699 patients) found **guided self-help** (SMD −0.42) as effective as face-to-face individual (SMD −0.43) and group (SMD −0.47) CBT, all superior to treatment-as-usual, while **unguided self-help was not** (SMD −0.21, non-significant). The clinical translation: internet-delivered CBT *with* therapist guidance, and VR exposure, are legitimate, evidence-backed options now — and the *guidance* is the active ingredient, exactly as it is when tapering a drug. This is also where nearly all the ongoing panic-specific trial activity actually lives (see the pipeline below).

## The exciting pharmacology is real — but not in panic

The psychedelic and glutamatergic renaissance is producing large effect sizes, and it is worth understanding precisely why it does **not** yet touch panic disorder.

**Psychedelics.** A 2024 meta-analysis across mental disorders found striking effects — psilocybin *g* = −1.49 (−1.67 to −1.30), ayahuasca −1.34, MDMA −0.83, LSD −0.65 — but the underlying trials are overwhelmingly in depression, end-of-life/cancer-related anxiety, PTSD and addiction, **not panic disorder**. The single most rigorous new anxiety signal is a Phase 2b RCT of **MM120 (lysergide/LSD)** in *generalized* anxiety disorder (198 adults): a single 100-µg or 200-µg dose cut HAM-A by a placebo-adjusted −5.0 points (−9.6 to −0.4) and −6.0 points (−9.8 to −2.0) at four weeks against a 2.5-point minimal-important difference, with lower doses non-significant. Impressive — and it is GAD, single-dose, and only now moving to Phase 3, with dose-dependent perceptual adverse events reaching ~100% at the top dose. None of this is panic data, and panic's core mechanism (catastrophic misinterpretation of bodily sensations) is one an acutely perception-altering drug could plausibly *aggravate* — a reason panic has been left out, not merely overlooked.

**Ketamine.** Its robust evidence base is in *depression* (repeated-dose racemic and intranasal esketamine), not anxiety; the anxiety-disorder data are a few small refractory-GAD/SAD trials showing transient benefit, with essentially nothing in panic. Ketamine is not a panic treatment on current evidence.

**Novel anxiolytic chemistry.** GABA-A **α-subunit-selective modulators** — designed to keep benzodiazepine-like anxiolysis while shedding sedation, dependence and tolerance — remain in early development, with several compounds stalled before or during clinical trials; a real long-term goal, not a 2026 option. Agomelatine and cannabidiol appear in anxiety-treatment updates as "gathering evidence," but for panic the cannabidiol evidence is already in and negative (below).

## Neuromodulation — works in generalized anxiety, not (yet) in panic

The cleanest negative in this whole landscape. A 2022 systematic review/meta-analysis of rTMS across generalized anxiety and panic disorder (13 studies, 677 participants) found large, significant improvement in **GAD** (anxiety SMD = 1.45, *P* < .001) but, in **panic disorder, no significant effect** — overall anxiety SMD = 0.24 (*P* = .48) and panic-severity SMD = 1.19 (*P* = .054), i.e. it missed significance. An evidence-based panic-disorder treatment review reaches the same conclusion, describing only "minimal data" supporting rTMS (or ECT) in panic. Transcranial direct-current stimulation and transcutaneous vagus/trigeminal stimulation have preliminary signals in social and generalized anxiety but **no completed panic-disorder RCT**. So neuromodulation is not a defensible panic option today; it is an open research question, and one small trigeminal-nerve-stimulation study is the nearest thing to a panic-specific test (pipeline below).

## Pharmacologically augmenting exposure has underdelivered

The theory — give a drug that boosts fear-extinction learning during exposure sessions — is elegant and has largely failed to translate. **D-cycloserine**, the most-studied example, gave only a small, non-durable augmentation in an individual-participant-data meta-analysis of 21 trials/1,047 patients (post-treatment mean difference −3.62, *d* = −0.25; **no longer significant at follow-up**, *d* = −0.19), and a Cochrane review of 21 RCTs (788 patients) found **no difference** from placebo augmentation (responder RR 1.10, 0.89–1.34), with only two of those trials in panic. **Cannabidiol** augmentation of exposure in treatment-refractory panic-with-agoraphobia and social anxiety was outright **negative** — no benefit over placebo on the Fear Questionnaire, no enhancement of within-session extinction. The lesson: extinction-enhancer augmentation is not a clinically usable panic strategy on current evidence.

## What's actually in the panic pipeline

The publication-bias/forward-looking check is where the "novel" question really gets answered, and the registry is blunt: of ~35 currently active interventional trials that enrol panic-disorder patients, the **overwhelming majority are psychotherapy-delivery studies** — internet/transdiagnostic/unified-protocol/self-compassion/schema/ACT/metacognitive CBT — not new drugs or devices. That distribution *is* the finding: the field's own bet for panic is better psychotherapy delivery, not novel pharmacology. The handful of genuinely device- or drug-novel panic-relevant trials to watch:

- **Trigeminal nerve stimulation** in panic/GAD/SAD (NCT04931134, active, readout ~end-2025) — the nearest thing to a panic neuromodulation test.
- **Capnometry-guided respiratory training / Freespira** breathing device (NCT02998502; and capnometry-guided respiratory intervention NCT05427708, readout 2027) — a panic-specific, mechanism-targeted (CO₂/hyperventilation) device approach.
- **fMRI neurofeedback**-enhanced cognitive reappraisal for anxiety incl. panic (NCT06563310, readout 2029) — early-stage.
- **Hydroxyzine vs escitalopram** pilot (NCT05737511, n≈80, readout Dec 2026) — repurposing an old antihistamine, not truly novel, and too small to move first-line.
- **MM120 (LSD)** Phase 3 program (built on NCT05407064) — the psychedelic signal to watch, but in **GAD**, not panic.

None of these is large or panic-first enough to expect a practice change before ~2027–2029.

## Where this breaks, and what would change it

This verdict is about **panic disorder specifically and about *now***. It would read differently for generalized anxiety (where rTMS, tDCS and MM120 already have positive signals) — so if the real patient is a mixed anxiety presentation, some of these "not-in-panic" options re-enter. It is also a snapshot: the psychedelic field is moving fast in adjacent disorders, and a positive Phase 3 for MM120 in GAD would make a panic trial likely within a few years. The specific things that would flip "watch" to "try": a positive panic-disorder RCT of an intranasal/oral GABA-A-selective anxiolytic; a completed, positive trigeminal-nerve-stimulation or Freespira panic trial; or a deliberately panic-focused psychedelic trial — none of which exists yet. Absent those, the evidence-based "novel" move for a panic patient past the standard algorithm is to **optimise and re-deliver what works** (guided digital or VR-delivered exposure CBT, alongside the pharmacotherapy sequence in the companion report), not to reach for a molecule or a device the panic evidence does not yet support.
