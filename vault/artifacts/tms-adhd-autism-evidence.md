# TMS for ADHD or autism — is the evidence strong enough to offer it?
*2026-07-17 · PubMed 16 · trials 12*

> Assumed (this decision was set by default — the intent-lock picker was not run in this autonomous session): the question is **whether to offer repetitive TMS (rTMS/theta-burst) as a treatment for the *core symptoms* of ADHD or of autism** — the call a clinician makes when a family asks for it, usually because a private "brain-stimulation" clinic markets it or because stimulants/behavioural supports were refused or fell short. Both disorders are covered because the request names both; the verdict is the same shape for each, with condition-specific detail. Say if you meant a narrower reading (a single disorder, a single symptom target, or the device economics).
> Skipped: **TMS for comorbid depression in an ADHD or autistic patient** — a *different and better-supported* indication (TMS is regulator-cleared for major depression), not to be conflated with treating the neurodevelopmental core; motor-cortex/plasticity *biomarker* studies that never targeted symptoms; and paediatric device-purchase economics.

**Verdict.** **Do not offer rTMS as a treatment for the core symptoms of ADHD or of autism outside a clinical trial.** For both, it is **investigational** — no TMS device is cleared by the FDA or any major regulator for either indication, the positive signals come from a handful of small, heterogeneous, mostly-unblinded trials, and the *most rigorous* syntheses do not show rTMS beating sham on the symptoms that matter. Where a least-weak signal exists: in **ADHD**, high-frequency or theta-burst rTMS to the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) may nudge inattention and hyperactivity, but the effect sizes are inflated by tiny samples and the pooled evidence is officially "inconclusive" — it is **not** an alternative to stimulants; in **autism**, low-frequency rTMS to the DLPFC has a small signal on *repetitive/stereotyped behaviour* in intellectually-capable youth only, with **no reliable effect on the social-communication core**. Tell families it is **safe but unproven** — do not promise symptom control and do not sell it as a replacement for stimulant medication (ADHD) or behavioural/educational intervention (autism). **Confidence: moderate** that it should not be routine care; the single thing that would move it is the set of adequately-powered multi-centre RCTs reading out 2026–2029.

## ADHD: the large effect sizes are an artefact of a few tiny trials

Read only the headline numbers and rTMS looks impressive. A 2025 meta-analysis reported that rTMS improved **inattention** with a Hedges *g* of **−1.44** (p=.002) and **hyperactivity/impulsivity** *g* = **−0.95** (p=.005) — yet the same paper's own conclusion is that "evidence for its therapeutic efficacy in ADHD remains inconclusive," because those pooled figures rest on **only 7 rTMS RCTs** inside a 33-RCT dataset dominated by tDCS. A second 2025 meta-analysis (8 studies, **325** patients) found inattention **SMD −0.94** (95% CI −1.33 to −0.56) and hyperactivity/impulsivity **SMD −0.98** (−1.27 to −0.69) — but *against "non-TMS interventions,"* not clean sham, which inflates the contrast. Effect sizes near −1.0 from ~300 patients spread across single-site trials with different coils, targets, frequencies and outcome scales are the textbook profile of a **fragile, over-estimated effect**, not an established one.

Point the more careful syntheses at the question and the effect thins to nothing. A 2024 meta-analysis found that **rTMS did not produce a significant therapeutic benefit** for ADHD symptoms (while tDCS did). A 2025 meta-analysis restricted to **adults** concluded the meta-analysis "did not reach significance for any" technique — neurofeedback, tDCS, or rTMS. A 2025 Bayesian **network meta-analysis** of 37 RCTs (1,615 participants) found **none of the non-invasive stimulation methods significantly improved inhibitory control versus sham** (the gains it did detect were tDCS montages on working memory). The 2021 benchmark review (18 studies; rTMS 4, tDCS 14; 311 patients) — the one that actually stimulated mostly the DLPFC — concluded there was "limited evidence" and it "did not support using rTMS or tDCS of the dlPFC as an alternative" therapy, while noting the uncomfortable fact that rTMS is **already offered clinically and commercially for ADHD despite the lack of solid evidence**. And a 2022 umbrella review that graded the whole neuromodulation field found, for ADHD, that only **tDCS** cleared the bar (SMD 0.23, GRADE moderate) — **TMS was not among the interventions shown to beat sham**. The deep-TMS-specific synthesis reached the same place: efficacy in the "hyperactivity/hyperarousal" disorders (ADHD, PTSD, schizophrenia) was **heterogeneous**, in contrast to the clean signals deep TMS shows in depression, OCD and addiction.

The most balanced current read comes from a 2025 double-blind-RCT-only review of neurodevelopmental disorders, which grants rTMS "promise" for hyperactivity, inattention and working memory in ADHD but immediately qualifies it: outcomes swing on **coil type (H5 vs H6) and target (right vs left DLPFC)**, sample sizes are small, and bias is a live concern — and it flags a **seizure during TMS in an ADHD trial** as a reminder that these are neurodevelopmental brains with elevated epilepsy risk. "Promise, protocol-dependent, unproven" is the honest ADHD verdict; "effective treatment" is not.

## Autism: a small signal on repetitive behaviour, nothing reliable on the social core

The autism literature has the same shape — a positive open-label surface over a null rigorous core — but it is worth separating the two symptom domains, because they do not move together. On **repetitive/restricted behaviour and cognition**, the positive syntheses cluster: a 2022 meta-analysis of intellectually-capable ASD reported **low-frequency rTMS to the DLPFC** improving cognition (**Hedges *g* 0.735**, 95% CI 0.242–1.228) and Criterion-B repetitive-behaviour symptoms (**g 0.435**, 0.359–0.511); a 2025 meta-analysis of 18 RCTs (813 intellectually-capable participants) found rTMS moving the Repetitive Behavior Scale (**SMD −0.62**, −1.17 to −0.07) and Aberrant Behavior Checklist (**SMD −0.53**, −0.79 to −0.26). But the 2022 paper rests on a body of work in which **only one study was a randomised, double-blind, sham-controlled trial** — the rest used waitlist controls, low pulse counts and no neuronavigation, most from a single research group — and a 2023 meta-analysis of 22 studies (829 participants) that found "positive effects" on repetitive behaviour immediately warned the evidence carries **moderate-to-high risk of bias and significant publication bias** and "must be treated with caution."

When the design is tightened to **RCTs versus sham only**, the rTMS signal collapses. The strongest autism synthesis — a 2024 **network meta-analysis of 16 RCTs (N=709)** — found that of fourteen active stimulation protocols, **only one tDCS montage** (anodal left-DLPFC + cathodal extracephalic) beat sham on overall autistic symptoms (SMD −1.40), and that **none of the interventions, rTMS and theta-burst included, significantly improved social-communication symptoms or restricted/repetitive behaviours**. The 2022 umbrella review agreed at the top level: for autism, only **tDCS** appeared (SMD 0.97, GRADE *very low*) — **TMS did not clear the bar at all**. So the defensible autism statement is narrow: a *small, bias-prone* signal that low-frequency DLPFC rTMS may reduce repetitive behaviour in **verbal, intellectually-capable** young people, and essentially **nothing** for the social-communication core that defines the condition or for minimally-verbal, intellectually-disabled children — the group for whom families most often seek it.

One conflation to refuse explicitly: a large share of "TMS in autism" trials actually treat **depression in autistic adults**, not autism itself. That is a legitimate, separately-evidenced use (TMS is cleared for major depression), and it should not be read as evidence that TMS treats autism.

## What is safe to say to a family, and where a trial is genuinely reasonable

The boundary is not safety — it is proof of benefit. rTMS in these populations is **well-tolerated**: a 2022 meta-analysis put the pooled adverse-event rate at **25%** (headache 10%, facial discomfort 15%, irritability 21%), all **mild and transient**, with serious events rare; the autism network meta-analysis found no intervention differed from sham on dropout and no serious adverse events. The real risk is a seizure — uncommon but non-zero, and weightier in neurodevelopmental disorders where the baseline epilepsy rate is raised (an ADHD-trial seizure is on record), so screening and standard TMS safety limits are mandatory.

Because it is safe, the reasonable uses are about *setting*, not efficacy. Enrolling in a **registered, sham-controlled trial** is defensible and helps answer the question. Offering TMS to an ADHD or autistic patient who *also* has **treatment-resistant major depression** is defensible — but on the depression clearance, framed as depression treatment, not as core-symptom treatment. What is **not** defensible is a family paying out-of-pocket at a commercial clinic for a course sold as treatment for inattention, hyperactivity, repetitive behaviour or social difficulty, or substituting it for stimulant medication or behavioural/educational support of established benefit. That is exactly the gap the 2021 review named — the marketing has run ahead of the evidence.

## What would change this verdict

Adequately-powered, blinded, sham-controlled multi-centre trials — the thing the field has never had — are finally running. In autism, the largest is a **6-site RCT of personalised-functional-sector-guided iTBS in low-functioning ASD (n=215, readout ~2026)** — precisely the intellectually-disabled group the current evidence cannot speak to; Yale (social cognition, n=100), CAMH and the Chang Gung group (theta-burst on superior temporal sulcus / inferior frontal gyrus) add targeted RCTs through 2027–2030. In ADHD, a cluster of sham-controlled trials reads out 2026–2029 — pre-SMA rTMS (Cincinnati, ~2027), accelerated iTBS in adolescents (Bradley, ~2029), paired-associative deep TMS (Ben-Gurion, n=90, ~2027), and an explicit "alternative to stimulants" trial (Calgary, ~2027) — plus two trials enrolling ADHD and autism together (Assiut, Foggia).

Weigh those against the publication-bias signal already in the registry: the industry **deep-TMS programmes in both disorders were quietly abandoned** — Brainsway's H-coil ADHD trials sit at "unknown"/unpublished status years past completion, and its deep-rTMS trial for low-functioning autism was **terminated**, as were a New-York repetitive-behaviour trial and a withdrawn Stanford irritability trial. Completed-but-unpublished and terminated device trials almost never carry good news. Until at least one of the large ongoing RCTs shows a benefit on a **validated clinical endpoint against real sham**, the verdict holds: investigational, not routine care.
