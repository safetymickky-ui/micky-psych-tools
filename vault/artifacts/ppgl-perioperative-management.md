# Perioperative Management of Pheochromocytoma & Sympathetic Paraganglioma (PPGL)
*2026-07-11 · PubMed 15 · trials 4 · books 0 · a phase-structured surgical-safety reference*

Assumed: adult non-metastatic functional PPGL for elective adrenalectomy; the reader wants doses, targets, and the hard safety rules, not tumour biology. ## Bottom line

**Every patient with a functional (catecholamine-secreting) PPGL gets pharmacologic preparation before elective resection — α-adrenergic blockade first, always before any β-blocker — titrated over ~7–14 days to a seated BP <130/80 mmHg (not <80/45 standing) and HR 60–70 bpm, with high-sodium diet and fluid loading to fill the contracted vascular space; intra-operatively, anticipate catecholamine surges on tumour manipulation (short-acting vasodilators ready) then abrupt hypotension after venous ligation (fluids/vasopressors ready); post-operatively, watch for hypotension and rebound hypoglycaemia for 24–48 h.** Confidence **moderate-high**: the *necessity* of α-blockade and the *alpha-before-beta* rule are near-universal guideline consensus, but the *choice of agent* (phenoxybenzamine vs a selective α1-blocker) is genuinely settled to "either" by a randomized trial, and whether truly normotensive tumours can skip blockade at all is being tested right now. The single non-negotiable: **never β-block an un-α-blocked PPGL** — unopposed α-stimulation can trigger a hypertensive crisis.

Surgery is the only cure, and the perioperative window is where the mortality lives: the catecholamine load that makes these tumours dangerous is released in bulk precisely when you handle them. Undiagnosed/unprepared disease is lethal — in autopsy series of unrecognised pheochromocytoma, **71%** died of cardiovascular causes, and pheochromocytoma carries an estimated **14-fold** increased risk of cardiovascular complications. Preparation converts that into a routinely survivable operation.

## Pre-operative preparation — the α-blockade window

**α-blockade is mandatory and comes first.** The Endocrine Society guideline is explicit: *all* patients with functional PPGL should undergo preoperative blockade to prevent perioperative complications, with a high-sodium diet and fluid intake to prevent postoperative hypotension. Two α-blockade strategies are in routine use:

- **Phenoxybenzamine** (non-selective, irreversible α1+α2): start **10 mg twice daily (2×10 mg)**, titrate up to **1 mg/kg/day** with BP driven to the lower limit of normal. Its covalent, long-half-life blockade gives the tightest intra-operative control but is the direct cause of the most **recalcitrant postoperative hypotension**.
- **Doxazosin** (selective, competitive α1): start **2 mg/day**, titrate up to **32 mg/day**. Fewer reflex-tachycardia and stuffy-nose/orthostatic side effects, shorter blockade, faster post-op recovery. (Prazosin and terazosin are the other selective α1 options.)

**Which agent? A randomized trial says it barely matters for the primary endpoint.** The PRESCRIPT multicentre RCT (n=134, non-metastatic PPGL, drug started 2–3 weeks pre-op on a BP-targeted titration) found the median cumulative intra-operative time outside the BP target (SBP >160 mmHg or MAP <60 mmHg) was **11.1% with phenoxybenzamine vs 12.2% with doxazosin (P=.75)** — no difference. Phenoxybenzamine did win on the secondary hemodynamic-instability score (**38.0 vs 50.0, P=.02**), but 30-day cardiovascular complication rates were similar (**8.8% vs 6.9%, P=.68**) with no mortality. Observational data sharpen the trade-off: selective blockade produces **more intra-operative hypotension** (SBP <80 mmHg in 67% vs 35% with phenoxybenzamine, p=.03; more post-op pressor/ICU need, p=.02) but phenoxybenzamine's longer blockade means a **longer hospital stay** (12.2 vs 6.2 days, p<.001). Net: either agent is acceptable; phenoxybenzamine for the most reactive tumours at the cost of post-op hypotension, a selective α1-blocker for a cleaner recovery.

**"Alpha before beta" — the load-bearing safety rule.** A β-blocker started *without* prior α-blockade risks a hypertensive crisis through unopposed α-adrenergic stimulation (β-mediated vasodilation removed while α-vasoconstriction runs unopposed). Only **after** adequate α-blockade, and only for reflex tachycardia, add a β-blocker: if HR is >100 bpm **3–4 days after** α-blockade began, start **propranolol 20 mg three times daily (up to 3×40 mg/day)** or **atenolol 25 mg/day (up to 50 mg/day)**. Tachycardia is common specifically with phenoxybenzamine.

**Adjuncts.** A **calcium-channel blocker** is added when α-blockade alone won't control BP, or used alone in normotensive/mildly hypertensive patients or those intolerant of α-blockers. **Metyrosine** (tyrosine hydroxylase inhibitor; start **250 mg orally twice daily**, increase every 48 h) is reserved for α-blocker intolerance or refractory hypertension, and may reduce intra-operative instability when combined with phenoxybenzamine.

**Volume expansion is part of the prescription, not an afterthought** — the whole point of pre-loading is to survive the post-ligation crash. High-sodium diet **5000 mg/day** plus fluid intake **2.5 L/day**, or **1–2 L of IV 0.9% saline the day before** surgery.

**Targets and duration.** Aim for seated BP **<130/80 mmHg**, standing BP **not below 80/45 mmHg**, HR **60–70 bpm seated / 70–80 standing**, with the patient home-charting BP and pulse ≥ twice daily. There is no trial-defined optimal duration, but **7–14 days** normalises BP and HR in most; even normotensive patients start low-dose α-blockade. Operate once controlled — one series used a threshold of **BP ≤130/85 mmHg**; another defined "adequately prepared" as steady BP <140/90 mmHg, and inadequate preparation (pre-surgical SBP >133 mmHg) carried a **6-fold** higher odds of hemodynamic instability.

## Intra-operative management — two opposite emergencies in one operation

**Set-up.** Radial arterial line for beat-to-beat pressure; large-bore venous or central access. Minimally invasive (laparoscopic) adrenalectomy is preferred for most pheochromocytomas, open resection for most paragangliomas. Approach matters for stability: in a 341-patient multicentre analysis the **retroperitoneal** approach carried higher odds of MAP <60 mmHg than transperitoneal (OR 6.26, 95% CI 1.13–34.2), and the operating centre itself was an independent predictor of instability on all definitions — arguing for a standardised anaesthetic.

**Phase 1 — hypertensive surges on manipulation.** Handling the tumour dumps catecholamines. First move is to **stop manipulation** and let pressure fall; pharmacologically, have short-acting titratable agents drawn up:

- **Phentolamine** 5 mg IV bolus, repeat as needed
- **Nicardipine** infusion 5 mg/h, up by 2.5 mg/h every 5 min to max 15 mg/h
- **Clevidipine** 1–2 mg/h, double every 90 s (max 32 mg/h)
- **Nitroglycerin** 5 µg/min up to 100 µg/min
- **Magnesium sulfate** (4 g in 250 mL 5% dextrose, ≤3 mL/min) for refractory hypertension — first-line in pregnancy
- **Esmolol** 0.5–1.0 mg/kg load then 50–150 µg/kg/min (max 300) — for tachyarrhythmia, and only on an α-blocked patient
- **Labetalol** (α:β ≈ 1:4–7) as an adjunct

**Phase 2 — the crash after venous ligation.** The instant tumour venous drainage is clamped, circulating catecholamines fall off a cliff into a vasodilated, α-blocked, relatively hypovolaemic circulation. Pre-emptive **IV isotonic fluid** is the mainstay; add vasopressors (**norepinephrine**, or **vasopressin 0.03–0.04 units/min** for catecholamine-resistant vasoplegia) as needed. Pure α-agonists such as phenylephrine are of limited use here because the α-receptors are still blocked from pre-op preparation.

## Post-operative care — 24–48 hours of watching for the two rebounds

Follow closely for **24–48 h** (ICU/high-dependency for higher-risk cases). In the PHEO-RISK cohort, **19.1%** had a post-surgical complication; **prolonged hypotension (9.9%)** was the commonest, and complications clustered in patients with diabetes, cerebrovascular disease, higher glucose, higher urinary metanephrines/norepinephrine, and tumours **>5 cm**.

- **Hypotension** (BP <90/60 mmHg): from the sudden catecholamine withdrawal plus residual α-blockade and a still-expanded vascular bed — worst and most protracted after phenoxybenzamine. Treat with fluids first, then vasopressors (norepinephrine, and vasopressin for resistant cases).
- **Rebound hypoglycaemia**: catecholamine excess suppresses pancreatic insulin; once the tumour is out, rebound hyperinsulinaemia drives glucose down, typically in the **first 4 hours**. Check glucose **every 6 h** (≥24 h), run **IV 5% dextrose** until oral intake, and re-adjust insulin in diabetics. β-blocker exposure worsens the risk via glycogenolysis effects.
- **Adrenal insufficiency**: not expected after unilateral adrenalectomy (contralateral gland compensates); anticipate and steroid-cover only bilateral/cortical-sparing cases or when clinically suspected. Cortical-sparing surgery prevents post-op insufficiency in up to 90%.
- Also watch for arrhythmia, myocardial injury/heart failure (including Takotsubo), stroke, and urinary retention.

## Risk stratification — who will be unstable

Predictors of intra-operative hemodynamic instability worth flagging pre-op: **large tumour (>4 cm; >5 cm for post-op complications)**, **urinary catecholamines >2000 µg/24 h**, a **postural BP drop >10 mmHg** after α-blockade, and **pre-op MAP >100 mmHg**. A validated nomogram (age, tumour shape, Mayo Adhesive Probability score, laterality, necrosis, BMI, surgical technique) reaches a C-index of **0.773**; instability still occurs in roughly **30%** of prepared patients — preparation reduces risk, it does not abolish it.

## Drugs & actions to AVOID (the safety banner)

- **β-blocker before α-blockade** — unopposed α-stimulation → hypertensive crisis. α-blockade **always** first.
- **Morphine and codeine** — the Endocrine Society guideline recommends avoidance (histamine/catecholamine effects); **fentanyl** is tolerated and a safe alternative.
- **Dopamine-receptor antagonists (e.g. metoclopramide), corticosteroids, and tricyclic antidepressants** — known to precipitate catecholamine crisis.
- **Unprepared/inadequately blocked surgery** — operating on an un-optimised functional PPGL is the core avoidable catastrophe; also a trigger for pheochromocytoma multisystem crisis alongside tumour manipulation, trauma, and non-adrenal surgical stress.

## What would change this verdict

The direction of travel is toward **less** blanket preparation for the mildest tumours, and toward "either α-blocker." Live/recent registry evidence:

- **PRESCRIPT (NCT01379898)** and a second RCT (**NCT03176693**, UCLA, n=39) both compared phenoxybenzamine vs doxazosin and found no decisive winner — cementing "either agent."
- **NCT05702944** (Seoul National University, PHASE 4, *recruiting*, n=24, primary completion **Aug 2027**) is a pilot RCT testing whether preoperative α-blockade can be **omitted** in *normotensive* pheochromocytoma. A positive result would carve out a genuine exception to "block everyone."
- A completed meta-analysis of selective vs non-selective blockade (**NCT04557072**, n=1344) continues to find no complication-rate difference.

Nothing on the horizon challenges *alpha-before-beta* or the *volume-loading + 24–48 h rebound watch* — those are stable.
