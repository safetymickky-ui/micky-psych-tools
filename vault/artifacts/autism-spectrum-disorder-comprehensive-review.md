Autism sits at the center of an unusually live argument about what a psychiatric category is *for*. Three tensions run through every section below: whether autism is one spectrum or a set of conditions that share a name, whether it is a disorder to be treated or a form of neurodivergence to be accommodated, and why — after decades of mechanistic progress — not a single drug modifies its core features. The novel concepts this review foregrounds are not fringe: they are the axes on which the field is currently turning.

## Definition & nosology

DSM-5 (2013) performed the defining nosological act: it **collapsed the former subtypes — autistic disorder, Asperger's disorder, PDD-NOS — into a single "autism spectrum disorder"** defined by two symptom domains (persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction; restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities) and graded by three severity levels reflecting support needs. ICD-11 aligned with a single spectrum construct. DSM-5-TR refined wording without reversing the merger. In practice the "single construct" masks heterogeneous ascertainment: in U.S. surveillance meeting the case definition, **74.7% of children had a documented diagnostic statement, 65.2% a special-education classification, 71.6% an ICD code, and only 37.4% had all three** — the same label attached by different routes.

That heterogeneity drives the sharpest nosological debate of the decade. The Lancet Commission on autism proposed the administrative term **"profound autism"** for the minority likely to need lifelong 24-hour access to a caregiver — typically those with substantial intellectual disability, minimal language, and high support needs — arguing that a single undifferentiated spectrum obscures their needs. Opposing that, the **neurodiversity paradigm** reframes autism "as one form of variation within a diversity of minds," challenging the deficit-based medical model in which autism has sat since it was first described. Whether the future of the diagnosis is *stratification* (profound autism as a distinct category) or *de-medicalization* (autism as identity) is genuinely unsettled, and it colors the treatment and prognosis sections that follow. A dedicated ICD-11 primary text and a DSM-5-TR severity-level validation study were not retrieved for this review *[thin]*.

## Epidemiology

Recorded autism prevalence has risen steeply and continues to. U.S. surveillance put prevalence at **27.6 per 1,000 eight-year-old children in 2020 — approximately 1 in 36** — up from roughly 1 in 150 two decades earlier, and household-survey data show a rise from **1.1% to 2.5% across 2009–2017**. Site-to-site variation is wide (23.1 to 44.9 per 1,000 across surveillance sites; statewide estimates of 1.5–2.3% for ages 3–21), which — as with ADHD — points to ascertainment rather than a uniform biological increase. The rise is best read as broadened criteria, greater awareness, service-linked diagnosis, and improved detection, not an established true epidemic.

Two epidemiological facts have shifted recently. The sex ratio is **3.8:1 boys to girls (43.0 vs 11.4 per 1,000, ≈4% of boys and 1% of girls)**, but that gap is now understood to be inflated by under-recognition of girls, whose presentations are more often missed. And for the **first time, 2020 prevalence was lower among non-Hispanic White children (24.3 per 1,000) than among Black (29.3), Hispanic (31.6), and Asian/Pacific Islander (33.4) children** — a reversal of the historical direction that reflects improving access for previously under-diagnosed groups. Diagnosis remains late (median age of earliest diagnosis **49 months**), and autism ranks among the top-ten nervous-system conditions for age-standardized disability worldwide.

## Etiology & pathophysiology

Autism is highly heritable: a five-country population cohort of over two million individuals estimated **median heritability at 80.8% (95% CI 73.2–85.5%)**, with **maternal effects of only 0.4–1.6%** — laying to rest the idea of a substantial in-utero maternal contribution. The genetic architecture is dual. On the rare-variant side, the largest exome study identified **102 risk genes** (false-discovery rate ≤ 0.1), of which 49 were more associated with severe neurodevelopmental delay and 53 more specifically with autism — a molecular hint that "autism with intellectual disability" and "autism without" may be partly distinct. On the common-variant side, GWAS identified **five genome-wide-significant loci**, seven of them shared with schizophrenia, depression, and educational attainment — pleiotropy that parallels the ADHD story and undergirds the co-occurrence section.

The leading mechanistic hypothesis is **excitation–inhibition (E/I) imbalance**: risk-gene expression is enriched in *both* excitatory and inhibitory cortical neuronal lineages, "consistent with multiple paths to an excitatory–inhibitory imbalance underlying ASD." This is the theory that most of the failed drug trials below were built to test. As with ADHD, the honest clinical bottom line on biomarkers is negative. Even the most promising early behavioral markers do not behave as unitary biological signatures — infant looking to eyes versus mouth and preference for faces versus objects "reflect distinct genetic influences, and neither appear to have a clear-cut relation to autism." **No genetic, imaging, EEG, or eye-tracking test diagnoses autism**; diagnosis remains behavioral.

## Clinical features & course

The core is the dyad written into the diagnostic criteria: **persistent deficits in social communication and social interaction, plus restricted, repetitive patterns of behavior, interests, or activities**, present from early development. Onset markers typically emerge in the second and third years, though presentation and timing vary widely. The clinically load-bearing fact for management is that **there are no approved pharmacological therapies for the core features** — everything that follows in treatment addresses associated symptoms, not autism itself.

Course understanding has been enriched by two newer concepts. **Camouflaging** — the effortful masking of autistic features — is more pronounced in females: autistic women show higher social reciprocity than autistic men despite equivalent underlying autistic-trait levels, and comparable theory-of-mind performance despite that greater reciprocity, a compensatory pattern that "may contribute to delay in the recognition of difficulties and provision of support." **Autistic burnout** — described in adults as chronic exhaustion, loss of skills, and reduced tolerance of stimulation, distinct from occupational burnout or depression and linked to sustained masking — names a real and previously unlabeled trajectory. Quantitative prospective infant-sibling trajectory data were not deeply harvested for this review *[thin]*.

## Diagnosis & assessment

Diagnosis is clinical and observational, anchored by standardized instruments. The Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule, Second Edition (ADOS-2) achieves **sensitivity 0.89–0.92 and specificity 0.81–0.85**, outperforming the Autism Diagnostic Interview–Revised (ADI-R: **sensitivity 0.75, specificity 0.82**); accuracy is consistently lower in routine clinical than in research settings (ADI-R specificity 0.72 clinical vs 0.85 research), a caution against over-reading a single instrument. For first-stage screening, the Modified Checklist for Autism in Toddlers (M-CHAT-R/F) pools to **sensitivity 0.83 (95% CI 0.77–0.88) and specificity 0.94 (0.89–0.97)**, with best performance when the follow-up interview is completed. Despite these tools, diagnosis remains late (median 49 months), and telehealth and machine-learning–assisted diagnostic approaches — an active development area — were not quantified here *[thin]*.

## Differential & comorbidity

Co-occurring conditions are the rule in autism, and they dominate its clinical burden. A meta-analysis of 96 studies places **ADHD in 28% (95% CI 25–32)** of autistic people — the single most common co-occurring psychiatric condition — followed by **anxiety disorders 20% (17–23)**, sleep–wake disorders 13%, disruptive/impulse-control disorders 12%, **depressive disorders 11% (9–13)**, OCD 9%, bipolar disorder 5%, and schizophrenia-spectrum disorders 4%; every estimate exceeds general-population rates, and clinical samples run higher than population registries.

The **AuDHD** overlap is the emerging concept that most changes management. Recognized as a dual diagnosis only since DSM-5 removed the prohibition in 2013, ADHD-in-autism is common enough (28%) that co-occurrence should be actively screened for rather than assumed absent. It matters therapeutically because the two disorders' first-line treatments collide: in the autistic patient with ADHD, **α-adrenergic agonists such as guanfacine may be better tolerated and more suitable than stimulants** for some, and **buspirone or mirtazapine are often preferred over SSRIs** for co-occurring anxiety. The ADHD side of this overlap, including the shared genetic architecture, is developed in [[ADHD — Comprehensive Review]].

Medical and cognitive comorbidity is heavy and unequally distributed. **Intellectual disability co-occurs in 37.9% of U.S. eight-year-olds with autism**, but with a striking disparity — present in **50.8% of Black versus 31.8% of White** autistic children — a gap that reflects differential ascertainment of milder, higher-functioning presentations across groups. Epilepsy is a well-recognized medical comorbidity, particularly alongside intellectual disability, though a clean pooled prevalence was not retrieved for this review *[unverified]*.

## Treatment

### Behavioral and developmental intervention

Early behavioral intervention is the standard of care, and the honest evidence for it is more fragile than its ubiquity suggests. The most rigorous synthesis (Project AIM: 130 samples, 6,240 children) found significant positive effects for behavioral, developmental, and naturalistic developmental-behavioral (NDBI) approaches **when study quality was ignored**; when analysis was restricted to randomized trials, benefit survived **only for developmental and NDBI** approaches; and when further restricted to randomized trials with outcomes free of detection bias, **no intervention type showed a significant effect on any outcome**. This does not mean early intervention is useless — it means the effect sizes are smaller and the evidence weaker than the field's confidence implies, and that unblinded parent-reported outcomes have inflated the record. Intervention remains recommended; certainty should be calibrated down.

### Pharmacological

No medication treats the core social-communication or restricted/repetitive features. Pharmacotherapy is confined to associated symptoms: **risperidone and aripiprazole are approved for irritability**, and **guanfacine** is used for hyperactivity/impulsivity and irritability depending on severity. That is the entire evidence-based drug armamentarium for autism itself.

### Emerging treatments — a record of honest failure

The novel-treatment pipeline is, so far, largely a catalogue of negative pivotal trials — which is itself the most important thing a clinician can know about it. **Intranasal oxytocin**, the most-hyped candidate, failed decisively: a 24-week phase 2 RCT in 290 children and adolescents found a between-group difference on the primary social-withdrawal outcome of **−0.2 (95% CI −1.5 to 1.0, P = 0.61)**, with no secondary-outcome separation. **Balovaptan**, a vasopressin V1a antagonist, missed its primary endpoint (SRS-2) in the phase 2 VANILLA trial despite a secondary adaptive-behavior signal, and vasopressin-antagonist trials as a class have been "unsuccessful in many cases." **Cannabidiol** proof-of-concept results were mixed and, by the authors' own judgment, "insufficient" — the primary parenting/behavior outcome did not separate from placebo, though a CGI-improvement signal appeared (49% vs 21% much/very-much improved, p = 0.005). **Microbiota transfer therapy** produced a striking ~80% reduction in gastrointestinal symptoms with behavioral improvement sustained at two years, but every published result is **open-label and uncontrolled**, which in a condition with large placebo and expectancy effects is close to uninterpretable. **Bumetanide**, built directly on the E/I-imbalance hypothesis, reached phase 3, but the pivotal program was subsequently halted *[final efficacy result unverified]*. The pattern — repeated failure of mechanistically rational drugs — is itself a finding: either the E/I and neuropeptide models are incomplete, or "autism" is too heterogeneous for a single molecule to move.

## Special populations

**Adults** are the most under-served group: services, diagnostic tools, and research all skew pediatric, even as the concepts of autistic burnout and camouflaging locate much of adult morbidity. **Women and girls** are systematically under- and later-diagnosed because of camouflaging and gender-atypical presentation, with downstream costs to mental health and support. The intersection of **autism, intellectual disability, and aging** is stark: among 14,477 adults with intellectual disability (with or without autism) followed against 506,207 controls, **23.7% died versus 13.8%, at a median age of 65.0 versus 80.0 years, with an age-standardized mortality ratio of 3.1 (95% CI 3.0–3.2)** and **31.7% of deaths deemed avoidable** — a health-inequality signal as much as a disease one.

## Prognosis

Outcomes are heterogeneous and, at the population level, marked by premature mortality. In a cohort of 27,122 autistic probands against 2.67 million controls, **2.60% died versus 0.91% of the general population (all-cause odds ratio 2.56, 95% CI 2.38–2.76)**, with excess mortality across nearly all cause-of-death categories and moderation by sex and intellectual ability; suicide and epilepsy are the classically cited leading excess causes (per-cause figures were not in the retrieved abstract). The intellectual-disability mortality data above corroborate the signal. Functional prognosis spans the full range from independent living and employment to lifelong high support needs, but hard population rates for employment, independent living, and "optimal outcome" were not retrieved for this review *[thin]*; the Lancet Commission remains the best narrative anchor for this heterogeneity, and it is precisely this spread that motivates the "profound autism" stratification debate.

## Controversies & future directions

**The vaccine hypothesis is settled science and should be stated as such.** A nationwide cohort of **657,461 children** (6,517 autism diagnoses over 5,025,754 person-years) found a fully adjusted MMR-vaccination hazard ratio for autism of **0.93 (95% CI 0.85–1.02)** — no association overall, none in susceptible subgroups, and none in any post-vaccination time window. There is no residual scientific controversy here, only a public-communication one.

The live controversies are conceptual. **Neurodiversity versus the medical model** is a genuine tension about whether autism is primarily a disability to be treated or a difference to be accommodated — and the answer is not the same across the spectrum, which is exactly why the **"profound autism" versus "one spectrum"** debate matters: a framework that fits an independent autistic adult may fail a nonverbal adult with profound intellectual disability, and vice versa. **Dimensional nosology** presses on autism as it does on ADHD, questioning where — or whether — the spectrum has a boundary.

The **therapeutic pipeline** is dominated by repurposed neuromodulators and, increasingly, gastrointestinal and novel-class agents, against the sobering backdrop of the failures above. Active registered trials include arbaclofen (GABA-B/mGluR) and bumetanide (still in a long-horizon phase 3), fecal microbiota transplantation, several cannabidiol and cannabinoid trials, pimavanserin (a 5-HT2A inverse agonist), propranolol for aggression, and an R-MDMA enantiomer — a pipeline notable for testing the E/I and social-cognition hypotheses from new angles while the field waits for its first core-symptom success. Whether the future lies in stratifying the spectrum before treating it, rather than seeking one treatment for all of it, is the question these trials will implicitly test.
