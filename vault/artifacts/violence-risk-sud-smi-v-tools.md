# Violence risk in SUD, the SMI-V layer: which structured tool for which job, and why the OAS is not a predictor

## Verdict

**Match the tool to the job: use a brief structured screen (V-RISK-10) at admission to grade monitoring intensity for the SMI-V population, reserve the HCR-20 for complex/forensic cases, and stop using the OAS as a risk-prediction tool — it measures an aggressive event after it happens, it does not predict one.** Concretely:

1. **For the SMI-V admission screen, use a brief structured tool — V-RISK-10.** It was purpose-built for acute, short-stay psychiatry (10 items, fast, acceptable inter-rater reliability, predictive validity in the useful range), which is exactly the SMI-V use-case. The HCR-20 (20-item structured professional judgment, forensic origin) has strong validity but is resource-heavy — reserve it for complex or forensic cases, not routine SMI-V screening.
2. **Read all these scores the way the evidence says they perform: good at ruling OUT, weak at ruling IN.** Structured violence tools have high negative predictive value (~91%) but only low-to-moderate positive predictive value (~41%). So they belong as **decision aids that safely de-escalate the low-risk majority**, never as the sole justification for detention, seclusion, or coercion.
3. **The OAS is a measurement instrument, not a predictor.** The Overt Aggression Scale (and its modified version) have the best psychometric properties for **documenting the severity of an aggressive incident after it occurs** — use it to quantify and track aggression events as an outcome metric, not to estimate future risk. Using OAS to "assess risk" is a category error.
4. **The substance layer is where the modifiable risk lives.** In psychosis, the strongest *dynamic* (changeable) violence risk factors are recent substance/alcohol/drug misuse, medication non-adherence, poor impulse control, and hostility — precisely the levers an addiction service can pull. That is why SMI-V "with substance behind it" is the right framing: the substance use is not incidental, it is the leading modifiable driver.
5. **Map the tools to the measure:** V-RISK-10 grades who needs closer monitoring; the dynamic factors (substance use, adherence, impulsivity) are the *intervention targets*; the OAS is the *event-tracking metric* that tells you whether the measure is working.

Confidence: **high** that the OAS is measurement-not-prediction and that structured tools are rule-out (not rule-in) aids; **moderate** on the specific choice of V-RISK-10 over other brief screens (the acute-psychiatry evidence favours it, but head-to-head data are limited).

## What structured violence tools can and cannot do

The single most important fact for building a defensible SMI-V measure is how these tools actually perform, because it dictates how their output may be used. The definitive meta-analysis of risk-assessment instruments — 73 samples, 24,827 people across 13 countries — found that when predicting violence, the tools produced **low-to-moderate positive predictive values (median 41%, IQR 27–60%) but high negative predictive values (median 91%, 81–95%)**. In plain terms: a low score is trustworthy (most low-risk people genuinely do not offend), but a high score is not — most people flagged high will not go on to be violent. The authors' conclusion is the governing principle for μ measure 3: the tools "identify low-risk individuals with high levels of accuracy," but "their use as sole determinants of detention, sentencing, and release is not supported." A structured tool is an aid to clinical judgment and a way to safely release the low-risk many — not a licence to detain the high-scoring few.

Within that ceiling, the individual tools have real, validated signal. The **HCR-20 (version 3)** shows strong inter-rater reliability and predictive validity for violence across follow-up windows in psychiatric and offender samples, and its structured-professional-judgment summary rating adds incremental value beyond the item scores. But it is a 20-item forensic instrument that is costly to complete at scale. The **V-RISK-10** was developed for the opposite constraint — a brief, structured screen for *acute and general psychiatry*, usable at admission and discharge, with acceptable inter-rater reliability; its family of tools reaches areas-under-the-curve around 0.76 for inpatient violence. For an SMI-V admission screen that has to be done quickly on every relevant patient, V-RISK-10's design fits; the HCR-20 is the second-line, complex-case instrument.

## What the OAS measures — and why it cannot do the SMI-V prediction job

This is the correction the SMI-V measure most needs. The Overt Aggression Scale is frequently spoken of as a "risk" tool, but the evidence is explicit about its role. A 2025 systematic review of informant-based scales for agitation and aggression concluded that the **OAS has the best psychometric properties for assessing aggression *following an incident*** — i.e. it is the instrument of choice for characterising and grading an aggressive event that has already happened, and it is (with the MOAS) the most-used such scale in substance-use populations. That is a *measurement* function — severity, type, frequency of observed aggression — not a *prediction* function.

The practical consequence: OAS scores describe the past and present, structured risk tools estimate the future, and the SMI-V measure needs both, kept in their lanes. Use the OAS to quantify aggression incidents and to track whether they fall over time (the outcome that shows the measure is working); use V-RISK-10 (or HCR-20 for complex cases) to decide monitoring intensity going forward. Feeding an OAS score into a "risk level" is the category error that would undermine the measure's credibility.

## The substance behind it: where the risk is actually modifiable

The reason addiction psychiatry belongs inside the SMI-V measure is that substance use is not a bystander variable — it is among the leading *modifiable* drivers of violence in serious mental illness. The meta-analysis of risk factors for violence in psychosis (110 studies, 45,533 individuals) found the strongest **dynamic** risk factors were recent drug misuse, recent substance and alcohol misuse, non-adherence with medication and with psychological therapy, poor impulse control, and hostile behaviour — the associations holding even when the outcome was restricted to severe violence. And a pooled analysis of seven population surveys found **paranoid ideation independently predicted violence (adjusted OR 2.26)**, not explained away by substance comorbidity, while most other psychotic experiences were violent only when co-occurring with antisocial personality disorder or substance misuse.

The strategic reading for μ measure 3: the static, historical risk factors (criminal history, past violence) set the baseline the tool captures, but the *actionable* risk sits in the dynamic factors — treat the substance use, secure medication adherence, manage acute intoxication and agitation, address paranoia. An SMI-V pathway that scores risk but does not act on the substance and adherence levers has measured the problem without touching the part it can change.

## Where this verdict inverts

- **Complex, forensic, or medico-legally weighty cases** justify the fuller HCR-20 structured professional judgment over the brief screen — the resource cost is warranted when the stakes and complexity are high.
- **Documenting or auditing aggression incidents** is exactly where the OAS/MOAS *should* be used — the "not a predictor" caution is about mis-use for risk, not a dismissal of the scale.
- **A high structured-tool score** should raise monitoring and trigger dynamic-risk intervention, but on the Fazel evidence it must not be the sole basis for coercive detention — clinical judgment and the modifiable factors carry that decision.

## What would change this verdict

The tool-performance ceiling is stable — successive meta-analyses reach the same rule-out-not-rule-in conclusion, so no near-term instrument is likely to deliver the high positive predictive value that would justify score-based detention. The genuinely useful development is the shift toward **dynamic, repeatable** risk assessment (rescoring the modifiable factors over time) rather than one-shot static prediction, which fits an SMI-V service that follows patients. Locally, the highest-value step is validation: the V-RISK-10 and OAS are validated abroad, and a Thai-language validation in the SMI-V population — reporting inter-rater reliability and predictive validity against locally-recorded aggression — would both strengthen μ measure 3 and be publishable. That, not a new tool, is where the evidence should be built.
