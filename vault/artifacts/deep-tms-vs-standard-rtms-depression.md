# Deep TMS or standard rTMS for treatment-resistant depression — is the H-coil worth choosing?
*2026-07-16 · PubMed 12 · trials 4*

> Assumed (this decision was set by default — the intent-lock picker was interrupted before it could be answered): the question is **coil choice for treatment-resistant unipolar depression in an adult TMS candidate** — Deep TMS (H1 H-coil) vs standard figure-8 rTMS — decided on outcomes, throughput, and cost. Say if you meant a different comparison (dTMS vs ECT, or a specific non-depression indication).
> Skipped: device-purchase capital economics beyond per-course cost; pediatric and bipolar populations (covered in the parent comprehensive review).

**Verdict.** For treatment-resistant depression, **do not choose Deep TMS for its depth** — the H-coil has not been shown to produce better outcomes than the cheaper, more focal figure-8 coil. Pick the platform by **access, throughput, and cost**: standard high-frequency rTMS — or **iTBS**, which delivers a comparable effect in ~3 minutes rather than ~20 — is the cost-effective default; Deep TMS is a reasonable choice **only** where it is the platform you can actually deliver (the installed device, trained staff) or where the same patient also has a cleared indication the H-coil serves better (OCD, smoking). **Confidence: moderate** — the one adequately-sized head-to-head was single-blind and enrolled less-refractory patients than a strict treatment-resistant cohort, and the definitive double-blind comparison does not read out until ~2027.

## The one head-to-head that matters, and what it actually showed

The only adequately-sized RCT to compare the two coils directly — n=228, three arms (H1 Deep TMS vs figure-8 rTMS vs pharmacotherapy alone), 20 adjunctive sessions, industry-independent — is decisive on the point that counts: on its **primary endpoint, remission (HAM-D17 ≤7 at 4 weeks), Deep TMS did not beat figure-8** (remission 60% vs 43% vs 11%; H1-vs-figure-8 OR 1.74, 95% CI 0.79–3.83, non-significant). Both coils crushed pharmacotherapy alone; neither crushed the other. The pro-depth signal shows up only in **secondary, softer outcomes** — response OR 2.33 (95% CI 1.04–5.21, p=0.040) and a larger mean symptom drop (HAM-D −59% vs −41%, p=0.048) — the kind of endpoint-fragile separation that cannot carry the weight of a purchasing decision when the pre-specified primary endpoint went the other way. The trial was **single-blind**, and its patients were general MDD on adjunctive treatment (baseline HAM-D ~17), not a strict ≥2-failure cohort — both reasons to discount the *secondary* win, neither a reason to believe the depth premium the *primary* endpoint denied. A smaller pilot (n=28) pitting figure-8 iTBS against H7 Deep TMS found nothing between them (week-6 HRSD difference −0.40, p=0.87; remission 20% in each arm), and a 2025 systematic review of every direct comparison to date (3 studies) concluded "similar efficacy and safety" while noting Deep TMS "is significantly more expensive." Even Deep-vs-Deep is a wash: an H1-vs-H7 RCT (n=169) found no difference (response 60.9% vs 64.2%).

## The indirect evidence points the same way — or worse

Network meta-analyses that rank the modalities do not put Deep TMS on top, and mostly do not separate it from sham at all. The largest and newest — 141 trials, 10,587 patients — ranks **theta-burst most efficacious** (SUCRA 87.2%) and finds **deep TMS not among the modalities significantly better than sham on response**, in a field where high-frequency-left (OR 3.34), bilateral (OR 3.93), and TBS (OR 4.47) all separated cleanly. An earlier network meta-analysis (113 trials, 6,750 patients) likewise ranked bilateral rTMS (OR 4.92), iTBS (OR 3.20), and HF-left (OR 3.17) among the ten strategies beating sham, with **deep TMS not among them**; a third (81 trials) stated plainly that deep rTMS "was not more effective than sham" while conventional HF-rTMS was (OR 3.07, 95% CI 2.24–4.21). Deep TMS unquestionably beats sham in its **own** trials — pooled response 45.3% vs 24.2%, remission 38.3% vs 14.4% across five RCTs (n=507) — but "better than sham" is not the question on the table. Measured against the comparator that matters, focal rTMS, the depth premium evaporates.

## The depth you would pay for is real physics but unproven benefit — and it costs more

The entire rationale for the H-coil is field reach: at 120% of motor threshold it induces a supra-threshold field to ~4–5 cm versus ~1.5 cm for a figure-8 coil. The physics is real; the clinical dividend is not established. Deep TMS is **more expensive per course**, and health-technology assessment finds **all rTMS modalities equivalent to one another and to ECT** on response and remission, naming **high-frequency rTMS and iTBS — not deep — as the cost-effective options** at a $50,000/QALY threshold. Throughput compounds the point: a standard high-frequency or Deep TMS session runs ~20 minutes, while iTBS delivers a comparable effect in ~3, so a clinic optimising access treats several times more patients per chair with focal iTBS than with the H-coil. Guidelines encode this neutrality — rTMS is a first-line option for depression after one failed antidepressant, but the recommendation is for the **modality**, never for the H-coil in particular.

## When Deep TMS is still the right call

The verdict inverts on **access, not depth**. Choose Deep TMS when it is the platform you can actually deliver — the device already installed, the protocol your staff is trained on — because an available H-coil course beats a focal course you cannot offer, and the two are clinically equivalent for depression. Choose it, too, when the same patient carries an indication where the H-coil is genuinely stronger or uniquely cleared — **OCD** (H7, the strongest sham-controlled dTMS signal: pivotal response 38.1% vs 11.1%) or **smoking cessation** (H4) — so one device serves more of the caseload. What does not justify choosing it is the marketing premise the evidence contradicts: that a deeper field buys a better antidepressant result.

## What would change this verdict

One thing would settle it: an adequately-powered, **double-blind** head-to-head. The best-designed such trial — accelerated figure-8 iTBS vs H1 Deep TMS theta-burst, n=108 — is registered but **not yet recruiting, primary completion ~November 2027**; a deep-vs-standard iTBS trial (n=90) reads out mid-2027. Until one of them shows a remission advantage the single existing head-to-head did not, the depth premium stays unproven. Worth noting how the evidence is shaped: the one **industry-independent** head-to-head is the conservative result (no remission superiority), while the depth-favourable pivotal and field-mapping data are **manufacturer-linked** — and that completed head-to-head (n=228) posted **no results to the registry** despite being published, so registry-only readers would miss the most decision-relevant trial that exists.
