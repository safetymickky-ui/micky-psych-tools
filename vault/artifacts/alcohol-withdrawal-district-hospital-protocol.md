# Which alcohol-withdrawal protocol for a district-hospital ward — and how to give thiamine
*2026-07-14 · PubMed 21 · trials 4 · books 0*

> Decision handed over pre-locked (no intent-lock interview): *which regimen to make the ward
> standard for inpatient alcohol withdrawal in a low-resource Thai district hospital — symptom-triggered
> benzodiazepine via CIWA-Ar, fixed/front-loaded taper, or phenobarbital-based — how to risk-stratify at
> admission, and how to dose and sequence thiamine.* The reading it beat: a general "state of the evidence
> on alcohol withdrawal" survey. Answered the protocol-choice question, because that is the one with your
> name on it.

## Verdict

**Adopt a risk-stratified, benzodiazepine-anchored protocol — not open symptom-triggered CIWA-Ar dosing as the ward default.** Concretely:

1. **Screen every admission with the PAWSS.** PAWSS **<4** → thiamine + supportive care + benzodiazepine only as-needed. PAWSS **≥4** (high risk of complicated withdrawal) → structured benzodiazepine regimen with closer observation.
2. **Make the workhorse a front-loaded long-acting benzodiazepine** — diazepam (e.g. 10–20 mg every 1–2 h until symptoms are controlled and the patient is lightly sedated, then stop and let the long half-life self-taper). Substitute **lorazepam** where there is significant liver disease, frailty, or old age. This depends on intensive monitoring only during the loading window, not on sustaining hourly CIWA-Ar scores for days.
3. **Keep phenobarbital as a protocolised second agent** for benzodiazepine-refractory or severe cases — not the first-line ward standard.
4. **Give thiamine parenterally, before any glucose:** 100–250 mg IV/IM daily for at-risk but asymptomatic patients; **500 mg IV three times daily for ~2–3 days** where Wernicke is suspected, stepping down to 250 mg daily. Never rely on oral thiamine in the acute phase. Replete magnesium alongside.
5. **Write the transfer criteria into the protocol** — established delirium tremens, withdrawal seizures, respiratory compromise, or refractoriness are at or beyond a non-ICU district ward's safe ceiling.

Confidence: **moderate-high** on the skeleton (risk-stratify → benzodiazepine-first → parenteral-thiamine-before-glucose → defined phenobarbital rescue and transfer rules). **Moderate** on the specific regimen, because the head-to-head data favouring symptom-triggered dosing come from specialist detox units and explicitly do *not* generalise to a general ward — the choice is setting-contingent, and the honest gap is that no trial has compared these regimens in a low-resource ward.

## Why not just adopt symptom-triggered dosing, the "textbook-best" regimen?

Because its advantage is real but conditional on a resource the district ward is least likely to have. In specialist detoxification units, dosing benzodiazepine only when a withdrawal score crosses threshold beats a fixed schedule on efficiency: the pooled meta-analysis of six RCTs (664 patients) found symptom-triggered therapy cut treatment duration by 60.4 hours (95% CI 39.7–81.1) and total benzodiazepine by 10.5 mg lorazepam-equivalents (95% CI 7.1–13.9), with no signal of more delirium (odds ratio 0.64, 95% CI 0.17–2.47) and one seizure in each arm. The landmark trial behind it randomised 117 patients to oxazepam and found only 39% of the symptom-triggered arm needed any drug at all versus 100% on the fixed schedule (37.5 vs 231 mg mean dose; 20 vs 62.7 h).

The trap is importing that result wholesale. The same meta-analysis concludes the evidence holds "in specialized detoxification settings of low-risk patients but the applicability of this evidence in general hospital settings is low," with insufficient evidence about mortality, seizure, or delirium in *any* setting. Symptom-triggered dosing *is* a monitoring-intensive regimen: it requires a nurse validly administering CIWA-Ar every one to four hours, and CIWA-Ar needs a communicating, cooperative patient — it degrades in delirium, sedation, language barriers, and medical comorbidity, the same review of general-hospital practice flagging these pitfalls explicitly. On a ward that cannot guarantee that scoring density, "symptom-triggered" silently collapses into either under-dosing (missed breakthroughs → seizure or delirium tremens) or de facto fixed dosing without the safety of a plan. The efficiency gain presupposes the very staffing the setting lacks.

## What the ward default should be: risk-stratify, then front-load a long-acting benzodiazepine

Benzodiazepines stay the anchor — nothing has displaced them. Against placebo they roughly quarter the seizure risk (Cochrane, relative risk 0.16, 95% CI 0.04–0.69), and the most recent network meta-analysis (41 studies, 4187 patients) found no medication or combination significantly better than benzodiazepines at reducing withdrawal severity. The question is therefore not *which drug* but *which delivery* fits a low-monitoring ward.

Front-loading exploits pharmacokinetics to do the tapering for you: repeated doses of long-acting diazepam until the patient is settled, then stop — the parent drug and its active metabolite provide a smooth, self-limiting decline without a scored taper. It concentrates the monitoring burden into a few hours rather than spreading a scored regimen across days, which is exactly the trade a thin nursing roster can afford. This is the "symptom-monitored loading" that the pragmatic reviews converge on — an initial dose set by risk factors, further dosing guided by severity — and it is what a risk-stratified benzodiazepine approach in the major guideline amounts to in practice. A fixed tapering schedule is the acceptable fallback when even the loading window cannot be watched closely; short-acting lorazepam (no active metabolites, predictable clearance) is preferred in hepatic impairment, the elderly, and the delirious.

## Who gets medicated, and who is safe on supportive care: PAWSS ≥4

The gate that makes stratification defensible is the Prediction of Alcohol Withdrawal Severity Scale — a 10-item bedside tool built from a systematic review of predictors of complicated withdrawal and prospectively validated in 403 medically ill inpatients: at a cutoff of ≥4 it identified complicated withdrawal with 93.1% sensitivity, 99.5% specificity, 93.1% positive and 99.5% negative predictive value, and near-perfect inter-rater reliability (Lin's concordance 0.963). An independent JAMA Rational Clinical Examination meta-analysis ranked PAWSS the most useful of the combination tools — likelihood ratio 174 (95% CI 43–696) at a score ≥4, and 0.07 (sensitivity 0.99) at ≤3 — while cautioning that most tools are not fully externally validated.

Crucially, PAWSS was validated in exactly this report's population — general medical and surgical inpatients — so a mixed district ward is its home turf, not an off-label use. Its one documented failure mode is the pure detox unit where almost everyone is already high-risk: in a community withdrawal-management service with 75% severe-withdrawal prevalence, a modified PAWSS fell to 44–55% sensitivity, the scale losing discrimination when the base rate is that high. The operational reading: use PAWSS to sort a general ward's mixed intake; on a dedicated detox unit, lean toward treat-and-observe rather than trusting the score to exclude risk.

## Where phenobarbital fits: reserve and adjunct, not first-line

Phenobarbital is genuinely attractive on paper for a low-resource ward — long half-life (self-tapering), a GABA action that still works when chronic drinking has downregulated the receptors benzodiazepines need, parenteral, and cheap. But the trial evidence does not support making it the front-line agent, and the discrepancy between study designs is the whole point.

Retrospective series look favourable: a phenobarbital protocol was associated with fewer respiratory complications than a benzodiazepine protocol (intubation 20% vs 51%; shorter hospital and ICU stay), and a single intravenous 10 mg/kg loading dose *added to* a lorazepam protocol cut ICU admission to 8% from 25% in a 102-patient RCT. But the better-controlled pooled evidence is null: the emergency-department meta-analysis (8 studies, 1507 patients) found no advantage of phenobarbital over benzodiazepines for ICU admission (relative risk 0.92, 95% CI 0.54–1.55), hospital admission (0.98), or adverse events (1.1); a second meta-analysis (12 studies, 1934 patients) found no difference in intubation (0.70, 95% CI 0.36–1.38), seizures, or length of stay. The systematic review of dosing found phenobarbital monotherapy "may be safe and effective" but resting on only two small RCTs amid heterogeneous dosing, and called for standardisation before wider adoption. When retrospective positivity meets meta-analytic nulls, believe the nulls: phenobarbital's edge over benzodiazepines is unproven, and its narrow therapeutic index, respiratory-depression risk, interactions, and unsettled dosing are most dangerous precisely where continuous observation is thin.

That places phenobarbital where the emergency-medicine guideline puts it — *in addition to* benzodiazepines for moderate-to-severe withdrawal being admitted, on low-to-very-low certainty — i.e. a protocolised rescue for benzodiazepine-refractory or severe cases with a single clear dosing rule, not a parallel first-line pathway a small team has to choose between under pressure.

## Thiamine: dose, route, and the one sequence rule that must not bend

The sequencing rule is non-negotiable and cheap to get right: **give parenteral thiamine before any glucose**, because a glucose load into a thiamine-depleted brain can precipitate Wernicke encephalopathy. Oral thiamine is inadequate in the acute phase — absorption is erratic and capped. Standard consensus dosing is 100–250 mg IV/IM daily for at-risk asymptomatic patients, and high-dose parenteral therapy for suspected or established Wernicke — commonly 500 mg IV three times daily for 2–3 days, stepping down to 250 mg daily, with magnesium (a required cofactor) repleted alongside.

Be honest about why the dose is high rather than proven. The only randomised dosing trial (a prophylaxis arm of 393 and a treatment arm of 127) found *no* significant benefit of 500 mg three times daily over intermediate or lower doses on cognitive or neurological outcomes — but it was underpowered, conducted in a clinically unwell, high-comorbidity, cross-cultural population, and its authors themselves fall back on patient-specific dosing plus correcting magnesium and other B vitamins. So the "give high-dose parenteral thiamine early, empirically, before glucose, and don't wait for diagnostic confirmation" rule does not rest on demonstrated dose superiority. It rests on the asymmetry a safety protocol is built for: under-treatment risks irreversible Korsakoff amnesia, over-treatment is nearly harmless (anaphylaxis to parenteral thiamine is rare and not a reason to withhold it). That asymmetry, not a dose-response curve, is the correct foundation for the protocol line.

## Where this verdict inverts

- **If the ward can genuinely sustain reliable hourly-to-2-hourly CIWA-Ar scoring by trained nurses on cooperative patients**, open symptom-triggered dosing becomes reasonable and will save benzodiazepine and bed-days. The recommendation against it is about the setting's monitoring capacity, not a claim that the regimen is inferior.
- **On a pure detox unit** (as opposed to a mixed medical ward) where nearly all admissions are high-risk, PAWSS loses its discriminating value; default toward treat-and-observe rather than using the score to send patients home on supportive care alone.
- **Complicated withdrawal — established delirium tremens, seizures, respiratory compromise, refractoriness** — sits at or past a non-ICU district ward's safe ceiling; untreated severe withdrawal carries high mortality. Here the single most valuable line in the protocol is not a drug dose but the threshold to escalate care or transfer.

## What would change this verdict

Very little in the near term, and that is itself a finding. Of 14 ongoing interventional alcohol-withdrawal trials on ClinicalTrials.gov, **none tests the regimen-choice or thiamine-dosing questions** — they are all adjuncts or novel agents (prazosin, NCT04793685, n=150, readout 2026; cannabidiol, NCT07148843; ketone supplementation, NCT06559995; auricular and vagal neurostimulation; bright-light therapy). The single phenobarbital RCT behind the adjunct signal is published, not a hidden negative (NCT01184417). The core management questions are not being re-litigated, so no pending trial is poised to overturn a benzodiazepine-anchored, risk-stratified protocol; "wait for better evidence" is not a reason to defer standardisation.

The real gap is not a missing drug but a missing trial: no RCT has compared symptom-triggered, front-loaded, and phenobarbital regimens *in a low-resource general ward*. Until one exists, the protocol should be built on the pragmatic logic above rather than on specialist-unit efficiency data imported without adjustment — and a district hospital that audited its own withdrawal outcomes against this protocol would be generating exactly the evidence the field is missing.
