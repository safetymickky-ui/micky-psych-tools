# Retention interventions in outpatient addiction treatment: what works, how to define retention, and how to defend a 31%→80% jump
*2026-07-14 · PubMed 11 · trials 0 · books 0*

> Decision handed over pre-locked (no intent-lock interview): *which retention interventions (flexible
> scheduling, phone/LINE check-in, assertive outreach) have measured effects, and how is retention standardly
> defined — so the service's 30.9%→79.8% retention improvement can be defended as an intervention effect and a
> retention KPI can be specified.* The reading it beat: a general essay on why people drop out of treatment.
> Answered the two operational questions — which levers to invest in, and how to measure and defend the number
> — because that is what the surveyor recommendation and the R2R paper both turn on.

## Verdict

**The retention levers with real evidence, in order, are contingency management (for attendance), case management / assertive outreach, and practical/structural supports (flexible scheduling, phones, transport) — motivational interviewing and generic added counselling are NOT reliable retention tools. Define retention by one explicit metric and hold it constant. Treat the 30.9%→79.8% change as a large, plausibly-real service-level improvement that a pre/post design can only associate — not cleanly attribute — to the intervention bundle.** Concretely:

1. **Best-evidenced lever: contingency management targeting attendance.** CM directly reduces dropout (Cochrane: RR 0.82) and, combined with a community-reinforcement approach, cut dropout dramatically in the definitive network meta-analysis (OR 3.63). Attendance-targeted CM has a smaller effect than abstinence-targeted CM but is cheaper — it is the natural entry build (see report #6).
2. **Case management / assertive outreach** has genuine, if modest, support — a review of reviews found case management significantly better than usual care for engagement and substance-use outcomes in the hardest-to-retain (homeless, disadvantaged) populations.
3. **Practical and structural supports** — flexible scheduling, providing a phone (or a LINE channel), transport, and removing access barriers — help *reach and keep* exactly the patients who drop out, without harming outcomes; supplying phones let a study retain a far more disadvantaged, homeless, unemployed cohort at rates equal to phone-owners.
4. **Do not rely on motivational interviewing for retention.** MI reduces substance use versus *no* treatment but is no better than treatment-as-usual, and the evidence for its effect on *retention specifically* is insufficient; as a stand-alone it under-performs. Likewise, adding generic psychosocial sessions to medication did not improve retention in low-risk-of-bias trials.
5. **Define retention once, explicitly.** There is no single field standard — pick one (recommended: still-in-treatment at fixed windows, e.g. ≥3 and ≥6 months, plus % of a defined intake completing), specify the denominator and the allowable gap, and never change it between periods you compare.

Confidence: **moderate** — the intervention evidence is genuinely mixed and thinner than for the pharmacotherapy questions, but the ranking, the definition guidance, and the interpretation framework are solid.

## Which retention interventions actually have measured effects

The honest headline is that the retention-intervention literature is heterogeneous and weaker than clinicians assume, and the interventions cluster into "real effect," "reaches/keeps hard cases," and "over-sold."

**Contingency management is the strongest lever**, because retention *is* a behaviour CM can reinforce directly. The Cochrane review of psychosocial treatments for stimulant use disorder found CM reduced dropout (RR 0.82, high certainty), and the network meta-analysis of cocaine/amphetamine psychosocial interventions found CM plus community reinforcement produced the largest reduction in dropout of any approach (OR 3.63 versus usual care). The trade-off is target: abstinence-targeted CM has a larger effect (voucher meta-analysis r = 0.32) than attendance-targeted CM (r = 0.15), but attendance CM is cheaper and is the direct retention tool — which is why it doubles as the entry build for the CM programme in report #6.

**Case management and assertive outreach** are the next tier, with evidence concentrated where retention is hardest. A systematic review of reviews in homeless people who use drugs found **case management was significantly better than treatment as usual** at reducing substance use and supporting engagement, while Housing First improved housing but not substance use. This is the evidence base under "assertive outreach": active, wrap-around contact that follows the patient rather than waiting for them to return.

**Practical and structural supports** do not have large trial effect sizes but solve the mechanical causes of dropout. Providing pay-by-month phones let a trial recruit and retain a markedly more disadvantaged cohort (86.7% recently homeless, mostly unemployed) at follow-up rates statistically equal to phone-owners (83% vs 88%) — evidence that removing a structural barrier keeps the people who otherwise vanish. Flexible scheduling, transport, and a low-friction check-in channel (phone/LINE) act on the same mechanism.

**Motivational interviewing is the over-sold one for this purpose.** The Cochrane MI review (59 studies, 13,342 participants) found MI reduced substance use versus no treatment (SMD 0.79 immediately post, decaying to non-significant long-term) but found **no significant advantage over treatment-as-usual** and had too little data to conclude anything about retention; a homeless-population review concluded MI should not be used as a stand-alone substance intervention. And a systematic review of adjuncts to buprenorphine found that adding psychosocial interventions did not improve retention or substance-use outcomes in the low-bias trials. The lesson for a KPI-driven service: budget the retention effort on CM-for-attendance, outreach/case management, and removing structural barriers — not on generic counselling relabelled as engagement.

## How retention is defined — pick one and hold it

The reason retention numbers are hard to compare (and easy to inflate) is that "retention" has at least four operational definitions in routine use, each giving a different number from the same clinic:

- **Still in treatment at a fixed timepoint** — the opioid-agonist-treatment standard, reported at 1, 3, 6, 12, and 24 months (the Lancet Psychiatry synthesis pooled retention this way, finding e.g. methadone retained more patients than buprenorphine at 6 months, RR 0.76). This is the cleanest KPI: unambiguous numerator and denominator.
- **Continuous enrolment until a gap of N days** — e.g. retained until a 45-day gap in treatment claims. Sensitive to how long a gap is "allowed."
- **Treatment completion = a planned discharge** — a real-world US state dataset (n = 7,351) using this definition found a 58.1% completion rate, lower for opioid/other-drug use and co-occurring mental illness. Depends entirely on how "planned" is coded.
- **Proportion of scheduled sessions/visits attended**, or **total days/episodes retained** — continuous measures, useful for dose-response but awkward as a headline KPI.

For the unit's KPI, the recommendation is the first: **the proportion of a defined intake cohort still in active treatment at ≥3 months and ≥6 months**, with the denominator (who counts as "started"), the treatment-gap tolerance, and the substance mix specified up front — and *never altered* between the periods being compared. Retention is a strong indicator precisely because it predicts post-treatment outcomes, but only if it is measured the same way each time.

## How to defend the 30.9%→79.8% jump

This is the crux for both the surveyor and the R2R paper, and it must be argued honestly. A move from 30.9% to 79.8% is a **large** change — a 2.6-fold increase, far bigger than the typical single retention-intervention effect (case management, outreach, reminders individually produce modest shifts) — which is consistent with a genuine *bundle* effect but also a size that demands scrutiny before it is called an intervention effect.

The honest position: **an uncontrolled pre/post comparison supports association, not clean causal attribution.** The specific threats to name, and neutralise, are:

- **Regression to the mean** — a 30.9% baseline is a low starting point, and low extremes tend to move toward the average on remeasurement regardless of any intervention.
- **A change in the retention definition** between periods — the single most common way a retention number "improves"; if the denominator or the allowed gap shifted, part of the jump is definitional, not real. Prove the definition was constant.
- **Cohort/case-mix shift** — if the later cohort had fewer opioid users, less comorbidity, or more self-referrals (all predictors of better retention), the case-mix moved, not just the care. Adjust for it.
- **Secular trend and co-interventions** — other simultaneous service changes, staffing, or policy could carry part of the effect.

To convert the number from a claim into publishable evidence: hold the retention definition fixed across periods; adjust for case-mix (baseline substance, comorbidity, referral source, the documented dropout predictors — younger age, more years of use, higher craving); and strengthen the design with a **contemporaneous comparison group or an interrupted-time-series** rather than a two-point before/after. State explicitly which bundle components were active (flexible scheduling, phone/LINE check-in, outreach) and, ideally, whether the improvement tracks their roll-out in time. Framed that way, the 30.9%→79.8% change is defensible as a real service-level improvement and a strong R2R candidate — while being upfront that a single-arm pre/post design is associative, which is exactly the honesty a surveyor and a reviewer will reward.

## Where this verdict inverts

- **If the clinic can only fund one thing**, attendance-targeted CM is the highest-yield retention build — it is the same infrastructure as report #6.
- **For a predominantly homeless/highly-disadvantaged caseload**, case management and structural supports move above CM in priority — they solve the access barriers that CM incentives cannot.
- **If retention is already high and the goal is outcomes**, the lever shifts from retention interventions to the treatment content (CM for abstinence, pharmacotherapy) — retention is necessary, not sufficient.

## What would change this verdict

The retention-intervention evidence base is the thin part of this portfolio, and better trials would sharpen the ranking — particularly head-to-head tests of outreach and digital check-in (LINE/text) against attendance CM, and standardised retention definitions to make studies comparable (the field's own reviews repeatedly call for this). The most directly relevant ongoing registry activity is the CM-implementation trials noted in report #6 (including Southeast-Asian and community-delivered programmes), several of which report retention as a primary or secondary outcome. A local evaluation that fixed the retention definition and added a comparison arm would not only defend the unit's number but contribute to exactly the evidence gap the literature names.
