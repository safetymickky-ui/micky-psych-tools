# Suicide risk in stimulant users: what an 8Q-positive pathway must contain in a non-psychiatric hospital
*2026-07-14 · PubMed 8 · trials 0 · books 0*

> Decision handed over pre-locked (no intent-lock interview): *given the epidemiology of suicidal ideation in
> the post-stimulant crash phase, what should the pathway for an 8Q-positive stimulant user contain in a
> non-psychiatric (district) hospital.* The reading it beat: a review of the neurobiology of stimulant-related
> suicidality. Answered the pathway-design question — the pathway you will author — because a screen without a
> response is the failure this is meant to prevent.

## Verdict

**The 8Q-positive pathway's active ingredient is not the screen — it is what happens after it: stratify → make the patient immediately safe → deliver a brief structured intervention → and, above all, sustain follow-up contact after discharge. A pathway that documents an 8Q score and discharges without follow-up is the failure mode, not the safe default.** Concretely:

1. **Stratify the 8Q-positive patient** into tiers (ideation only / ideation with plan / recent attempt), then layer the acute modifiers a district ED can see: current intoxication or the peak-crash dysphoric window, agitation, access to lethal means, and any prior attempt.
2. **Make the patient safe now:** means restriction (actively remove access to the method), close or one-to-one observation for the high tier, treat agitation and intoxication first, and **do not discharge a patient during acute intoxication or the peak of the post-use crash** — the risk assessment made in that state does not hold.
3. **Deliver a brief structured intervention** — early therapeutic engagement plus a **collaborative safety plan** (Stanley–Brown style). But know its limit: safety planning *alone* is weak; it works only as part of a bundle.
4. **Sustain follow-up contact — the load-bearing component:** a crisis card with a hotline, and scheduled brief-contact follow-up (caring phone/LINE recontact at defined intervals, caring messages/postcards) after discharge. This is what actually reduces repeat attempts, and it maps directly onto Thailand's existing DMH follow-up structures.
5. **Set explicit transfer/referral criteria:** recent serious attempt, ongoing intent, or co-occurring psychosis → transfer to psychiatric care; every 8Q-positive stimulant user → active linkage to addiction treatment, because the stimulant use is the engine of the risk.

Confidence: **moderate-high** on the skeleton (stratify → immediate safety → brief intervention → sustained follow-up → linkage). The individual brief-contact effect sizes are modest and heterogeneous, which is exactly why the pathway relies on the *bundle*, not any single element.

## Why the pathway is worth building: the post-crash risk is real and time-located

Stimulant users are a genuinely elevated-risk suicide population, and the risk clusters in the phase when they present to a general hospital. A prospective cohort found injection methamphetamine use independently raised the risk of a suicide attempt by 80% (adjusted hazard ratio 1.80). A large population cohort found every category of drug use disorder significantly increased self-harm or suicide, with methamphetamine among the elevated group. Methamphetamine use is associated with suicidal ideation in community samples (odds ratio ~2.6), and among patients who have already self-injured, methamphetamine involvement was associated with *greater* injury severity — the attempts are more medically serious, not less.

Mechanistically and temporally, the danger concentrates in the **post-use "crash"** — the dysphoria, depression, hopelessness, and impulsivity that follow heavy stimulant use and withdrawal. That is precisely the state in which an 8Q-positive stimulant user arrives at a district ED. The pathway therefore has a specific window to act in, and a specific trap to avoid: discharging the patient while still intoxicated or at the dysphoric nadir, when both the risk and the unreliability of the assessment are highest.

## What the pathway must contain — and the evidence for each part

The components are ordered by when they act, and each is chosen because the evidence says the *combination* — not any single piece — is what reduces attempts.

**Immediate safety comes first and is the least evidence-controversial:** means restriction and observation are foundational suicide-prevention measures, and in a stimulant crash they buy time for the acute dysphoria to lift. Treat intoxication and agitation before making any disposition decision.

**A brief structured intervention — but not safety planning as a standalone.** This is the pivotal design point. A systematic review of brief psychological interventions for suicidal presentations found that interventions combining **early therapeutic engagement, a safety plan, and follow-up contact sustained for at least 12 months** reduced suicide attempts and suicides. But the meta-analysis of safety planning as a *standalone* intervention in adolescents found **no significant effect** on ideation, behaviour, attempts, or re-presentation. The lesson for the pathway: the safety plan is necessary but not sufficient — it must be wrapped in engagement and, critically, follow-up. A safety plan handed over at discharge with nothing after it is documentation, not prevention.

**Sustained follow-up contact is the component that carries the effect.** Brief-contact interventions — postcards/caring letters, text or phone check-ins, crisis cards offering a hotline — reduce repeated self-harm and suicide attempts in the post-discharge period, and they are cheap and easy to implement. The most developed model, the French VigilanS/ALGOS algorithm, operationalises exactly this: a crisis card for every patient, a structured phone recontact between roughly day 10 and 21 to reassess, and a series of personalised postcards if contact fails — a "stay-in-contact" case-management dimension built on Motto's original caring-letters work. This is the template the 8Q-positive pathway should copy, because it is both evidence-based and structurally compatible with the DMH's existing suicide-surveillance follow-up.

**Linkage to addiction treatment closes the loop**, because the modifiable driver is the stimulant use itself — the pathway that resolves the acute crisis but does not connect the patient to treatment has treated the symptom and left the cause.

## Where this verdict inverts

- **Recent medically serious attempt, active intent, or co-occurring psychosis:** admit/transfer to psychiatric care rather than discharge-with-follow-up — the outpatient brief-contact model is for the lower and middle tiers, not the acutely high-risk.
- **Pure acute intoxication without established ideation once sober:** the pathway's disposition may be observation until the crash resolves and re-screening, not a full suicide-prevention bundle — but the re-screen after sobriety is mandatory, not optional.
- **No means restriction possible (patient declines, agitated, absconding risk):** that itself escalates the tier and the case for admission.

## What would change this verdict

The direction of travel strengthens rather than overturns this pathway: the field is actively scaling the Safety Planning Intervention *with follow-up* (SPI+) into emergency settings, including via telehealth consultation models, precisely because the "+follow-up" component is what makes it work — reinforcing the design above. What is genuinely under-studied is the **stimulant-specific** pathway: most brief-intervention evidence comes from mixed self-harm ED populations, not stimulant-crash presentations specifically, and there is no dedicated methamphetamine-crash suicide-pathway trial. A district hospital that implemented this pathway and tracked 8Q-positive stimulant users' re-presentation and follow-up-contact completion would be generating exactly that missing evidence — and, given the DMH surveillance infrastructure, is well placed to.
