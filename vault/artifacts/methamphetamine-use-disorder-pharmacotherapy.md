# Methamphetamine use disorder pharmacotherapy: what is usable off-label now, and what to wait for

## Verdict

**Nothing is approved, effects are modest, and contingency management remains the backbone — but two off-label options are defensible to *offer* a moderate-to-severe patient who understands the modest expected benefit: the ADAPT-2 combination, and mirtazapine.** Concretely:

1. **Most defensible off-label "try now": injectable naltrexone + extended-release bupropion (the ADAPT-2 regimen)** — the only adequately-powered positive RCT (NEJM). It is justifiable for a motivated adult with moderate-to-severe methamphetamine use disorder, framed as a **modest** improvement in reducing use, not a cure.
2. **Reasonable adjunct: mirtazapine 30 mg at night** — small but replicated reduction in use, and it earns its place specifically where **insomnia, depression, or high-risk sexual behaviour** coexist; cheap, tolerable, and its benefits persisted despite poor adherence.
3. **Marginal: bupropion alone** — a small effect, best in lower-frequency users on a longer course; not a first reach.
4. **Watch, don't routinely start: agonist-type therapy (dexamfetamine, methylphenidate)** — the most *consistent* positive signal in the reviews, but the evidence is still limited and the regulatory/diversion barriers to prescribing a stimulant for a stimulant use disorder make it unsuitable as routine practice in a Thai public clinic now.
5. **The backbone is not a drug.** Contingency management is the strongest single intervention for this disorder; pharmacotherapy is adjunctive to it, never a substitute — which is why report #6 (contingency management) is the higher-yield service investment.

Verdict on off-label justifiability vs wait: **for the individual patient**, an informed off-label trial of the ADAPT-2 combination or mirtazapine (layered on psychosocial treatment) is defensible; **for the service**, invest in contingency management now and *watch the frontier* — GLP-1 agonists, ketamine, and better-delivered ADAPT-2 are the live near-term evidence.

Confidence: **high** that no pharmacotherapy is approved and CM is the backbone; **moderate** on ADAPT-2 as the best off-label try-now; **moderate** on mirtazapine; **moderate-low** on agonist therapy (promising signal, not yet deployable here).

## What "usable now" actually means, agent by agent

The honest starting point is that **no medication is approved anywhere for methamphetamine use disorder**, and the systematic reviews are blunt about it: across 43 trials, no pharmacotherapy yielded convincing results, most studies were underpowered with high dropout, and the least ineffective signals came from agonist therapy, naltrexone, and topiramate, with weaker signals for bupropion and mirtazapine. Against that backdrop, "usable now" means *defensible to offer off-label with informed expectations*, and only a few agents clear even that bar.

**The ADAPT-2 combination is the one adequately-powered positive result.** In a 403-participant NEJM trial, extended-release injectable naltrexone (380 mg every 3 weeks) plus oral extended-release bupropion (450 mg/day) produced a response — at least three of four methamphetamine-negative urines at the end of a stage — in a weighted **13.6% versus 2.5% on placebo, a treatment effect of 11.1 percentage points (P<0.001)**, roughly an NNT of 9. That effect is real and replicated across sexes in a secondary analysis, but it must be sold honestly: the *response* is reduced use, not abstinence; the absolute rate is low; and it requires the injectable plus high-dose bupropion. It is worth offering to a moderate-to-severe patient who wants to try, and worth no overselling.

**Mirtazapine is the reasonable adjunct.** Two randomised trials in men and transgender women who have sex with men found fewer methamphetamine-positive urines — relative risk **0.57** in the first (NNT 3.1 in that sample) and **0.67 at 12 weeks, 0.73 at 36 weeks** in the replication, with benefits persisting after treatment stopped *despite* medication adherence under 40% — plus reductions in sexual-risk behaviour, depression, and insomnia. The meta-analysis reins the enthusiasm in appropriately: pooled, mirtazapine gives a **small** reduction in use (RR 0.81, moderate certainty) and does not improve retention or depression scores. So mirtazapine is a low-cost, well-tolerated adjunct that is most attractive exactly where its side-effect profile is a bonus — the patient who also cannot sleep, is depressed, or is at behavioural risk.

**Bupropion alone is marginal.** The meta-analysis of eight RCTs (1,239 participants) found a small reduction in amphetamine-type-stimulant use (RR 0.90) and craving (SMD −0.38), rated very-low-quality, and larger with longer treatment and in lower-frequency users. It is a weak monotherapy; its main current role is as the bupropion half of the ADAPT-2 combination.

**Agonist-type therapy is the frontier's most consistent signal but not yet a clinic tool.** Dexamfetamine and methylphenidate show the most reproducible reductions in use across reviews, but the trials remain small and the practice of prescribing a controlled stimulant for stimulant use disorder carries diversion, regulatory, and monitoring burdens that place it beyond routine use in a Thai public setting today. It belongs in the "watch and possibly pilot under strict governance" column, not the standing formulary.

## Why the drug is the adjunct, not the treatment

The intervention with the strongest evidence for this disorder is behavioural. The Cochrane review of psychosocial treatments for stimulant use disorder (64 RCTs, 8,241 participants) found that psychosocial treatment reduces dropout (RR 0.82, high certainty) and probably increases continuous abstinence at end of treatment (RR 1.89), with **contingency management the most-studied and most promising** approach. Set against pharmacotherapy's modest and inconsistent effects, the implication for a service is clear: the highest-yield investment is a contingency-management programme (report #6), with medication layered on for individual patients who want more. A protocol that led with a drug and treated CM as optional would have the evidence backwards.

## Where this verdict inverts

- **Comorbid opioid use disorder on methadone/buprenorphine:** the naltrexone component of ADAPT-2 is problematic (opioid antagonism) — mirtazapine or bupropion adjuncts, plus CM, fit better; several ongoing trials specifically target this co-occurring population.
- **Prominent depression or insomnia:** mirtazapine moves up as the single agent serving two indications.
- **A research-governance pathway exists:** agonist therapy (dexamfetamine/methylphenidate) becomes a legitimate option under structured monitoring, closer to its evidence.
- **Psychosis is present:** that is a different decision — see the methamphetamine-associated-psychosis consult protocol; antipsychotics treat the psychosis, not the use.

## What would change this verdict — the live frontier

Unlike the alcohol formulary, this field is genuinely moving, and the honest thing to tell the meeting is *where*. The registry shows the near-term frontier concentrated in three places: **GLP-1 agonists** — semaglutide is in at least two Phase 2 trials for methamphetamine use disorder (NCT07509112 "SHIFT"; NCT07204249, n=162), reading out **2027–2030**; **ketamine** — the NIDA CTN-0132 randomised trial (NCT06496750, n=120) reads out **2026**; and **better-delivered ADAPT-2** — an implantable naltrexone-plus-bupropion pellet is in early testing. Alongside these, the ongoing behavioural pipeline is dominated by **contingency-management** implementation trials (including in low- and middle-income and Southeast Asian settings), reinforcing that CM, not a molecule, is where the deployable gains are today. So the frontier statement is: *nothing new is prescribable yet; the ADAPT-2 combination and mirtazapine are the defensible off-label options now; GLP-1 agonists and ketamine are the trials to watch over the next two to four years.*
