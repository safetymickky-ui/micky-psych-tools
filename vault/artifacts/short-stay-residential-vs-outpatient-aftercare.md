# Short-stay residential vs outpatient: is the bed-stay the treatment, or is the aftercare?
*2026-07-14 · PubMed 7 · trials 0 · books 0*

> Decision handed over pre-locked (no intent-lock interview): *does brief (≈14-day) inpatient rehabilitation
> outperform outpatient care once aftercare is controlled for — so the Home ward + 10-visit follow-up can be
> reframed against what the evidence actually credits.* The reading it beat: a cost comparison of bed-days. The
> literature mostly says the aftercare is the active ingredient — this report tests that and turns it into a
> service reframe.

## Verdict

**No — brief inpatient rehabilitation does not reliably outperform outpatient care for most patients, and the durable active ingredient is continuing care, not the bed-days. Reframe the 14-day admission as a stabilisation-and-engagement phase and the 10-visit follow-up as the evidence-based core — then protect the follow-up *dose*, because that is what the evidence rewards.** Concretely:

1. **Setting is not the active ingredient for most patients.** When patients are randomised or matched by level-of-care criteria, those assigned to inpatient do **not** have significantly better outcomes than intensive outpatient — even among patients who met inpatient criteria. Residential care is not magic: completion is often low and unplanned departure high.
2. **Continuing care is where the durable effect lives — but only when it is delivered right.** Pooled continuing-care effects are *small*, and they become **consistently positive only when the continuing care is longer in duration and makes active efforts to keep patients engaged**. Higher-relapse-risk patients benefit more.
3. **The reframe:** treat the 14 days as **stabilisation + engagement + treatment initiation** (genuinely appropriate for higher-severity or unstable patients), and treat the **10-visit follow-up as the treatment**. The service's structure is right *if* the emphasis and resources sit on the follow-up, not the bed.
4. **Make the follow-up "active," not passive.** The evidence rewards assertive, sustained contact (phone/LINE check-in, outreach — links to report #7), targeted more heavily at higher-risk patients, ideally with a contingency-management component (report #6) and adaptive step-up when a patient destabilises. A short, passive, appointment-only follow-up is the failure mode.
5. **Keep residential for whom it is actually indicated:** medically/psychiatrically unstable patients, those in unsafe or using environments, or those who have failed outpatient care — the "outpatient is equivalent" finding is for the general population with adequate support.

Confidence: **high** that continuing care is the active ingredient and that setting is not superior for most; **moderate** on the exact setting-equivalence, because the head-to-head trials are older and one small Thai study points the other way (see boundary).

## Setting: the bed-stay is not where the effect is

The cleanest test of "does inpatient beat outpatient" is to match patients to a level of care and see whether the match pays off. It largely does not. A randomised evaluation of the ASAM placement criteria found that alcohol- and cocaine-dependent patients **correctly matched to inpatient care did not have significantly better outcomes than those treated in intensive outpatient** — and, in a more focused analysis, alcohol patients who *met* the ASAM criteria for inpatient care did no better in that setting than in intensive outpatient (cocaine patients showed only marginal, non-significant short-term benefit). The authors' reading is that the psychosocial criteria for inpatient placement are "probably too broad," with the important caveat that the sample were male veterans without acute medical or psychotic needs — i.e. exactly the population for whom outpatient *should* be adequate.

The corollary is that residential care is neither uniquely powerful nor self-sufficient. A residential rehabilitation cohort showed **low programme completion and high rates of unplanned departure and eviction** — a reminder that putting someone in a bed for a fixed period does not, by itself, produce recovery. The bed buys containment, stabilisation, and an engagement opportunity; it does not buy a durable outcome.

## Continuing care: the active ingredient, with conditions

If the setting is not the lever, the follow-up is — and the continuing-care literature is unusually clear about *what kind* of follow-up works. The authoritative reviews conclude that continuing care for substance use disorder has **supported but small pooled effects**, which become **consistently positive when the continuing care is of longer duration and makes more active efforts to keep patients engaged**, and that **higher-relapse-risk patients benefit to a greater degree**. The earlier synthesis reached the same three conclusions — longer planned duration, active delivery, and (a fourth) adaptive algorithms that step treatment up or down as the patient's status changes — and telephone-based continuing care after residential treatment showed that **greater participation yielded better outcomes**, with the first months post-discharge the decisive window.

Translated to the Home ward: the 10-visit follow-up is not an add-on to the "real" treatment in the ward — it *is* the evidence-based treatment, and its effectiveness depends on three tunable parameters the service controls. **Duration:** ten visits is a reasonable dose only if they are spread to cover the high-risk months, not front-loaded and finished. **Activity:** the visits must be assertive contact (reach out, follow up on no-shows, phone/LINE between visits) rather than passive appointments the patient must remember to attend. **Targeting:** the higher-risk patients — the ones the reviews say benefit most — should get the heavier follow-up dose, with the option to step care up when someone destabilises. Add a contingency-management element and the follow-up becomes stronger still.

## Where this verdict inverts

- **Medically or psychiatrically unstable patients, unsafe/using home environments, or outpatient failures** genuinely need the residential structure — the equivalence finding is explicitly for patients without those needs, and level-of-care matching still applies at the extremes.
- **A contrary local data point exists:** a small, non-blinded Thai DDTC study found an inpatient model (FAST) *outperformed* an outpatient model (Matrix) — but it confounds setting with treatment model and is underpowered, so it does not overturn the broader equivalence finding; it does argue for measuring your own setting-stratified outcomes rather than assuming.
- **If the follow-up cannot be delivered actively** (no outreach capacity, passive appointments only), the continuing-care benefit largely evaporates — in which case the honest move is to fix the follow-up before defending the bed-days.

## What would change this verdict

The setting-comparison trials are dated, and a modern RCT in a low-resource general-hospital context (rather than US veterans) would sharpen the equivalence claim — notably because the one local study points the other way. But the continuing-care conclusion is robust and improving: the active, adaptive, technology-augmented continuing-care models (telephone/mHealth check-in, incentives, step-up algorithms) are exactly where the evidence is strengthening, and they are cheap and deployable. The highest-value local project is therefore not to test the 14-day bed again but to **measure and strengthen the follow-up** — track 6- and 12-month abstinence by follow-up-dose received, and demonstrate that the Home ward's durable results come from the 10 visits, not the 14 nights. That reframes the service around its evidence-based core and produces a publishable service-evaluation in the process.
