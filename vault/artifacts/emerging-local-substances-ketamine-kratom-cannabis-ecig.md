# Emerging local substances: ketamine, kratom + cannabis, and adolescent e-cigarettes
*2026-07-14 · PubMed 6 · trials 0 · books 0*

> Decision handed over pre-locked (no intent-lock interview): *the three emerging-substance trends the situation
> analysis itself names — ketamine use disorder (including uropathy), kratom + cannabis post-legalization clinical
> epidemiology in Thailand, and adolescent e-cigarette cessation / school-based intervention — each as a small
> run.* The reading it beat: a general new-psychoactive-substances survey. Answered the three named trends, each
> with its own service response.

## Verdict

**Three distinct watch-items, each needing a different response: for ketamine, screen chronic users for uropathy and treat with abstinence + coordinated urology–addiction care; for kratom and cannabis, the clinical risk is in adulterated kratom "cocktails" and in cannabis's perception–evidence gap and psychosis risk, not in traditional low-dose use; for adolescent vaping, deploy both school-based prevention and a low-cost digital cessation offer — behavioural/digital, not adult pharmacotherapy.** Confidence: moderate — the clinical directions are clear, but several rest on small or observational studies.

## A. Ketamine use disorder — the bladder is the tell

**Verdict: any chronic ketamine user with lower-urinary-tract symptoms has ketamine-induced uropathy until proven otherwise; the only disease-modifying treatment is abstinence, and it must be managed jointly by urology and addiction — do not treat the bladder without treating the use.**

The signature harm of chronic frequent ketamine use is **ketamine-induced ulcerative/haemorrhagic cystitis** — a painful, potentially irreversible bladder disease (frequency, urgency, dysuria, haematuria, progressing in severe cases to a contracted, low-capacity bladder). It is tightly associated with heavy, daily use, and is frequently missed because patients do not volunteer ketamine use. Alongside it, chronic use produces **neurocognitive impairment** — most robustly in working and episodic memory — and **psychological dependence**, with many frequent users reporting they have tried but failed to stop. There are also reports of **gastrointestinal/hepatobiliary toxicity** (abdominal pain, abnormal liver function — "ketamine cholangiopathy"). Management is largely supportive and hinges on **abstinence**: early cessation can reverse bladder damage, but late disease may require urological surgery, so the urological and addiction interventions must be coordinated rather than run in parallel. The service action: ask about ketamine use in any young patient with unexplained LUTS, and build a joint urology–addiction pathway.

## B. Kratom and cannabis post-legalization — where the real risk sits

**Verdict: traditional low-dose use of both is comparatively low-risk; the clinical concern is adulterated kratom "cocktails" in youth and cannabis's post-legalization perception–evidence gap with its psychosis risk. Monitor both, and screen for the psychosis/harm pathways rather than pathologising traditional use.**

**Kratom** has a long, culturally-embedded, comparatively low-risk history of rural use in Thailand and Southeast Asia (for fatigue, social use, and folk medicine), and self-report studies suggest prolonged traditional use does not typically cause serious health harm or impair social functioning. Two newer trends are the concern: kratom is increasingly used to **self-manage opioid withdrawal** in rural settings, and — the higher-risk pattern — **adulterated kratom "cocktails"** (the "4×100"-type mixtures) are consumed by younger urban users for euphoria, i.e. a polydrug pattern, not traditional use. Legal sanctions have run ahead of good science, so the evidence base is thin. The service reading: distinguish traditional single-substance kratom use from adulterated-cocktail polydrug use — the latter is the clinical target.

**Cannabis** post-legalization data are directly Thai and revealing. A respondent-driven survey of medical-cannabis users in the first year after Thailand's 2019 legalization (n=485) found most used an oral crude-oil extract for cancer, neuropsychiatric symptoms, and pain — **indications largely not supported by evidence** — sourced mostly from **illegal/underground suppliers**, with **low awareness of harms** (fewer than half recognised psychosis, memory impairment, panic, or palpitations as possible effects). The perception–evidence gap is the finding: users believe cannabis treats conditions it does not, and underestimate its harms. The service should expect cannabis-related presentations (psychosis, hyperemesis, unregulated-product harms), counter the perception gap in counselling, and screen for the psychosis pathway (which connects to the methamphetamine-psychosis and violence work — reports #2 and #11).

## C. Adolescent e-cigarettes — two evidence-based tools, build both

**Verdict: for adolescents who already vape, a text-message cessation program works; for prevention, school-based programs are the evidence base. Build both — and do not import adult cessation pharmacotherapy, which is not established in adolescents.**

Adolescent vaping now has, for the first time, an **empirically-tested cessation intervention**: a randomised trial of an automated, interactive **text-message program** delivering coping-skills and social support to 13–17-year-old vapers who wanted to quit found 30-day point-prevalence abstinence of **37.8% versus 28.0%** in assessment-only controls (relative risk 1.35, 95% CI 1.17–1.57), with **no evidence of transition to combustible tobacco**. That is a low-cost, scalable model directly transferable to a text/LINE-based adolescent cessation offer. On the prevention side, **school-based programs** are the established setting, and co-designed digital school interventions (e.g. OurFutures Vaping) are in rigorous trials. The important boundary: the adult cessation pharmacotherapy that report #4 recommends (varenicline, NRT, cytisine) is **not established in adolescents** — the adolescent evidence base is behavioural and digital. The service-matched response is therefore a two-part build: school-based prevention plus a digital cessation offer for those already vaping.

## Where these verdicts invert

- **Ketamine:** occasional/low-frequency use carries much lower uropathy risk than daily heavy use — the screening priority is the frequent user; and severe, established bladder disease is a urological emergency that outruns the addiction pathway.
- **Kratom/cannabis:** a genuinely traditional, low-dose, single-substance user does not need pathologising — over-medicalising traditional use is its own error; the target is the adulterated-cocktail and the harm-unaware cannabis user.
- **Adolescent vaping:** a nicotine-dependent adolescent with heavy use may need more than a text program — but the digital/behavioural tools are first-line, not pharmacotherapy.

## What would change these verdicts

All three are fast-moving. Ketamine uropathy management is largely consensus/observational and would benefit from treatment trials. The kratom and cannabis pictures will shift as Thailand's regulatory frameworks evolve (the cannabis policy in particular remains in flux) and as local clinical-epidemiology data accumulate — a unit that tracked its own cannabis- and kratom-related presentations would fill exactly the evidence gap the legalization literature names. Adolescent vaping cessation is the most active — the school-based prevention RCTs read out over the next few years, and the digital cessation model is ready to deploy now.
