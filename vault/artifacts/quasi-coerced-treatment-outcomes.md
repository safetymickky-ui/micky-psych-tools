# Quasi-coerced treatment: do legally-pressured referrals do worse than self-referral?
*2026-07-14 · PubMed 4 · trials 0 · books 0*

> Decision handed over pre-locked (no intent-lock interview): *do legally-pressured referrals (ม.114-type
> diversion) show worse retention/remission than self-referral, and what moderates it (MI intensity) — to
> inform how to triage the two streams.* The reading it beat: a debate on the ethics of coercion. Answered the
> triage question — how to handle the diverted vs self-referred streams.

## Verdict

**It depends entirely on *what kind* of coercion. Detention-based compulsory treatment shows no benefit over voluntary care and some evidence of harm — so a ม.114 diversion must not be a locked/compulsory model. But legally-*leveraged* treatment that preserves the patient's autonomy (diversion into genuine, evidence-based outpatient care) retains and completes at rates equal to or better than self-referral — legal pressure is not inherently worse, and can improve engagement. Triage both streams into the same evidence-based treatment, and front-load motivational engagement in the diverted stream.** Concretely:

1. **Compulsory/detention treatment is out.** The systematic review of compulsory drug treatment (locked facilities, forced programs) found **no evidence of benefit and some evidence of harm** — a ม.114 pathway built as detention would be both ineffective and a human-rights liability.
2. **Legal leverage is not the same thing, and it works.** When the legal system provides the external pressure to *enter and stay* in genuine treatment while the person keeps their autonomy, legally-coerced clients complete treatment **as often or more often** than voluntary clients, and can have equal or better substance-use outcomes at follow-up.
3. **The two streams differ in profile, not in whether treatment can work:** diverted clients tend to present **earlier and less severe** (less psychological distress) than self-referrers, who often arrive later and more impaired. So diverted patients often have *better* prognostic starting points but *lower internal motivation*.
4. **The moderator is motivation, converted by engagement.** Legal leverage gets the patient through the door; internal motivation sustains change. Build motivational engagement into the diverted stream to convert external pressure into internal commitment — while recognising MI's retention effect is modest (report #7), so it complements, not replaces, the legal leverage.
5. **Triage implication:** route both streams into the *same* evidence-based treatment (CM, structured psychosocial, retention supports); do not run diversion as a lesser or a locked track; expect the diverted stream to retain well but to need earlier engagement work.

Confidence: **moderate-high** on the leverage-vs-detention distinction (well-established in the literature); **moderate** on the exact moderator weighting.

## The distinction that decides the answer

The single most important move is refusing to treat "coercion" as one thing. The evidence splits cleanly by *how much autonomy the patient keeps*.

**Detention-based compulsory treatment fails.** The systematic review of compulsory drug treatment identified nine studies across drug-detention facilities, compulsory inpatient and community programs, and prison-based treatment; the majority showed **no significant benefit over controls, and two showed negative impacts** (worse criminal recidivism), with only isolated positive findings. The authors conclude that evidence does not support compulsory approaches and that, given the human-rights risks of compulsory settings, non-compulsory modalities should be prioritised. For a ม.114 diversion, the lesson is unambiguous: do not build a locked or forced program.

**Legally-leveraged treatment succeeds — often better on engagement.** When the coercion is *leverage* (a legal incentive to enter and remain in real, autonomy-preserving treatment) rather than *detention*, the picture inverts. In a large cannabis-treatment cohort, clients legally coerced into treatment had **less psychological distress and a greater likelihood of completing treatment** than non-coerced clients, and were more likely to be abstinent at follow-up; the voluntary clients, by contrast, tended to present later and more severely impaired. The clinical-review literature concurs that coercion-as-leverage "can be effective and may be warranted in some circumstances," distinguishing legitimate leverage from coercive detention. So legally-pressured referral is not a prognostic penalty — it is, on retention and completion, frequently an advantage.

## Why the streams differ, and how to triage them

The two referral streams are not two qualities of patient so much as two points in the illness course. Diverted (legally-pressured) patients are pushed into treatment *earlier*, before the disorder has produced the distress that eventually drives self-referral — hence their lower baseline severity and distress. Self-referrers arrive when the problem has become painful enough to seek help unprompted — later, more severe, but more internally motivated. This has three triage consequences:

- **Same treatment, both streams.** Because legally-leveraged treatment works, the diverted stream should receive the *same* evidence-based care (contingency management, structured psychosocial treatment, retention supports) as self-referrers — not a diluted or punitive track.
- **Front-load engagement in the diverted stream.** External legal pressure explains why diverted patients *stay*; it does not supply the internal motivation that sustains change after the legal leverage lapses. Motivational engagement early in the diverted pathway is the mechanism for converting external → internal motivation — with the caveat (report #7) that MI's retention effect is modest, so it is an adjunct to, not a substitute for, the leverage and the core treatment.
- **Expect different severity mixes.** The diverted stream will skew earlier/less-severe and the self-referred later/more-severe, which matters for resource allocation and for interpreting each stream's outcomes — a lower remission rate in the self-referred stream may reflect case-mix, not worse care.

## Where this verdict inverts

- **If the ม.114 pathway is, in practice, detention** (locked facility, no autonomy), the Werb evidence applies and the expected result is no benefit and possible harm — the leverage advantage requires genuine, autonomy-preserving treatment.
- **Severe, distressed self-referrers** may need more intensive treatment and step-up than the earlier-stage diverted patients — matching intensity to severity still applies within both streams.
- **If engagement/MI capacity is absent**, the diverted stream's retention advantage may not convert into durable change once legal leverage ends — the engagement component is what makes the leverage pay off long-term.

## What would change this verdict

The compulsory-vs-voluntary distinction is stable and increasingly codified in international guidance (away from compulsory detention, toward voluntary/leveraged community treatment). The open local question is the *moderator* — how much motivational engagement, delivered how, best converts legal leverage into durable remission in a Thai diversion population. A unit that tracked retention and remission for its ม.114-diverted vs self-referred streams, stratified by baseline severity and engagement received, would answer exactly that and produce a publishable service evaluation — while confirming (or refining) the front-load-engagement rule locally.
