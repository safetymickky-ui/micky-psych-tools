# Smoking-cessation pharmacotherapy for a counselling-only public clinic: what to add, and what it buys
*2026-07-14 · PubMed 8 · trials 0 · books 0*

> Decision handed over pre-locked (no intent-lock interview): *a Thai public clinic currently running
> counselling only (~11% sustained abstinence) wants to add cessation pharmacotherapy — which agent(s) to
> adopt as standard, weighing efficacy, safety after EAGLES, and cost-effectiveness for a public system:
> varenicline, NRT, bupropion, or cytisine.* The reading it beat: a pharmacology survey of nicotinic
> agents. Answered the formulary decision — what to stock and offer first, and the abstinence gain it buys
> on top of the counselling already in place.

## Verdict

**Add pharmacotherapy to the existing counselling — it roughly doubles sustained abstinence — and make cytisine the first-line workhorse, with varenicline and combination NRT as the co-first-line options and bupropion reserved.** Concretely:

1. **The headline number:** adding a first-line agent to counselling approximately **doubles** sustained quit rates. Against placebo-plus-support, varenicline gives an odds ratio of about **2.3–2.9**, cytisine about **2.2**, combination NRT about **1.9**; single-form NRT and bupropion are a tier below (~1.4). For a clinic sitting at ~11% on counselling alone, that is the difference between roughly 1 in 9 and closer to **1 in 5** quitting — the single highest-yield change available.
2. **First-line for a public system: cytisine.** It is **comparable in efficacy to varenicline**, causes **fewer adverse events**, and costs a fraction as much — and the trial that most resembles this setting (community-based, low-intensity support, high-smoking-prevalence indigenous population) found it at least as effective as varenicline. Cost and availability are the binding constraint in a public clinic, and cytisine is where efficacy and affordability meet.
3. **Co-first-line: varenicline and combination NRT.** Varenicline has the highest point estimate and is the right choice for heavier or previously-failed quitters; **combination NRT** (patch + a fast-acting form) matches varenicline and is the most accessible, titratable option. **Single-form NRT** works but is weaker — combine it.
4. **Bupropion: reserved / co-indication.** Effective but second-tier, with a seizure risk (~1:1000–1500) and contraindications; its niche is the patient with comorbid depression where one drug serves two ends.
5. **Safety is settled — EAGLES removed the fear.** In 8,144 smokers with and without psychiatric disorders, varenicline and bupropion showed **no significant excess of neuropsychiatric adverse events** versus nicotine patch or placebo. Psychiatric comorbidity is **not** a reason to withhold varenicline — directly relevant to a mental-health clinic population.
6. **Keep the counselling.** Pharmacotherapy and behavioural support are additive; the drug is added *to* counselling, never *instead of* it.

Confidence: **high** that adding pharmacotherapy roughly doubles abstinence and is safe post-EAGLES; **moderate-high** on cytisine-first, because cytisine's efficacy near-equivalence to varenicline is moderate-certainty and its optimal dose/duration is still unsettled (a short course underperformed in one head-to-head).

## What the pharmacotherapy actually buys over counselling alone

The clinic's ~11% counselling-only figure is close to what the trial literature calls the placebo-plus-support quit rate, and the comparative evidence is unusually deep — the definitive Cochrane component network meta-analysis pooled **319 RCTs and 157,179 participants**. It places three interventions in the top efficacy tier with high certainty: **varenicline (OR 2.33, 95% CrI 2.02–2.68), cytisine (OR 2.21, 95% CrI 1.66–2.97), and nicotine e-cigarettes (OR 2.37)** — each yielding roughly **7–8 extra quitters per 100** over control. **Combination NRT** (patch plus a fast-acting form) sits just below with an overlapping interval (OR 1.93). A clear second tier — **nicotine patch alone (OR 1.37), fast-acting NRT alone (OR 1.41), and bupropion (OR 1.43)** — adds about 2–3 quitters per 100. The older Cochrane overview gives the same ordering in absolute terms (varenicline OR 2.88 vs placebo; NRT and bupropion ~1.8).

Translated to the clinic: layering a top-tier agent onto the counselling that already delivers ~11% should push sustained abstinence toward and past 20%. Even the weakest first-line option beats counselling alone. The decision is not *whether* to add pharmacotherapy — the evidence for that is overwhelming — but *which* agent a public formulary should lead with.

## Which agent should a public clinic lead with?

Efficacy alone points at varenicline; cost-effectiveness in a public system points at cytisine, and the gap between them is small enough that cost wins. The dedicated Cochrane review of nicotinic partial agonists found **varenicline superior to bupropion (RR 1.36), to single-form NRT (RR 1.25), and equivalent to dual-form NRT**, with a very high absolute efficacy (RR 2.32 vs placebo). Head-to-head against cytisine, varenicline had a slightly higher point estimate (cytisine-vs-varenicline RR 0.83) but the confidence interval crossed 1 — i.e. no reliable difference — and cytisine produced **fewer serious adverse events**. The cytisine-specific meta-analysis (14 RCTs, 9,953 participants) reached the same place: cytisine beats placebo (RR 2.25) and NRT (RR 1.39), is **statistically indistinguishable from varenicline** (RR 1.13, NS), and causes fewer adverse events than varenicline (RR 0.67).

The trial that should carry the most weight for this setting is the New Zealand **RAUORA** trial in indigenous Māori (n=679) — a community-based, open-label, low-intensity-support design that mirrors a busy public clinic far better than an academic RCT does. Cytisine delivered **12.1% CO-verified continuous abstinence at 6 months versus 7.9% for varenicline** (RR 1.55), with **significantly fewer adverse events**. The honest counter-evidence: a Croatian/Slovenian primary-care trial found the reverse — a standard 4-week cytisine course reached 23% versus 32% for a 12-week varenicline course at 24 weeks — but cytisine still had better adherence and fewer adverse events, and the likely explanation is the *shorter cytisine regimen*, not an inferior drug. Adjudicating the two: cytisine is at least in varenicline's neighbourhood on efficacy, reliably cheaper and better tolerated, and its apparent underperformance tracks with under-dosing — so a public clinic should lead with cytisine at an adequate course length, hold varenicline for failures and heavier smokers, and offer combination NRT as the accessible, titratable alternative.

## Safety after EAGLES — why psychiatric comorbidity is not a barrier

This is the part that most changes practice in a mental-health setting, and it is settled. **EAGLES** randomised 8,144 motivated quitters — half with a psychiatric disorder — to varenicline, bupropion, nicotine patch, or placebo. It found **no significant increase in moderate-to-severe neuropsychiatric adverse events** attributable to varenicline or bupropion versus patch or placebo, in either the psychiatric or non-psychiatric cohort, while confirming varenicline as the most effective agent (OR 3.61 vs placebo; 1.68 vs patch; 1.75 vs bupropion). This is the evidence that led regulators to **remove varenicline's boxed neuropsychiatric warning**. The practical consequence: the reflex to withhold varenicline (or bupropion) from patients with depression, anxiety, or stable psychotic illness is not evidence-based — these are exactly the high-prevalence, high-yield smokers a psychiatric clinic sees, and they should be offered first-line pharmacotherapy with routine monitoring, not excluded from it. (Bupropion's genuine caution is the seizure threshold, not neuropsychiatric risk.)

## Where this verdict inverts

- **Cytisine unavailable or unregistered** locally: lead with combination NRT or varenicline instead — the cost argument only holds if cytisine can actually be stocked.
- **Comorbid depression or a bupropion co-indication**: bupropion moves up, serving two indications with one drug.
- **Contraindications** shift the choice — bupropion is out with a seizure history or eating disorder; cardiovascular disease is *not* a contraindication to first-line agents (their cessation benefit outweighs the risk), but is a reason to prefer the best-tolerated option and monitor.
- **E-cigarettes rank in the top efficacy tier** in the meta-analysis, but for a clinic that is a policy and harm-reduction question, not a prescription — it belongs to a separate decision about a regulated cessation pathway, not the first drug written for a patient.

## What would change this verdict

Little on the core question. Cessation pharmacotherapy is one of the most heavily-trialled areas in medicine — the comparative-efficacy verdict rests on 300+ RCTs and >150,000 participants, so no unpublished or ongoing trial can plausibly overturn "add a first-line agent, it roughly doubles abstinence." The genuinely moving evidence is elsewhere: the **e-cigarette** Cochrane review is a *living* systematic review (high-certainty that nicotine e-cigarettes beat NRT for quitting, but a distinct harm-reduction debate), and the open questions for cytisine are its **optimal dose and duration** and its **regulatory availability** (the drive to license standardised cytisine will widen access) — not whether it works. A clinic that logged its own 6-month CO-verified abstinence before and after adding a cytisine-first formulary would produce directly usable local data.
